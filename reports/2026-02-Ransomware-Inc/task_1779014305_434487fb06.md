# Strategic Targeting and Victimology of Ransomware Operations in 2025–Early 2026

Ransomware in 2025 and early 2026 has evolved from opportunistic endpoint encryption into a strategic resilience-disruption model: operators increasingly select victims, entry points, and internal targets based on the likelihood of maximizing business interruption, data exposure, and recovery failure. Public-sector and industry reporting indicates that ransomware remained one of the most directly disruptive cybercrime threats, with ENISA assessing that ransomware accounted for 81.1% of cybercrime activity affecting EU organizations during its 2025 reporting period, while the broader ecosystem continued to fragment into new variants, affiliate programs, and data-extortion brands after law-enforcement disruption and internal criminal competition ([ENISA, 2025](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf)).

Victimology data shows both broad opportunism and clear concentration around economically significant, operationally dependent sectors. In the EU, cybercrime activity most heavily affected digital infrastructure and services at 13.7%, manufacturing at 13.26%, and business services at 9.7%; within ransomware claims specifically, manufacturing led with 14.9%, followed by digital infrastructure and services at 10.3%. Data-breach claims were concentrated in digital infrastructure and services at 28.2% and public administration at 16.8%, underscoring how ransomware and extortion groups prioritize organizations that hold sensitive data, support downstream customers, or operate time-sensitive services ([ENISA, 2025](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf)). Geographic concentration similarly reflects economic value and digital dependency: the top EU Member States named in ransomware and data-breach claims were Germany at 23.4%, Italy at 11.33%, Spain at 9.8%, France at 9.5%, and Belgium at 3.7%, with manufacturing remaining the most consistently targeted sector across all five ([ENISA, 2025](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf)).

The strategic logic behind this targeting is increasingly centered on coercive leverage rather than encryption alone. Manufacturing, transport, digital infrastructure, finance, healthcare, media, and public administration present high extortion value because outages can rapidly translate into production stoppages, public-service disruption, regulatory exposure, patient-safety risk, reputational harm, and contractual penalties. ENISA noted ransomware-related disruption across EU organizations in the second half of 2024 and highlighted concern over healthcare impacts on medical procedures, while financial institutions remained attractive due to their concentration of financial and personal data ([ENISA, 2025](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf)).

A defining feature of 2025–early 2026 ransomware operations is deliberate targeting of recovery infrastructure, especially backup platforms, identity services, hypervisors, and disaster-recovery environments. Attackers seek to compromise or disable backups to remove the victim’s fastest path to restoration; seize Active Directory or hybrid identity systems to control authentication, privilege escalation, lateral movement, and security-policy enforcement; and target virtualization platforms such as VMware ESXi because hypervisors concentrate many critical workloads behind a smaller number of administrative control points. Reporting on 2025 activity noted live intrusion use of VMware ESXi zero-days into January 2026, while identity-focused assessments emphasized that Active Directory compromise can turn a limited intrusion into an enterprise-wide business-continuity event by impairing access to applications, incident-response tooling, and administrative recovery paths ([Industrial Cyber, 2026](https://industrialcyber.co/ransomware/vulncheck-finds-ransomware-operators-increasingly-relying-on-zero-days-raising-risk-in-ot-environments/); [Blair Technology, 2026](https://www.blairtechnology.com/blog/cybersecurity-resolutions-for-identity-protection-in-2026)).

This resilience-crushing behavior aligns with a wider shift toward perimeter bypass at scale. Rather than relying primarily on phishing or single-host compromise, ransomware affiliates and extortion groups increasingly exploit edge devices, VPN appliances, enterprise software, managed-service dependencies, and vulnerable drivers—assets that often sit outside mature endpoint detection coverage but provide privileged access into many networks. Industry reporting tied 2025 ransomware activity to exploitation of enterprise software suites and VPN infrastructure, including Clop’s mass exploitation of Oracle E-Business Suite flaws and Akira’s continued monetization of SonicWall vulnerabilities, illustrating how a single exposed platform can yield many victims across sectors and geographies ([GuidePoint Security, 2026](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)).

Edge-device exploitation has become especially important because these systems combine internet exposure, high trust, weak telemetry, delayed patching, and access to authentication flows or network traffic. Trend Micro’s 2026 analysis, drawing on public vulnerability and breach data, states that edge-device exploitation rose from 3% to 22% of vulnerability-exploitation breaches in a single year and argues this is a structural shift driven by attacker economics: perimeter appliances are cheaper to exploit than browser or mobile targets, are slower for defenders to patch, and can enable credential theft, traffic interception, and internal access ([Trend Micro, 2026](https://www.trendmicro.com/vinfo/us/security/news/cybercrime-and-digital-threats/edge-under-siege-how-state-sponsored-actors-exploit-your-perimeter)). This pattern also blurs the line between state-sponsored and criminal tradecraft, as techniques pioneered in espionage campaigns—edge exploitation, zero-day use, and stealthy persistence—are increasingly observed in or adjacent to ransomware intrusion chains.

Vulnerable-driver abuse and privilege-escalation flaws further reinforce this perimeter-bypass model. Bring-your-own-vulnerable-driver activity allows ransomware operators to impair endpoint defenses after initial access, while kernel-level privilege escalation can support credential theft, lateral movement, and large-scale deployment without requiring novel malware on every host. Reporting on 2025 ransomware-linked vulnerabilities cited a Baidu Antivirus driver flaw used to bypass endpoint detection and response before DeadLock ransomware deployment, and separate patch-cycle analysis highlighted active exploitation of a Windows kernel privilege-escalation vulnerability that could enable SYSTEM-level access after foothold establishment ([Industrial Cyber, 2026](https://industrialcyber.co/ransomware/vulncheck-finds-ransomware-operators-increasingly-relying-on-zero-days-raising-risk-in-ot-environments/); [Nightwing, 2025](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/november-2025-patch-cycle-edge-identity-zero-day-exploitation-esbte)).

The overall result is a ransomware ecosystem that is more fragmented in branding but more convergent in strategic behavior: steal data, exploit exposed infrastructure, compromise identity, suppress recovery, and intensify extortion pressure. Broadcom reported that claimed ransomware attacks in 2025 reached their highest recorded level, with only temporary dips following the closure of major operations such as RansomHub before activity resumed through other brands and affiliates ([Broadcom, 2026](https://sed-cms.broadcom.com/sites/default/files/2026-01/RWN-2026-WP100_1.pdf)). GuidePoint similarly characterized 2025 as a record-setting year marked by RaaS instability, collaboration among groups, mass exploitation, and renewed Q4 acceleration, suggesting that disruption of individual ransomware brands is no longer sufficient to reduce systemic risk when affiliates can rapidly migrate to new programs ([GuidePoint Security, 2026](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)).

This report therefore frames 2025–early 2026 ransomware victimology as a study in operational dependency: attackers are not merely choosing victims with money or data, but organizations whose identity systems, backups, edge appliances, software suppliers, and ICT dependencies can be converted into leverage. The most exposed organizations are those with complex hybrid identity, externally reachable appliances, concentrated virtualization estates, weak backup isolation, unmanaged third-party access, or dependence on MSPs and shared software platforms. Understanding this strategic targeting pattern is essential for defensive prioritization, because the decisive question is no longer only whether ransomware can enter an environment, but whether it can disable the organization’s ability to authenticate, contain, restore, and continue operating under pressure.

## 2025–Early 2026 Ransomware Landscape: Ecosystem Fragmentation, RaaS Evolution, and Record Activity Levels

### Strategic Judgments, Evidence Baseline, and Measurement Caveats

No existing subtopic reports or prior written sections were supplied for this larger report; therefore, the analysis below introduces net-new coverage and does not intentionally duplicate earlier report content.

The 2025–early 2026 ransomware landscape should be assessed as a high-volume, adaptive criminal market rather than a linear sequence of malware “families.” The strongest strategic signal is not merely that more victims are being named on data leak sites, but that ransomware operators have become better at converting access into coercive leverage. Across 2025, ransomware-as-a-service (RaaS) ecosystems absorbed law-enforcement disruption, affiliate churn, brand implosion, and rebranding by shifting toward smaller operating cells, reusable access supply chains, and increasingly specialized extortion roles. GuidePoint Security’s GRIT reporting described a record ransomware-group population in 2025, monitoring 117 groups and identifying fragmentation after disruption of major RaaS brands such as RansomHub, BianLian, and 8Base; however, a smaller set of high-output groups—especially Clop, Qilin, Akira, and RansomHub—continued to generate disproportionate victim volume ([1](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)). A parallel GuidePoint public release placed the 2025 active-group count at 124, a 46% year-over-year increase, and reported that Q4 2025 reached 2,287 posted victims, the highest quarterly figure in that dataset ([2](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/ransomware-victims-threat-groups-surge-110800052.html)).

Early 2026 did not show a return to pre-2025 baselines. Check Point Research monitored more than 70 ransomware data leak sites in Q1 2026 and recorded 2,122 newly listed victims, a 12.2% decline from the Q4 2025 peak of 2,416 but still the second-highest Q1 on record and 117% above Q1 2024; monthly victim counts remained stable at 732 in January, 684 in February, and 706 in March, indicating sustained throughput rather than a single campaign spike ([3](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)). Check Point further assessed that the top 10 ransomware groups accounted for 71% of Q1 2026 victims, showing that the ecosystem was consolidating around fewer high-impact operators after a period of fragmentation ([4](https://blog.checkpoint.com/research/q1-2026-ransomware-report-fewer-groups-higher-impact/)). This apparent contradiction—record group counts in 2025 followed by Q1 2026 concentration—is best understood as a two-stage market cycle: disruption creates many brands, but affiliates, initial-access brokers, negotiators, and leak-site operators eventually gravitate toward platforms that provide better payment conversion, victim pressure, tooling, and reputation.

The victim-count numbers above should be interpreted cautiously. Data leak site statistics are useful for trend analysis, but they are not a complete incident census. They omit victims who pay before publication, incidents with encryption but no leak-site listing, extortion events handled privately, and attacks by groups that do not maintain public leak portals. Conversely, they may include duplicate listings, misattributed claims, and victims counted differently across vendors. Nevertheless, the convergence among GuidePoint, Check Point, CYFIRMA, ENISA, and industry incident-response observations supports a high-confidence judgment: ransomware activity in 2025 and early 2026 remained near record levels even after major law-enforcement disruption and brand churn.

A second strategic judgment is that ransomware is increasingly shaped by access scalability rather than by the novelty of encryption payloads. CYFIRMA characterized the January 2026 ecosystem as a service-oriented criminal economy defined by psychological, operational, and supply-chain leverage, with activity concentrated against professional services, manufacturing, and information technology; it also reported that December 2025 reached 801 incidents, the highest monthly total in its four-year dataset, while January 2026 rose to 683 incidents compared with 511 in January 2025 and 284 in January 2024 ([5](https://www.cyfirma.com/research/tracking-ransomware-jan-2026/)). This aligns with the broader trend in which ransomware operators increasingly treat malware deployment as only one monetization option. Many groups now combine data theft, regulatory pressure, public shaming, customer notification threats, denial-of-service pressure, and selective encryption depending on the victim’s resilience posture and perceived willingness to pay.

The third strategic judgment is that ransomware targeting has become resilience-aware. Operators do not simply seek access to business systems; they deliberately target the mechanisms that allow victims to contain, restore, and govern recovery. Backup servers, backup catalogs, hypervisor management consoles, Active Directory, Entra ID, privileged access systems, endpoint management tools, disaster recovery networks, and remote monitoring and management platforms are all attractive because they determine whether an incident remains a localized breach or becomes a business-wide outage. Semperis reported that 83% of successful ransomware attacks in its research involved compromise of identity infrastructure, underscoring how identity compromise turns ransomware from a device-level event into an enterprise-control crisis ([6](https://www.semperis.com/blog/fighting-ransomware-in-2026-what-cisos-need-to-know/)). The same logic applies to backup platforms: if attackers can delete, encrypt, corrupt, or exfiltrate backup repositories and recovery credentials, they can undermine restoration confidence and increase the perceived necessity of ransom payment.

A fourth strategic judgment is that perimeter bypass is now industrialized through edge-device exploitation, exposed management interfaces, vulnerable drivers, and upstream ICT dependencies. Google threat-intelligence findings summarized by PacketWatch indicated that exploitation of vulnerabilities was the most common ransomware initial-access method in 2025, accounting for roughly one-third of cases, with frequent exploitation of VPNs and internet-facing firewalls from vendors including Fortinet, SonicWall, Palo Alto, and Citrix; additional exploited services included Veritas Backup Exec, Zoho ManageEngine, Microsoft SharePoint, and SAP NetWeaver ([7](https://packetwatch.com/resources/threat-intel/cyber-threat-intelligence-report-03-23-2026?hs_amp=true)). This trend matters because edge devices often sit outside normal endpoint detection coverage, operate with high trust, and provide direct routes into internal identity, management, and backup environments. The CISA Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog and #StopRansomware guidance reinforce the defensive imperative to prioritize known-exploited vulnerabilities, reduce exposed services, harden identity, and maintain tested offline backups ([8](https://www.cisa.gov/known-exploited-vulnerabilities-catalog)), ([9](https://www.cisa.gov/stopransomware/ransomware-guide)).

### Victim Demographics: Sector Concentration, Geography, and Extortion Leverage

Ransomware victimology in 2025 and early 2026 shows a consistent preference for organizations where downtime, data sensitivity, regulatory exposure, or downstream dependencies increase coercive leverage. The targeting pattern is not purely sector-specific in the way traditional espionage campaigns may be. Instead, ransomware operators generally optimize for monetizable disruption: the victim must have enough revenue, operational urgency, cyber-insurance or liquidity, and reputational exposure to make payment plausible. This explains why manufacturing, technology, business services, healthcare, retail and wholesale, digital infrastructure, professional services, and public administration repeatedly appear across datasets.

Manufacturing was one of the clearest global concentrations. GRIT reported that manufacturing accounted for 22% of all tracked ransomware attacks in its 2025 reporting, followed by technology at 14%; a related GuidePoint release using a broader yearly framing cited manufacturing at 14%, technology at 9%, and retail/wholesale at 7%, illustrating that exact percentages vary by dataset and classification method but that manufacturing remains consistently dominant ([1](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)), ([2](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/ransomware-victims-threat-groups-surge-110800052.html)). Manufacturing’s attractiveness is structural: production environments often run legacy systems, depend on time-sensitive logistics, maintain complex supplier/customer obligations, and suffer immediate revenue loss when systems supporting scheduling, quality control, shipping, enterprise resource planning, or plant operations are disrupted. Even when ransomware does not directly encrypt operational technology, disruption to IT systems that support production planning, identity, remote access, or inventory can force shutdowns.

Technology and digital infrastructure are also priority targets because they offer both direct and downstream leverage. ENISA’s 2025 Threat Landscape found that in the EU, cybercrime incidents affected digital infrastructure and services most frequently at 13.7%, followed by manufacturing at 13.26% and business services at 9.7%; within ransomware-specific claims, manufacturing accounted for 14.9% and digital infrastructure and services for 10.3% ([10](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf)). Technology-sector victims may hold customer credentials, source code, managed services access, software deployment pipelines, telemetry, or hosted data. This creates a multiplier effect: a compromise of a technology provider can become an extortion event against the provider, its customers, and sometimes its customers’ customers. The economics therefore favor attacks on ICT intermediaries because one intrusion can create many pressure points.

Healthcare remained a highly sensitive victim segment even where some RaaS programs publicly discourage attacks on hospitals. GuidePoint observed that the top four impacted industries by victim volume—manufacturing, technology, retail and wholesale, and healthcare—remained consistent across 2024 and 2025, and noted that healthcare was disproportionately impacted by Qilin among the top sectors ([1](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)). Healthcare victimization is driven by a combination of patient-care urgency, protected health information, high identity-value data, complex vendor dependencies, and limited tolerance for downtime. Attackers can exploit the moral and operational pressure created when clinical scheduling, pharmacy, imaging, billing, or electronic health record access is degraded. Even when public backlash is possible, the probability of payment or rapid negotiation can remain attractive to criminal actors.

Professional services, legal, consulting, accounting, engineering, real estate, and construction sectors also rose in prominence in early 2026 telemetry. CYFIRMA reported that in January 2026, “Professional Goods & Services” was the most affected industry in its dataset with 142 victims, followed by manufacturing with 109, information technology with 78, consumer goods and services with 73, real estate and construction with 62, healthcare with 55, materials with 49, government and civic entities with 44, telecommunications and media with 36, and finance with 34 ([5](https://www.cyfirma.com/research/tracking-ransomware-jan-2026/)). Professional-services firms are attractive because they aggregate sensitive client information, contracts, litigation files, merger-and-acquisition data, intellectual property, credentials, and privileged communications. They may also have weaker security maturity than large regulated financial institutions despite holding similarly sensitive data.

Geographically, the United States remained the single largest ransomware victim market by a wide margin, but early 2026 showed meaningful geographic diversification by some actors. GuidePoint reported that more than half of 2025 ransomware victims were based in the United States, at 55% ([2](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/ransomware-victims-threat-groups-surge-110800052.html)). Check Point reported that the United States accounted for just under half of Q1 2026 data leak site victims, at 49.6%, while Western developed economies collectively made up the clear majority of targets ([3](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)). The U.S. concentration is consistent with attacker incentives: high revenue density, extensive cyber-insurance penetration, large numbers of midmarket firms with uneven security maturity, a litigation-heavy environment, and large volumes of regulated personal data.

However, focusing only on the U.S. risks underestimating the global nature of ransomware exposure. Check Point observed that LockBit’s early 2026 comeback appeared more geographically distributed across Europe, Latin America, and other regions, suggesting an effort to reduce exposure to aggressive enforcement jurisdictions while maintaining victim throughput ([4](https://blog.checkpoint.com/research/q1-2026-ransomware-report-fewer-groups-higher-impact/)). Check Point also identified multi-actor convergence in countries such as Turkey and Japan in Q1 2026: Turkey had 23 listed victims, with LockBit, DragonForce, and The Gentlemen responsible for roughly 70% of the country’s victim total; Japan had 21 listed victims, with The Gentlemen, Everest, and Nightspire accounting for about 62%, and both The Gentlemen and Nightspire linked in reporting to exploitation of the same FortiGate vulnerability, CVE-2024-55591 ([3](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)). This convergence suggests that geography can be shaped not only by victim wealth or sector composition, but also by the installed base of vulnerable technologies, local patch latency, language capabilities, and availability of brokered access.

The demographic pattern also reveals a shift from opportunistic mass victimization to “opportunistic precision.” Ransomware actors often begin opportunistically—exploiting exposed appliances, buying credentials, abusing remote access, or following commodity loader infections—but they then prioritize victims with strong extortion value. As a result, the initial access phase can be broad and automated, while the monetization phase becomes selective. This explains why industries with large attack surfaces may not always suffer the highest ransom pressure, whereas organizations with sensitive data, fragile operations, or downstream trust relationships receive intense follow-on extortion.

### RaaS Market Recomposition: Fragmentation, Affiliate Migration, and Brand Resilience

The 2025 ransomware ecosystem was defined by instability at the brand layer but continuity at the capability layer. Takedowns, leaks, sanctions, internal disputes, and exit scams can damage a named RaaS operation, yet they rarely eliminate the underlying people, credentials, access brokers, malware developers, negotiators, money-laundering channels, bulletproof hosting, or criminal knowledge base. The result is a market in which brands die faster than capabilities disappear. Affiliates and service providers migrate to new programs, rebrand, or operate independently until another platform offers better economics.

GuidePoint’s observation of 117 monitored groups in 2025, combined with the public figure of 124 active groups, reflects a crowded market with low barriers to entry for leak-site branding and extortion claims ([1](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)), ([2](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/ransomware-victims-threat-groups-surge-110800052.html)). Yet high victim volume remained concentrated among better-capitalized or better-organized groups. Qilin, Akira, Clop, RansomHub, and the re-emerging LockBit brand illustrate that ransomware success depends on more than malware. It depends on recruiting productive affiliates, obtaining reliable initial access, maintaining payment infrastructure, managing leak-site credibility, enforcing internal rules, and providing enough technical support to move from intrusion to extortion efficiently.

The Q1 2026 consolidation signal is especially important. Check Point found that the top 10 groups accounted for 71% of victims, while Qilin remained the most active operation for the third consecutive quarter with 338 victims, and LockBit posted 163 victims after re-entering the global top tier ([4](https://blog.checkpoint.com/research/q1-2026-ransomware-report-fewer-groups-higher-impact/)). This implies that the fragmentation of 2025 did not permanently decentralize ransomware risk. Instead, the market entered a selection phase in which affiliates assessed which brands could still provide reach, reputation, tooling, victim pressure, and payment outcomes. The reappearance of LockBit is particularly notable because criminal brands can retain symbolic and practical value even after disruption. A familiar brand can attract affiliates, intimidate victims, and signal operational continuity, even if the membership, infrastructure, or tooling has changed.

RaaS evolution is also moving away from a single standardized model. Traditional RaaS involved a core developer/operator leasing malware to affiliates for a revenue share. The current model is more modular. Some actors specialize in initial access. Others specialize in data theft, negotiation, leak-site administration, malware development, infrastructure, credential markets, laundering, or pressure operations. A victim may experience the attack as one “ransomware incident,” but the intrusion chain may involve several criminal service providers. This service-oriented structure increases resilience because removing one component does not collapse the entire criminal supply chain.

| RaaS Component | 2025–Early 2026 Evolution | Defensive Implication |
|---|---|---|
| Brand / leak-site operator | More brands emerged after disruptions, but top groups regained share in Q1 2026 | Tracking names alone is insufficient; defenders must track behaviors, access methods, and infrastructure dependencies |
| Affiliate workforce | Affiliates migrated across programs after takedowns and exits | TTP continuity may persist even when group names change |
| Initial-access brokers | Greater reliance on brokered credentials, exposed appliances, and pre-compromised access | Exposure management and credential hygiene become ransomware controls, not just vulnerability-management tasks |
| Data-theft specialists | Extortion increasingly uses stolen data even without full encryption | Data-loss monitoring, egress control, and retention minimization reduce leverage |
| Negotiation and coercion teams | More professional pressure tactics, including regulatory and customer-impact narratives | Incident response must integrate legal, communications, privacy, and executive decision-making |
| Malware / encryption function | Encryption remains impactful but is only one monetization mechanism | Backup and restoration are necessary but not sufficient where sensitive data has been exfiltrated |

The RaaS market is also adopting enterprise-like efficiencies. GRIT noted that ransomware actors were becoming more efficient, professional, and business-like, adopting enterprise techniques including automation, AI integration, and franchise-style operations; it also highlighted the democratization of advanced capabilities such as bring-your-own-vulnerable-driver (BYOVD) techniques from more sophisticated toolkits into commodity ransomware contexts ([1](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)). The analytical significance of BYOVD is not that every group is technically elite, but that offensive techniques diffuse through markets. Once a method reliably disables security tooling or increases privilege, it is packaged, copied, sold, or incorporated into affiliate playbooks. The barrier between “advanced” and “commodity” therefore erodes over time.

The service economy also encourages specialization in psychological leverage. CYFIRMA assessed that the early 2026 ransomware economy was defined less by technical exploitation alone and more by psychological, operational, and supply-chain leverage, with extortion strategies increasingly prioritizing human and regulatory pressure over technical disruption ([5](https://www.cyfirma.com/research/tracking-ransomware-jan-2026/)). This shift changes the defender’s problem. A technically successful restoration may not end the crisis if attackers hold sensitive data, threaten customers, or exploit disclosure deadlines. Conversely, a technically contained intrusion can still become a severe business event if communications, legal analysis, and executive decision-making are delayed.

The most strategically important RaaS change is that ransomware groups increasingly monetize “control over recovery.” Traditional ransomware monetized encrypted files. Modern ransomware monetizes the victim’s uncertainty about whether it can restore, whether stolen data will be released, whether identity is trustworthy, whether backups are intact, whether cloud tenants are clean, whether privileged accounts remain compromised, and whether downstream customers will be harmed. This explains why groups invest in attacking identity, backup, virtualization, and management systems. These systems determine confidence. When confidence collapses, ransom pressure rises.

### Recovery Infrastructure as a Primary Target: Backups, Identity, Hypervisors, and Disaster Recovery

Ransomware operators deliberately target recovery infrastructure because recovery capability is the main variable that determines payment pressure. If a victim can rapidly isolate the intrusion, validate clean identity, restore critical services from immutable backups, rebuild virtual infrastructure, and communicate confidently with customers and regulators, the attacker’s leverage falls sharply. If the victim cannot trust identity, cannot locate clean backups, cannot restore hypervisors, cannot access administrative tools, or cannot determine whether data was exfiltrated, ransom demand becomes one of the few apparently fast options. Thus, recovery infrastructure is not collateral damage; it is a strategic objective.

Identity infrastructure is the most important resilience target because it governs enterprise authority. Active Directory, Entra ID, privileged access management systems, federation services, certificate services, and identity synchronization mechanisms determine who can authenticate, administer systems, access backups, deploy software, and approve recovery actions. Semperis reported that 83% of successful ransomware attacks in its research involved compromise of identity infrastructure, highlighting the centrality of AD and Entra ID to ransomware impact ([6](https://www.semperis.com/blog/fighting-ransomware-in-2026-what-cisos-need-to-know/)). Once attackers control identity, they can impersonate administrators, weaken security policies, access sensitive repositories, move laterally, create persistence, and disrupt recovery coordination. Even after encryption is stopped, compromised identity forces defenders to ask whether every account, token, session, service principal, and trust relationship is contaminated.

Backups are the second critical resilience target. Backup platforms often hold broad credentials, access to nearly every server, privileged service accounts, and catalogs that reveal where critical data resides. They may also contain historical data that is valuable for extortion. Attackers target backup servers and repositories to delete restore points, encrypt backup storage, corrupt backup indexes, disable scheduled jobs, steal backup credentials, or exfiltrate backup data. The goal is not merely to damage a technical control; it is to eliminate the victim’s best alternative to payment. CISA’s ransomware guidance emphasizes maintaining offline, encrypted, immutable backups and regularly testing restoration, reflecting the centrality of backup survivability to ransomware resilience ([9](https://www.cisa.gov/stopransomware/ransomware-guide)).

Hypervisors and virtualization-management systems are similarly attractive. Modern enterprises often run large portions of their server estates on virtualization platforms. Compromise of a hypervisor-management layer can allow broad disruption of virtual machines, snapshots, datastores, and administrative consoles. Attackers do not need to compromise every workload individually if they can disrupt the platform that hosts many workloads. This creates asymmetric impact: one management-plane compromise can affect domain controllers, application servers, file servers, databases, backup proxies, and security tools. Defensive telemetry may also be weaker at the hypervisor layer than on managed endpoints, creating visibility gaps.

Disaster recovery environments are often assumed to be safer than production, but they can mirror production weaknesses. If DR networks maintain persistent connectivity to production, share identity infrastructure, use the same privileged accounts, replicate corrupted data, or lack independent monitoring, they may be compromised before the organization declares a disaster. Attackers understand that synchronous replication can become a liability when destructive changes, encrypted files, or compromised credentials propagate into recovery environments. Recovery environments therefore require segmentation, separate administrative trust boundaries, independent identity controls, and routine recovery exercises that include ransomware-specific assumptions.

| Recovery Asset | Why Attackers Target It | Typical Business Effect | Defensive Priority |
|---|---|---|---|
| Active Directory / Entra ID | Controls authentication, authorization, administrative privilege, and recovery access | Loss of trust in accounts, delays in restoration, inability to safely administer systems | Tiered administration, MFA resistant to phishing, privileged access controls, AD-specific recovery planning |
| Backup servers and repositories | Determines whether ransom can be avoided through restoration | Loss of restore points, uncertainty over backup integrity, higher payment pressure | Immutable/offline backups, separate credentials, restore testing, backup-platform monitoring |
| Hypervisor management | Provides broad control over virtualized workloads | Many servers disrupted through one management plane | Management-plane segmentation, least privilege, hardened admin access, independent logging |
| Disaster recovery sites | Provides continuity if production fails | DR contamination, failed failover, extended outage | Isolated DR identity, clean-room recovery, ransomware-specific failover exercises |
| Endpoint / remote management tools | Enables software deployment and administrative action at scale | Attackers can disable controls or push malicious changes broadly | Strong authentication, allowlisting, change-control monitoring, restricted administrative scopes |
| Certificate and federation services | Supports trust, authentication, and secure communication | Persistent impersonation risk and uncertainty over identity trust | Key protection, certificate-service monitoring, emergency rotation procedures |

The defensive implication is that resilience cannot be measured only by backup existence. Many organizations report having backups, but fewer can prove that backups are immutable, isolated, complete, recent, and restorable under identity-compromise conditions. A backup protected by the same domain admin accounts that attackers compromise is not meaningfully independent. A DR environment that depends on the same identity provider and management network as production may fail at the exact moment it is needed. An incident-response plan that assumes administrators can log in normally may be unrealistic if identity systems are compromised.

Identity recovery deserves special attention because restoring identity is prerequisite to restoring everything else. If defenders rebuild servers while identity remains compromised, attackers may re-enter and re-encrypt restored systems


## Victimology and Demographic Targeting: Industries, Critical Sectors, and Geographic Concentrations

### Evidence Boundaries and Incremental Scope Beyond Prior Demographic Findings

While the earlier section titled “Victim Demographics: Sector Concentration, Geography, and Extortion Leverage” already established that ransomware victimology is shaped by payment capacity, operational urgency, data sensitivity, and business-continuity pressure, this section narrows the lens to the demographic mechanics behind those outcomes: how sectoral exposure, critical-infrastructure dependencies, geography, and resilience architecture combine to make particular organizations more attractive in 2025 and early 2026. The distinction is important. The prior material summarized the overall concentration of victims; this section evaluates why those concentrations form, how they differ across datasets, and how recovery infrastructure and perimeter-bypass trends are altering the victim profile.

The available evidence base is heterogeneous. Public ransomware datasets generally mix leak-site observations, incident-response telemetry, law-enforcement reporting, sectoral cyber-center reporting, and open-source victim claims. Each source therefore captures a different slice of the ransomware economy. Leak-site datasets tend to overrepresent double-extortion incidents where victims did not pay quickly or where operators chose public coercion. Law-enforcement complaint data undercounts victims that do not report and may understate total loss because many costs—business interruption, restoration, legal expenses, customer notification, cyber-insurance deductibles, and reputational damage—are not always included. Sectoral authorities may see more of the critical-infrastructure subset than general industry datasets. For this reason, numerical differences between sources should be interpreted as directional indicators rather than exact global totals.

A comparison of 2025 reporting shows strong agreement on three points despite methodological variance. First, ransomware remained heavily concentrated in economically significant sectors where outage costs and data sensitivity increase extortion leverage. Second, manufacturing, technology or digital infrastructure, healthcare, retail and wholesale, business services, and public administration repeatedly appear in high-impact clusters. Third, the United States and Western developed economies remained disproportionately represented, but European national clusters, sector-specific concentrations, and emerging regional victim pools show that ransomware is not merely a U.S.-centric phenomenon. ENISA’s 2025 threat landscape found that cybercriminal activity in the EU affected both NIS2 and non-NIS2 sectors, with digital infrastructure and services identified as the most targeted EU sector overall at 13.7%, followed by manufacturing at 13.26% and business services at 9.7%; within cybercrime, ransomware claims primarily affected manufacturing at 14.9% and digital infrastructure and services at 10.3% ([1](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf)). GuidePoint Security’s GRIT reporting, by contrast, observed manufacturing as 22% of tracked ransomware attacks and technology as 14%, while a later public release cited manufacturing at 14%, technology at 9%, and retail/wholesale at 7%, illustrating how different counting periods, victim taxonomies, and source pools can change percentage distributions without altering the strategic conclusion that manufacturing and technology remain leading victim classes ([2](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)), ([3](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/ransomware-victims-threat-groups-surge-110800052.html)).

The most useful analytical interpretation is therefore not that one percentage is “the” ransomware sector distribution, but that multiple independent lenses converge on a set of exposed demographic categories. Manufacturing is consistently prominent because it combines high downtime cost, distributed plants, legacy operational technology, supplier integration, and limited tolerance for production stoppage. Technology and digital infrastructure are prominent because they provide both direct ransom opportunities and indirect leverage over downstream customers. Healthcare remains attractive because of patient-care urgency and sensitive records, even where some ransomware programs claim to discourage healthcare attacks. Retail and wholesale face payment-card, customer-data, and seasonal revenue pressures. Public administration and education often have constrained budgets, large identity estates, legacy systems, and politically visible disruption. Critical infrastructure is a cross-cutting category rather than a single sector: it captures organizations whose failure can propagate into public safety, economic continuity, or national resilience.

The demographic profile also reflects a shift from purely opportunistic compromise toward scalable targeting through shared technologies. Attackers increasingly exploit the fact that organizations in different sectors may depend on the same edge appliances, identity platforms, virtualization stacks, managed service providers, remote monitoring tools, backup systems, and software suppliers. This changes victimology in two ways. First, sector is no longer the only predictor of exposure; technology commonality can create simultaneous cross-sector victim clusters. Second, victims may be selected after initial access is obtained at scale. In such a model, operators or access brokers can scan broad populations, compromise reachable systems, then prioritize victims whose sector, geography, revenue, insurance posture, or data sensitivity makes monetization more likely. This explains why reports can show broad sectoral distribution while still identifying strong concentrations in manufacturing, digital infrastructure, healthcare, technology, and public services.

A defensive interpretation must therefore avoid a false binary between “targeted” and “opportunistic.” Ransomware operators in 2025 and early 2026 often behaved opportunistically at the access layer but strategically at the monetization layer. They did not always begin with a named victim in mind. However, once access existed, the decision to deploy ransomware, steal data, pressure executives, contact customers, or attack recovery infrastructure was strongly shaped by victim demographics. The target profile is therefore dynamic: it emerges from the intersection of exploitable exposure, organizational value, disruption sensitivity, and recoverability.

### Sector Exposure Matrix: Operational Pressure, Data Value, and Criticality

Ransomware victimology in 2025 was dominated by sectors where the economic cost of downtime rises rapidly and where data exposure creates parallel legal, regulatory, and reputational pressure. Manufacturing, digital infrastructure and services, technology, healthcare, retail/wholesale, finance, business services, transportation, energy, public administration, and education each present different combinations of coercive leverage. A manufacturing victim may be pressured through halted production and supply-chain penalties; a healthcare victim through patient-care continuity and protected health information; a technology provider through downstream customer trust; a public-administration victim through civic disruption; and a retailer through revenue-cycle timing and customer-data exposure.

The manufacturing sector stands out across nearly every source. ENISA identified manufacturing as the second most targeted EU sector for cybercrime overall at 13.26% and the leading ransomware-claimed sector at 14.9% in its EU reporting period ([1](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf)). GuidePoint’s GRIT report found manufacturing accounted for 22% of tracked ransomware attacks in one dataset, while its annual public summary placed manufacturing at 14% of attacks, still the most heavily impacted industry in that release ([2](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)), ([3](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/ransomware-victims-threat-groups-surge-110800052.html)). KELA’s critical-sector reporting, as summarized by Industrial Cyber, stated that ransomware attacks against manufacturing rose from 520 to 838 between comparable January–September periods, a 61% increase, making manufacturing one of the clearest growth areas in the 2025 victim set ([4](https://industrialcyber.co/reports/half-of-2025-ransomware-attacks-hit-critical-sectors-as-manufacturing-healthcare-and-energy-top-global-targets/)). IT-ISAC reporting cited by MSSP Alert similarly observed critical manufacturing as the largest critical-infrastructure ransomware category, representing about 20%, or 733, of roughly 3,500 tracked attacks across multiple critical-infrastructure sectors ([5](https://www.msspalert.com/news/ransomware-attacks-on-critical-infrastructure-ai-use-to-grow-in-2025)).

Manufacturing’s exposure is not simply a function of weak security. It is structural. Modern manufacturers combine enterprise IT, plant-floor systems, third-party maintenance access, engineering workstations, supplier portals, warehouse systems, industrial IoT, just-in-time logistics, and enterprise resource planning. Even when ransomware does not directly target industrial control systems, encryption or disruption of adjacent IT services can stop production planning, quality control, shipping, invoicing, procurement, and maintenance workflows. The attacker’s leverage comes from the victim’s need to restore synchronized operations rather than from the malware’s sophistication alone. A plant that cannot access bills of material, production schedules, maintenance records, identity services, or virtualized application servers may be operationally frozen even if programmable logic controllers and safety systems remain untouched.

Healthcare is demographically distinct because the extortion pressure derives from patient-care urgency, regulated health data, and public trust. GuidePoint observed that healthcare remained one of the four most impacted industries by victim volume in both 2024 and 2025, and that Qilin disproportionately affected healthcare compared with other top sectors in its observations ([2](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)). CISA’s StopRansomware advisory index notes that Rhysida has been deployed predominantly against education, healthcare, manufacturing, information technology, and government sectors since May 2023, and that Royal ransomware attacks spread across multiple critical infrastructure sectors including manufacturing, communications, healthcare and public health, and education ([7](https://www.cisa.gov/stopransomware/official-alerts-statements-cisa)). The demographic implication is that healthcare remains attractive even when some ransomware brands claim restrictions, because affiliates and splinter groups may not honor such rules, because data-extortion-only operations can still monetize patient records, and because the urgency of restoring clinical operations increases negotiation pressure.

Technology and digital infrastructure are central to 2025 victimology because they represent both high-value victims and enabling terrain. ENISA found digital infrastructure and services to be the most targeted EU sector for cybercriminal activities overall at 13.7%, and the second most affected ransomware-claimed sector at 10.3% ([1](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf)). GuidePoint’s GRIT reporting placed technology second behind manufacturing at 14% in one victim distribution, while the public release placed technology at 9% ([2](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)), ([3](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/ransomware-victims-threat-groups-surge-110800052.html)). The significance is not only that technology firms suffer direct disruption. Technology providers often store source code, customer data, administrative credentials, integration tokens, remote management access, and service-level obligations. A ransomware incident at such an organization can become a reputational crisis and, in some cases, a downstream risk event for customers that rely on the victim’s hosted platforms, managed services, or software updates.

Retail and wholesale organizations are exposed through revenue timing, distributed infrastructure, customer-data holdings, and third-party logistics. GuidePoint’s public release identified retail/wholesale as the third most affected industry at 7% of attacks in its 2025 summary, after manufacturing and technology ([3](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/ransomware-victims-threat-groups-surge-110800052.html)). The demographic logic is straightforward: retail has high tolerance constraints during peak seasons, dependence on point-of-sale ecosystems and inventory systems, and large volumes of customer or loyalty-program data. Wholesale and distribution firms add warehouse-management, shipping, and supplier-integration dependencies. Ransomware groups can exploit the short window in which outage costs escalate: a few days of disruption near peak sales cycles or contractual delivery deadlines may produce more pressure than a longer outage in a less time-sensitive environment.

Public administration and government entities appear in multiple reporting streams because they combine large digital estates, legacy systems, public visibility, and constrained modernization budgets. ENISA observed that data breaches were primarily claimed against digital infrastructure and services at 28.2% and public administration at 16.8% in its EU cybercrime activity assessment, which is significant because ransomware operations increasingly combine encryption with theft and leak threats ([1](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf)). CISA’s advisory index also identifies government among sectors affected by Rhysida, while FBI-related reporting notes that BlackSuit/Royal ransomware targeted critical-infrastructure sectors including government facilities, healthcare and public health, critical manufacturing, and commercial facilities ([6](https://industrialcyber.co/reports/fbi-reports-cyber-threats-to-critical-infrastructure-intensify-as-us-cybercrime-losses-hit-21-billion-exposes-risk/)), ([7](https://www.cisa.gov/stopransomware/official-alerts-statements-cisa)). Public-sector targeting has a different coercive profile from private-sector targeting: payment capacity may be lower or legally constrained, but service disruption, political pressure, citizen-data exposure, and media visibility can be high.

Energy, transportation, finance, food and agriculture, and communications are often discussed under the critical-infrastructure umbrella rather than as isolated sectors. KELA’s reporting stated that 2,332 of 4,701 ransomware incidents between January and September 2025—50%—targeted critical sectors including manufacturing, healthcare, energy, transportation, and finance, reflecting ransomware’s evolution into a systemic national-resilience threat ([4](https://industrialcyber.co/reports/half-of-2025-ransomware-attacks-hit-critical-sectors-as-manufacturing-healthcare-and-energy-top-global-targets/)). IT-ISAC’s sector breakdown cited commercial facilities at 17%, healthcare and public health at 9%, IT at 8%, financial services at 7%, and food and agriculture at 5%, after critical manufacturing at 20% ([5](https://www.msspalert.com/news/ransomware-attacks-on-critical-infrastructure-ai-use-to-grow-in-2025)). The pattern suggests that ransomware actors are not uniformly selecting critical infrastructure for ideological reasons; instead, they are monetizing the fact that critical sectors have low downtime tolerance, complex supplier dependencies, and high public or regulatory consequences.

A critical analytical point is that “critical sector” status does not necessarily mean attackers are pursuing strategic disruption as a primary objective. Most ransomware remains financially motivated. However, financial motivation and national-resilience impact can coexist. A ransomware crew may choose a manufacturer because it expects payment, not because it wants to weaken national supply chains; nevertheless, the outcome can disrupt essential production, logistics, employment, downstream customers, and public confidence. The same applies to healthcare, local government, food distribution, and transportation. In 2025, the line between financially motivated cybercrime and national-security-relevant disruption continued to blur because criminal targeting repeatedly intersected with essential services.

Sector targeting also varies by ransomware group and affiliate culture. ENISA reported country-level differences in actor prominence, such as Qilin, Akira, and FOG in Spain, Qilin, Hunters International, and CL0P in France, and RansomHouse, Play, SafePay, and Qilin in Belgium, with manufacturing frequently the most targeted sector in those countries and digital infrastructure and services often following ([1](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf)). GuidePoint observed that Akira claimed the highest number of victims across four of five top industries, excluding healthcare, where Qilin had disproportionate impact in its dataset ([2](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)). This indicates that sector distribution is partly shaped by affiliate access pipelines. If a group’s affiliates have access brokers specializing in certain remote-access technologies, geographies, or industry verticals, that group’s victimology may deviate from the aggregate ransomware economy.

From a defender’s perspective, sector-level victimology should not be used only to rank industries by probability of attack. It should be used to identify which business functions create coercive leverage. A hospital may have radiology, pharmacy, patient scheduling, identity, and electronic health record dependencies that determine whether a cyber incident becomes a patient-care crisis. A manufacturer may depend on ERP, warehouse management, domain services, plant historians, engineering file shares, and remote vendor access. A municipality may rely on tax, permitting, emergency dispatch support systems, payment portals, and citizen records. The ransomware-relevant demographic attribute is therefore not just industry classification; it is the set of functions that convert cyber disruption into urgent organizational pain.

### Geographic Clustering: Country-Level Concentrations, Regional Sector Mix, and Reporting Bias

Geographic concentration in 2025 and early 2026 reflected a combination of victim wealth, digital maturity, legal and regulatory exposure, language reach, cyber-insurance penetration, technology deployment patterns, and reporting visibility. The United States remained the largest single observed victim geography in several datasets, but Europe showed substantial sectoral concentration, especially in manufacturing and digital infrastructure, and country-level actor differences. Critical-sector reporting also shows that ransomware’s geographic impact cannot be evaluated solely by victim count; the national-resilience consequences of attacks on energy, healthcare, manufacturing, public administration, finance, transportation, and ICT providers may be disproportionate to the number of incidents.

GuidePoint’s public release stated that more than half of ransomware victims in 2025—55%—were based in the United States, while KELA’s reporting, summarized by Industrial Cyber, found the U.S. accounted for 21% of global incidents in the January–September 2025 period ([3](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/ransomware-victims-threat-groups-surge-110800052.html)), ([4](https://industrialcyber.co/reports/half-of-2025-ransomware-attacks-hit-critical-sectors-as-manufacturing-healthcare-and-energy-top-global-targets/)). The difference between 55% and 21% should not be read as a contradiction so much as a reminder that “victim” and “incident” datasets differ. Leak-site-based datasets often heavily reflect U.S. companies because of the size of the U.S. private sector, English-language extortion markets, greater public discoverability, and the likelihood that U.S. victims have data, revenue, and litigation exposure that make them valuable for extortion. Broader global incident counts may include more non-U.S. events, different collection methods, or different thresholds for inclusion. Both point toward the same strategic reality: the U.S. remains a premier ransomware target, but it does not monopolize global risk.

The U.S. target profile is demographically attractive for several reasons. It has a high density of mid-sized and large enterprises, extensive healthcare and education systems, complex federal-state-local government structures, a mature cyber-insurance market, significant legal exposure for data breaches, and a large base of managed IT and cloud-service dependencies. Many U.S. organizations also operate in supply chains where downtime has contractual and revenue consequences. For ransomware operators, this creates an unusually rich pool of victims with plausible payment capacity and strong reputational pressure. FBI-related reporting indicates that IC3 received more than 3,600 ransomware complaints in 2025, with reported losses exceeding $32 million, and that ransomware was among the highest reported cyber threats to critical-infrastructure organizations ([6](https://industrialcyber.co/reports/fbi-reports-cyber-threats-to-critical-infrastructure-intensify-as-us-cybercrime-losses-hit-21-billion-exposes-risk/)). The reported loss figure should be treated as a floor rather than a comprehensive measure because complaint data rarely captures full business interruption, restoration, legal, forensic, and opportunity costs.

Europe’s ransomware geography is more varied by country and sector. ENISA’s EU-wide analysis found digital infrastructure and services, manufacturing, and business services leading cybercrime sectoral impact, while ransomware claims were concentrated in manufacturing and digital infrastructure/services ([1](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf)). ENISA’s country-level observations also show different ransomware-brand mixes. Spain saw Qilin first, followed by Akira and FOG, with manufacturing targeted most, then business services and public administration. France was most impacted by Qilin, Hunters International, and CL0P. Belgium saw activity from RansomHouse and Play, alongside SafePay and Qilin. In both Belgium and France, manufacturing was the most targeted sector, followed by digital infrastructure and services ([1](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf)). This country-level variation matters because it suggests that actor ecosystems adapt to local opportunity structures: industrial composition, language, exposed technologies, local MSP markets, and affiliate relationships can all influence the victim pool.

Geographic victimology should be evaluated through at least four layers. The first is economic attractiveness: higher-revenue regions produce more viable ransom targets. The second is sector composition: countries with dense manufacturing, technology, healthcare, finance, logistics, and public-service infrastructure naturally produce more high-pressure victims. The third is exposure composition: if a country or sector has widespread deployment of a particular vulnerable edge device, virtualization product, remote-access platform, or managed-service provider, it can experience clustered victimization independent of national wealth. The fourth is observability: countries with breach-notification rules, active media, mature cyber-insurance claims, and English-language corporate footprints appear more frequently in public datasets.

These layers help explain why the U.S. can dominate one dataset while critical-sector percentages remain globally distributed. Ransomware crews and affiliates do not need perfect geopolitical targeting models. They can rely on scalable access methods and then monetize victims


## Resilience-Denial Targeting of Backup, Identity, Edge, and ICT Trust Dependencies in 2025–Early 2026 Ransomware Operations

### 1. Coercion Logic: From File Encryption to Deliberate Resilience Suppression

While the earlier section titled **“Recovery Infrastructure as a Primary Target: Backups, Identity, Hypervisors, and Disaster Recovery”** catalogued the recovery assets most attractive to ransomware operators, this section takes a different analytical angle: it examines **why resilience-denial has become a strategic targeting model**, how it alters victim selection, and how it interacts with 2025–early 2026 intrusion trends such as edge-device exploitation, brokered access, supply-chain compromise, and identity-first persistence.

The central strategic change is that ransomware operators increasingly treat the victim’s **ability to recover** as the primary variable that determines extortion leverage. Encryption remains important, but it is no longer the only, or even always the preferred, mechanism of coercion. CYFIRMA’s January 2026 ransomware assessment describes a criminal economy increasingly defined by “psychological, operational, and supply-chain leverage,” with operators prioritizing browser-centric access, user-mediated compromise, brokered initial access, long-lived loaders, silent data theft, and delayed extortion rather than immediate encryption alone ([1](https://www.cyfirma.com/research/tracking-ransomware-jan-2026/)). This matters because resilience infrastructure—backup platforms, Active Directory, Entra ID, privileged access systems, hypervisor management, disaster recovery tooling, and remote management platforms—determines whether a victim can contain the event, restore services, and resist ransom pressure.

The economic model can be summarized as follows: ransomware profitability rises when attackers can simultaneously increase **operational outage**, **data-disclosure risk**, **regulatory exposure**, **customer pressure**, and **uncertainty about recovery integrity**. If a victim has clean identity, usable backups, isolated disaster recovery capacity, and tested restoration procedures, the attacker’s leverage declines. If the victim cannot authenticate administrators, cannot trust domain controllers, cannot validate backup integrity, cannot access hypervisor management safely, or cannot determine whether stolen data exists in backup repositories, the attacker can frame ransom payment as the fastest route to business continuity—even if that framing is unreliable. ENISA’s 2025 threat landscape reinforces that encrypting ransomware remained the most directly impactful cybercrime threat for EU organizations, with ransomware comprising 81.1% of cybercrime activity targeting EU organizations during the reporting period and data breaches often documented as resulting from ransomware incidents ([2](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf)).

The targeting of recovery infrastructure also reflects adaptation to law-enforcement disruption and declining confidence in ransom payment outcomes. The 2025 ransomware ecosystem was fragmented by disruption of major brands and internal competition, but disruption did not remove the business logic of extortion; it pushed operators and affiliates toward more agile, opportunistic, and leverage-focused behavior. GuidePoint Security’s GRIT 2026 report notes that disruption and fragmentation of major RaaS brands, including RansomHub, BianLian, and 8Base, contributed to a record 117 monitored groups, while Clop, Qilin, Akira, and RansomHub remained among the most prolific high-impact actors ([3](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)). CyberFortress and Veeam’s 2025 ransomware trends report similarly identifies law-enforcement pressure, data-exfiltration growth, declining ransom payments, legal consequences of payment, and the need for proactive cyber resilience as defining trends ([4](https://recovery.cyberfortress.com/hubfs/Veeam%20Documents/ransomware-trends_v2.pdf)).

This creates a coercive feedback loop. As more victims invest in endpoint detection, backup tools, and incident response retainers, attackers increasingly seek to invalidate those investments by targeting the underlying trust and recovery layers. The objective is not merely to encrypt production systems, but to make defenders doubt whether recovery is safe. Compromise of identity infrastructure causes uncertainty about every account, session, token, service principal, domain controller, federation trust, privileged access workflow, and administrative action. Compromise of backup infrastructure causes uncertainty about restore-point integrity, backup completeness, malware persistence inside backup images, and whether backup data has already been stolen. Compromise of hypervisors or virtualization management can disrupt many server workloads simultaneously, including systems required for recovery coordination. Compromise of disaster recovery networks can cause failover plans to inherit the same compromise that affected production.

This resilience-denial model is also consistent with the shift from purely technical disruption to **human and regulatory pressure**. CYFIRMA describes extortion strategies as increasingly prioritizing human and regulatory pressure over technical disruption, with silent data theft and delayed extortion complicating detection, attribution, and response ([1](https://www.cyfirma.com/research/tracking-ransomware-jan-2026/)). In this model, the attacker may not need to encrypt immediately. Long-lived loaders and brokered access preserve optionality: the access can be sold, used for data theft, used for credential harvesting, or converted into a ransomware event at a strategically chosen moment. A victim may believe it has avoided encryption, while the attacker has already mapped identity dependencies, backup systems, SaaS repositories, legal data, customer records, and privileged administrative paths.

The result is a ransomware model better understood as **enterprise coercion** rather than malware deployment. Encryption is one possible pressure mechanism. Data theft, identity destruction, backup corruption, regulatory notification threats, public leak-site naming, supplier disruption, and recovery uncertainty are others. The common target across these mechanisms is the victim’s ability to make confident operational decisions under crisis conditions. When the recovery fabric is compromised, defenders lose not only systems but also decision certainty.

### 2. Victim Selection Through the Lens of Recovery Dependency and Extortion Yield

This section differs from prior victimology coverage by focusing not simply on which sectors and countries were targeted, but on **why those demographics are especially exposed when recovery infrastructure is attacked**. The same sector counts can carry different strategic meaning when interpreted through recovery dependency: a manufacturer’s backup failure can stop production; a hospital’s identity outage can prevent access to care systems; an IT service provider’s compromise can affect downstream clients; a public administration outage can trigger civic pressure and media scrutiny.

Across 2025 and early 2026 reporting, the most exposed sectors were those combining high operational dependency, data sensitivity, complex identity environments, and low tolerance for downtime. CYFIRMA’s January 2026 data shows ransomware activity concentrated in data-rich and operationally sensitive sectors: Professional Goods & Services recorded 142 victims, Manufacturing 109, Information Technology 78, Consumer Goods & Services 73, Real Estate & Construction 62, Healthcare 55, Materials 49, Government & Civic entities 44, Telecommunications & Media 36, and Finance 34 ([5](https://www.cyfirma.com/research/tracking-ransomware-jan-2026/)). GuidePoint’s 2026 report similarly identifies Manufacturing, Technology, Retail & Wholesale, and Healthcare as the four most impacted industries by victim volume across 2025 and 2024, noting their exposure to financial or operational losses and their possession of high-value data such as PII and PHI ([3](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)).

ENISA’s EU-focused reporting provides a complementary regional view. Over the ENISA reporting period, digital infrastructure and services was the most targeted sector for cybercrime incidents at 13.7%, followed by manufacturing at 13.26% and business services at 9.7%; within ransomware claims, manufacturing accounted for 14.9% and digital infrastructure and services for 10.3% ([6](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf)). The FBI’s 2025 IC3 reporting also identified Critical Manufacturing, Healthcare and Public Health, and Government Facilities as the critical sectors most impacted by the top ten reported ransomware variants ([7](https://www.ic3.gov/AnnualReport/Reports/2025_IC3Report.pdf)).

The defensive significance is that ransomware impact in industrial environments may arise not from direct manipulation of controllers but from disruption of the IT and virtualization systems that support production visibility, engineering, and recovery.

Geographically, ransomware activity remained concentrated in economically developed regions with dense ICT dependency, high insurance penetration, large data stores, and complex supply chains. CYFIRMA states that ransomware remained dominated by the United States and Western countries entering 2026 ([1](https://www.cyfirma.com/research/tracking-ransomware-jan-2026/)). This geographic pattern should not be interpreted only as attacker preference for wealthy victims. It also reflects where recovery dependency is most monetizable. Advanced economies have more integrated identity estates, larger SaaS footprints, broader managed-service relationships, more virtualization, greater reliance on cloud-hosted collaboration, and tighter regulatory reporting duties. These features create both defensive capability and attacker leverage. A small outage in a highly digitized process can produce outsized financial, legal, or public-service effects. Conversely, organizations with mature backups but weak identity isolation may still face extended downtime if attackers corrupt the administrative trust layer required to restore systems.

The United States remains especially attractive because of its large concentration of healthcare systems, manufacturing firms, professional services, technology providers, and critical infrastructure operators. The FBI’s IC3 report received more than 3,600 ransomware complaints in 2025, with reported ransomware losses exceeding $32 million, while emphasizing that reported losses are artificially low because they usually exclude lost business, time, wages, files, equipment, and third-party remediation services, and because some entities report directly to FBI field offices rather than IC3 ([7](https://www.ic3.gov/AnnualReport/Reports/2025_IC3Report.pdf)). This caveat is central to recovery-infrastructure analysis: the true cost of compromised backups or identity is often not the ransom amount but the extended recovery labor, business interruption, legal review, replacement systems, emergency consulting, customer notification, and delayed restoration.

In the EU, ENISA’s data suggests that ransomware operators pursue broadly lucrative objectives rather than strict sector ideology. ENISA notes that cybercrime incidents showed a broadly distributed targeting pattern, likely reflecting prioritization of lucrative objectives over sector-specific targeting ([6](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf)). However, a “broadly distributed” pattern does not mean random. It means attackers are selecting for exploitable combinations of access, leverage, and payout probability. Recovery infrastructure becomes the common denominator across sectors because every sector depends on identity, backups, administration, and third-party ICT services.

### 3. Perimeter Bypass at Scale: Edge Devices, Remote Access, Vulnerable Drivers, and ICT Supply-Chain Trust

This section is intentionally distinct from prior coverage of victim demographics and recovery assets. It focuses on **how attackers reach the recovery fabric in the first place**, especially through systems that sit at the boundary of trust: VPNs, firewalls, SD-WAN controllers, remote access tools, MSP platforms, virtualization management interfaces, endpoint drivers, browser-mediated access, and software supply-chain dependencies. The pattern is not simply “perimeter compromise”; it is the abuse of infrastructure that defenders themselves rely on for secure connectivity, administration, visibility, and continuity.

A major 2025–early 2026 shift was the intensified exploitation of internet-facing perimeter technologies. Industrial Cyber reports that across 2024 and 2025, adversaries aggressively exploited vulnerabilities in VPNs, firewalls, and edge appliances from vendors including Ivanti, Palo Alto Networks, Fortinet, F5, and Cisco, with many flaws enabling authentication bypass, privilege escalation, or remote code execution through exposed web interfaces and VPN services ([10](https://industrialcyber.co/ransomware/ransomware-surge-in-2025-exposes-mounting-ot-risk-as-industrial-impacts-outpace-it-narratives/)). The significance for ransomware is strategic: edge devices often sit before endpoint agents, are inconsistently logged, run specialized firmware, and may not be integrated into normal vulnerability management cycles. If compromised, they can provide access before traditional endpoint telemetry has visibility.

CISA’s Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog illustrates the urgency of this exposure class. On May 14, 2026, CISA added CVE-2026-20182, a Cisco Catalyst SD-WAN Controller authentication bypass vulnerability that could allow an unauthenticated remote attacker to obtain administrative privileges, with mitigation deadlines under Emergency Directive 26-03 and associated hunt and hardening guidance ([11](https://www.cisa.gov/known-exploited-vulnerabilities-catalog)). CISA also listed CVE-2026-20128, a Cisco Catalyst SD-WAN Manager vulnerability involving recoverable password storage that could allow privilege gain by a low-privileged local attacker, again tied to emergency mitigation instructions ([12](https://www.cisa.gov/known-exploited-vulnerabilities-catalog-print)). Although CISA marked ransomware usage as “Unknown” for these specific entries, the broader ransomware relevance is clear: SD-WAN and edge management systems are high-value trust brokers connecting users, sites, cloud workloads, and administrative networks.

The edge-device problem is compounded by delayed patch availability, zero-day exploitation, and visibility gaps. Disaster Recovery Journal summarized AWS threat intelligence reporting on exploitation of a Cisco Firewall Management Center vulnerability, CVE-2026-20131, with honeypot exploitation observed before Cisco released a patch, describing it as part of a broader series of critical vulnerabilities affecting major security vendors and edge devices in 2026 ([13](https://drj.com/industry_news/cyber-security-intelligence-analysis-of-edge-devices-amid-growing-vulnerabilities/)). The defensive implication is that “patch fast” is necessary but insufficient when exploitation can occur before patches exist. Organizations need exposure management, compensating controls, log retention, configuration validation, and rapid hunt capability around edge infrastructure.

Ransomware operators also exploit trust concentration in managed service providers and remote management platforms. ThreatDown’s 2025 Ransomware Emergency Kit describes MSPs as prime targets for ransomware gangs and cites a case in which MDR stopped a three-month malware campaign against an MSP that made extensive use of living-off-the-land techniques to avoid detection ([14](https://www.threatdown.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/CORP_2025-REK-v6.pdf)). MSP targeting is strategically rational because MSPs often hold administrative reach into multiple customer environments. A compromise of one service provider can produce many downstream opportunities, including access to backup consoles, endpoint management platforms, remote monitoring tools, cloud tenants, and identity administration.

Software supply-chain and ICT dependency risks further expand the attack surface beyond the victim’s perimeter. The May 2026 Digital Forensics Magazine roundup references a TanStack npm supply-chain attack that prompted OpenAI to ask macOS users to update, as well as rapidly targeted authentication-bypass vulnerabilities in AI-related software and Cisco SD-WAN exploitation ([15](https://digitalforensicsmagazine.com/news-roundup-15th-may-2026/)). Not every supply-chain event becomes ransomware, but these incidents demonstrate why ransomware operators increasingly value ICT dependencies: they can create trusted distribution channels, access development environments, steal credentials, or compromise components that defenders implicitly allow. In a resilience-denial model, compromise of software update channels, remote administration tools, or dependency packages can undermine restoration confidence because rebuilt systems may reintroduce compromised components.

The vulnerable-driver dimension is another pathway for bypassing defensive controls at scale. At a high level, attackers may abuse legitimately signed but vulnerable drivers to interfere with endpoint security, tamper with telemetry, or gain privileged access below normal user-mode controls. This is not a replacement for phishing, credentials, or edge exploitation; rather, it is often a post-access enabler that helps attackers suppress detection before targeting backups or identity. The defensive relevance is that driver abuse exploits a trust assumption: operating systems and security tools often treat signed kernel components as legitimate unless blocklists, memory-protection features, or endpoint controls are properly configured. Because the specific mechanics can be offensive if described in detail, the appropriate defensive framing is governance-based: maintain operating-system and EDR driver blocklists, enforce application and driver control policies, monitor for unusual kernel-driver load events, and ensure tamper protection is tested under realistic incident conditions.

The common theme is that perimeter bypass increasingly targets **trusted control planes** rather than individual endpoints. Edge devices control access. Identity systems control authority. MSP tools control administration. Backup platforms control recovery. Hypervisors control server concentration. Software dependencies control what gets installed. Vulnerable drivers can weaken visibility. Ransomware operators target these layers because they allow rapid scaling, stealth, and leverage.

This shift also changes defensive prioritization. Traditional perimeter security assumed a boundary between outside and inside. Modern ransomware operations exploit the fact that the boundary is now distributed across VPNs, SaaS identity, cloud access brokers, endpoint agents, remote management platforms, CI/CD pipelines, and third-party service providers. A control failure in any one of these layers can give attackers a path to the recovery fabric. Therefore, resilience must be assessed as an end-to-end dependency graph rather than a set of isolated security products.

### 4. Identity, Backup, and Data-Theft Dependency Chains Before Encryption

This section overlaps thematically with previous recovery-infrastructure discussion, so the distinction is explicit: the earlier material described **which recovery assets are targeted and their business effect**; this section analyzes **the dependency chains and timing logic** by which attackers compromise identity and backup functions before encryption, often combining silent exfiltration with delayed coercion.

Identity is the first dependency chain because it mediates nearly every recovery action. If attackers gain privileged identity control before triggering visible disruption, they may weaken logging, create persistence, access cloud services, modify group policies, alter administrative memberships, disrupt domain controllers, or compromise service accounts used by backup and recovery platforms. Dela Security’s 2025 discussion of AD recovery states that ransomware groups increasingly target identity infrastructure because disabling Active Directory can mean no user logins, no email, no applications, and no recovery without identity; it further warns that many organizations treat AD backup as an afterthought with outdated tools, untested recovery plans, or no usable backups ([16](https://delasecurity.com/why-ad-backup-and-restore-is-critical-for-ransomware-resilience-in-2025/)).

The identity issue extends to cloud identity. Dela Security also notes that many organizations assume Microsoft backs up Microsoft Entra ID in a way that satisfies their recovery needs, while the organization remains responsible for protecting and recovering many identity objects, configurations, and operational dependencies ([17](https://delasecurity.com/why-ad-backup-and-restore-is-critical-for-ransomware-resilience-in-2025/)). Whether or not every statistic in commercial reporting should be treated as independently verified, the operational point is sound: cloud identity misconfiguration, token theft, SaaS administrative compromise, and identity synchronization failures can prevent recovery even when on-premises systems are restored.

Attackers benefit from compromising identity before encryption because it creates stealth and optionality. With valid credentials or session access, attackers can appear as legitimate administrators, access SaaS data directly, identify high-value repositories, examine backup schedules, enumerate privileged groups, and understand which systems are most critical. Industrial Cyber’s Dragos-based reporting describes affiliates using valid credentials, commodity infostealers, or IAB-provided access to authenticate into VPN portals, firewall interfaces, or vendor tunnels before moving laterally toward virtualization and OT-support systems ([8](https://industrialcyber.co/ransomware/ransomware-surge-in-2025-exposes-mounting-OT-risk-as-industrial-impacts-outpace-it-narratives/)). That pattern shows why credential security is not merely an access-control issue; it is a resilience issue.

Backup dependency chains are similarly vulnerable because backup systems often require broad read privileges, service accounts, network reachability, storage access, catalog visibility, and administrative consoles. Even where backups exist, attackers can increase uncertainty by targeting metadata, indexes, credentials, snapshots, replication processes, or backup management planes. CyberFortress and Veeam emphasize that resilience depends on securing backups, verifying integrity, and maintaining clear recovery processes before ransomware occurs, arguing that recovery should be the last step in a prepared defense rather than an improvised response ([18](https://recovery.cyberfortress.com/hubfs/Veeam%20Documents/ransomware-trends_v2.pdf)). This is particularly important where backup platforms protect both production and disaster recovery systems: compromise of the backup control plane can affect the victim’s entire recovery estate.

A critical dependency-chain problem is **identity-contaminated recovery**. If backup restoration depends on the same domain, same privileged accounts, same federation provider, same PAM system, or same remote management platform that attackers compromised, restoration may simply recreate attacker access. This is why organizations need recovery assumptions that include identity failure: break-glass access, clean administrative workstations, offline identity documentation, isolated recovery credentials, and tested procedures for rebuilding identity services before restoring dependent applications. Without these safeguards, organizations may restore servers into an environment where attackers still control authentication or can re-enter through previously established persistence.

Silent data theft changes the recovery calculation further. If attackers exfiltrate sensitive data before encryption, a victim’s ability to restore from backups does not eliminate extortion pressure. CYFIRMA’s January 2026 report highlights silent data theft and delayed extortion models that complicate detection, attribution, and response ([1](https://www.cyfirma.com/research/tracking-ransomware-jan-2026/)). ENISA similarly reports data breaches as a significant cybercrime category and notes that documented data breaches often resulted from ransomware incidents ([2](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf)). Thus, the attacker may pursue two parallel objectives: undermine restoration and obtain disclosure leverage.

The timing often works in favor of the attacker. Long-lived loaders and access-brokered footholds can allow reconnaissance before disruption. CYFIRMA notes a shift toward browser-centric, user-mediated access, brokered initial access models, and long-lived loaders that preserve optionality rather than immediately deploying encryption ([1](https://www.cyfirma.com/research/tracking-ransomware-jan-2026/)). This means that the most important defensive opportunities may occur before ransomware is visible. Unusual backup-console access, privilege changes, abnormal identity synchronization events, mass SaaS downloads, suspicious use of remote management tools, unexplained edge-device log gaps, or new service accounts may be early indicators of future extortion.

| Dependency Chain | Attacker Strategic Objective | Why Detection Is Difficult | Recovery Risk If Missed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Identity → privileged administration → backup access | Use legitimate authority to access backup consoles, disable protections, or learn recovery architecture. | Activity may appear as administrator behavior unless identity analytics


## Initial Access at Scale: Edge Devices, VPNs, Enterprise Software, and ICT Supply Chains

### Technology-Commonality Victimology: How Initial Access Is Decoupling Victim Selection From Sector Alone

While prior sections addressed overall victim demographics and the strategic targeting of recovery infrastructure, this section narrows the lens to a different causal layer: **how ransomware operators obtain scalable entry before selecting which victims are most monetizable**. The distinction matters. Sector-level victimology explains who suffers visible impact; technology-commonality analysis explains why unrelated victims in different sectors, countries, and sizes can be compromised in the same campaign window because they share the same exposed VPN, firewall, remote access stack, enterprise application, managed service provider, identity platform, virtualization layer, or software supplier.

The 2025 ransomware landscape shows that attackers increasingly build victim pools through **technology exposure first**, then triage targets by ransom leverage. This is consistent with ENISA’s assessment that EU cybercrime incidents remained broadly distributed across sectors, with cybercrime activity affecting both NIS2 and non-NIS2 entities and ransomware claims concentrated in manufacturing and digital infrastructure rather than a single narrow vertical ([1](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf)). In ENISA’s reporting period, cybercriminal activity targeting EU organizations was dominated by ransomware, which accounted for **81.1%** of documented cybercrime activity, while data breaches accounted for **15.2%**, often as a ransomware-linked consequence ([1](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf)). At the sector level, ENISA identified **digital infrastructure and services** as the most targeted EU sector for cybercrime overall at **13.7%**, followed by **manufacturing** at **13.26%** and **business services** at **9.7%**; within ransomware-specific claims, manufacturing led at **14.9%**, followed by digital infrastructure and services at **10.3%** ([1](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf)).

Those figures should not be interpreted only as attacker preference for factories or IT providers. They also reflect the fact that these sectors operate dense estates of internet-facing infrastructure, remote support channels, third-party integrations, operational technology support systems, supplier portals, backup environments, and identity federation paths. Manufacturing victims frequently have distributed plants, legacy remote access, engineering workstations, supplier connectivity, and high downtime costs. Digital infrastructure and services firms, by contrast, are attractive because they may provide access, hosting, administration, connectivity, or software dependencies to many downstream customers. Business services organizations are often targeted because they maintain sensitive client data and privileged connectivity into customer environments. The ransomware victim is therefore increasingly not just the organization with the weakest password or most lucrative revenue profile; it is often the organization whose **shared technology footprint** overlaps with a weaponized exposure.

GuidePoint Security’s GRIT data reinforces this shift toward broad but structurally patterned targeting. GRIT tracked **7,515 publicly posted ransomware victims** in 2025, an average of **20.6 victims per day**, and monitored **124 named ransomware groups**, reporting a **58% year-over-year increase** in observed victims ([2](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)). GRIT also found that manufacturing accounted for **22%** of observed ransomware attacks and technology for **14%**, with manufacturing, technology, retail and wholesale, and healthcare remaining the four most impacted industries across 2024 and 2025 ([2](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)). These sectors share a common feature: their operational model magnifies the value of initial access. Manufacturing interruption can stop production and logistics; technology providers may expose source code, customer environments, credentials, or managed infrastructure; retail and wholesale organizations process payments and supply-chain data; healthcare organizations combine operational urgency with sensitive regulated data.

Geographic concentration also has to be read through technology-commonality rather than only through national targeting intent. ENISA noted country-level ransomware patterns in Europe, including Qilin, Akira, and FOG activity in Spain, Qilin, Hunters International, and CL0P activity in France, and RansomHouse, Play, SafePay, and Qilin activity in Belgium; in these countries, manufacturing was repeatedly among the most targeted sectors, often followed by digital infrastructure and services or business services ([1](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf)). This does not prove a single unified campaign against one national economy. A more defensible interpretation is that wealthy, digitized economies with dense industrial bases, managed IT ecosystems, and high insurance or revenue potential create larger pools of monetizable victims once scalable access is obtained.

The same logic applies to the United States, Canada, Australia, the United Kingdom, and Western Europe more broadly. These economies have high concentrations of internet-exposed remote access appliances, cloud identity integrations, MSP-dependent small and mid-sized enterprises, critical infrastructure suppliers, healthcare networks, and manufacturing clusters. Attackers do not need to decide at the outset that a particular country or sector is the objective. They can compromise exposed technologies at scale, enrich the resulting access with business intelligence, and then select victims based on revenue, downtime sensitivity, data sensitivity, cyber-insurance posture, geopolitical visibility, or likelihood of paying.

This “access first, victim selection second” model helps explain why ransomware operators and access brokers have converged on edge devices, VPNs, enterprise software, and ICT suppliers. These targets produce **batchable access**. A single vulnerability class, exposed management interface, supplier compromise, credential-theft campaign, or remote monitoring platform abuse can yield multiple downstream candidates. Ransomware affiliates can then prioritize organizations where extortion leverage is highest: manufacturers facing production stoppage, hospitals facing clinical disruption, municipalities facing public-service interruption, technology firms facing customer trust loss, or MSPs whose compromise threatens many clients.

National cyber defense agencies have implicitly recognized this shift through their emphasis on known-exploited vulnerabilities, secure configuration, identity hardening, and third-party risk. CISA’s Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog is structured around the operational reality that adversaries repeatedly exploit the same high-value vulnerabilities across many organizations, making remediation prioritization a strategic risk decision rather than a purely technical patching function ([4](https://www.cisa.gov/known-exploited-vulnerabilities-catalog)). Joint #StopRansomware guidance from CISA, the FBI, NSA, and partner agencies similarly emphasizes that ransomware actors commonly gain access through phishing, stolen credentials, exploitation of vulnerabilities, and remote access services before escalating privileges, disabling defenses, exfiltrating data, and deploying ransomware ([5](https://www.cisa.gov/stopransomware)). The policy signal is clear: ransomware initial access is no longer a scattered collection of one-off intrusions; it is a repeatable ecosystem that exploits common enterprise dependencies.

### Edge and VPN Exposure as a Ransomware Market-Making Mechanism

While previous content discussed perimeter bypass in general terms, this section focuses on the **market-making function** of edge exploitation: compromised VPNs, firewalls, secure gateways, SD-WAN appliances, and management consoles create a tradable supply of access for ransomware affiliates, access brokers, and extortion crews. The operational importance of edge devices is not simply that they are internet-facing. It is that they often sit at the intersection of authentication, routing, remote administration, network segmentation, logging, and trust enforcement. When these systems fail, the attacker may enter through the same pathway legitimate administrators use to maintain the environment.

Edge devices are especially valuable for ransomware operators because they can compress the early intrusion timeline. A compromised VPN or firewall may provide a route into internal address space without triggering endpoint detection at the point of entry. Many edge appliances run specialized operating systems, generate logs that are not retained as long as Windows or cloud identity logs, and are managed by network teams rather than security operations teams. Firmware-level visibility is often weaker than endpoint visibility. Some devices lack mature endpoint detection integrations altogether. As a result, the first visible sign of compromise may not be exploitation of the edge device itself, but subsequent authentication events, lateral movement, anomalous administrative access, backup-console interaction, or data staging.

Public reporting across 2024 and 2025 showed ransomware and state-linked actors rapidly exploiting flaws in internet-facing security and remote-access technologies. Industrial Cyber summarized that adversaries aggressively exploited perimeter technologies including VPNs, firewalls, and edge appliances from vendors such as Ivanti, Palo Alto Networks, Fortinet, F5, and Cisco, with many vulnerabilities enabling authentication bypass, privilege escalation, or remote code execution through exposed interfaces and VPN services ([6](https://industrialcyber.co/ransomware/ransomware-surge-in-2025-exposes-mounting-ot-risk-as-industrial-impacts-outpace-it-narratives/)). Verizon’s DBIR also reported that vulnerability exploitation accounted for about **20%** of breaches, rising **34% year over year**, driven significantly by flaws in internet-facing edge devices such as VPNs and firewalls ([3](https://www.verizon.com/business/resources/reports/dbir/)). This is a major strategic shift: vulnerability exploitation is not merely an opportunistic supplement to phishing; it has become a scalable acquisition channel.

The early 2026 edge-device environment further illustrates why “patch quickly” is necessary but insufficient. Disaster Recovery Journal, summarizing AWS threat intelligence, reported exploitation activity against Cisco Firewall Management Center vulnerability CVE-2026-20131 detected in honeypot telemetry before Cisco released a patch, placing the issue within a broader pattern of critical vulnerabilities affecting major security vendors and edge devices in 2026 ([7](https://drj.com/industry_news/cyber-security-intelligence-analysis-of-edge-devices-amid-growing-vulnerabilities/)). The defensive implication is not that every organization can prevent all zero-day exposure. Rather, organizations must assume that edge devices can be compromised before normal patch workflows begin and must therefore maintain compensating controls: management-plane isolation, reduced exposure, independent log export, configuration baselining, privileged-session monitoring, and rapid containment procedures.

The ransomware ecosystem benefits from edge exploitation in at least five ways. First, these devices are widely deployed across high-value sectors, producing broad target sets. Second, the devices are reachable from the internet, allowing scanning and exploitation at scale. Third, they often mediate remote work, vendor support, branch connectivity, or disaster recovery connectivity, increasing the attacker’s chance of reaching privileged internal systems. Fourth, edge compromise may yield credentials, configuration files, internal routes, certificates, or session material that helps attackers blend into legitimate traffic. Fifth, remediation is often disruptive, because emergency replacement or reimaging of a firewall, VPN concentrator, or SD-WAN gateway can interrupt business connectivity.

| Edge Access Attribute | Why It Matters to Ransomware Operators | Victimology Consequence | Defensive Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Internet exposure | Attackers can scan for vulnerable versions or misconfigurations across many organizations. | Victim pools form around shared product exposure rather than sector alone. | Maintain continuous external attack-surface inventory and compare exposed assets against KEV and vendor advisories. |
| Pre-endpoint position | Entry may occur before endpoint telemetry has visibility. | Intrusions can mature before EDR alerts appear on internal hosts. | Export edge logs to SIEM, retain them long enough for retroactive hunting, and monitor management-plane changes. |
| Administrative trust | Edge devices often terminate VPN sessions, enforce access rules, and connect privileged users. | Compromise can provide a route to identity systems, management networks, backup platforms, or hypervisors. | Restrict administrative interfaces, require phishing-resistant MFA for management, and segment management networks. |
| Patch friction | Firmware updates may require maintenance windows, HA failover planning, or vendor support. | High-downtime sectors may delay patches, creating longer exposure windows. | Use emergency change procedures for exploited edge flaws and compensating controls when immediate patching is impossible. |
| Configuration sensitivity | Firewall and VPN configuration can reveal internal topology and trust relationships. | Attackers can identify high-value internal systems before noisy activity. | Treat configuration files as sensitive secrets; monitor unauthorized exports and changes. |
| Vendor concentration | A small number of products are deployed across many sectors and geographies. | A single vulnerability can create simultaneous multi-sector exposure. | Use product exposure mapping to assess concentration risk and prioritize strategic diversification or compensating architecture. |

The operational-technology dimension is particularly important for manufacturing, energy, water, transportation, and logistics. Dragos reporting cited by Industrial Cyber observed ransomware affiliates using valid credentials, commodity infostealers, or initial-access-brokered access to authenticate into VPN portals, firewall interfaces, or vendor tunnels before pivoting toward OT boundary networks; once inside, affiliates used common administrative protocols to move toward VMware ESXi hypervisors and OT-support servers hosting SCADA, HMI, historian, and engineering workloads ([6](https://industrialcyber.co/ransomware/ransomware-surge-in-2025-exposes-mounting-ot-risk-as-industrial-impacts-outpace-it-narratives/)). The strategic issue is not that ransomware crews necessarily understand industrial processes deeply at the beginning of every intrusion. It is that edge access can place them close enough to systems whose unavailability creates acute pressure to pay.

For victim organizations, this changes the meaning of “perimeter.” Traditional perimeter security assumed a boundary between outside and inside. In 2025 and early 2026, ransomware operators increasingly targeted the devices that define and enforce that boundary. If a VPN concentrator, firewall, secure access gateway, or SD-WAN controller is compromised, the attacker is not merely bypassing a control; the attacker may be operating through a trusted control plane. This is why national agencies and industry responders increasingly emphasize configuration verification and compromise assessment after edge-device exploitation, not just patch installation. A patched but previously compromised appliance may still have altered configurations, stolen credentials, malicious accounts, persistence mechanisms, or compromised downstream systems.

The risk is amplified by the mismatch between enterprise asset management and edge reality. Many organizations have mature inventories for servers and endpoints but weaker inventories for virtual appliances, cloud-hosted gateways, branch firewalls, legacy VPN concentrators, test portals, and acquired-company infrastructure. Ransomware operators exploit those inconsistencies. An unmaintained appliance at a small branch, a forgotten vendor VPN, or a management interface exposed for troubleshooting may be enough to start an intrusion chain. This helps explain why SMBs and mid-market organizations appear heavily affected in ransomware breach data. Verizon’s finding that ransomware was present in **88%** of SMB breaches compared with **39%** of large-enterprise breaches is consistent with the hypothesis that smaller organizations face a harsher combination of exposure, limited telemetry, dependence on MSPs, and reduced patch capacity ([3](https://www.verizon.com/business/resources/reports/dbir/)).

Edge exploitation also produces geographic clustering without requiring geographic intent. Countries with higher concentrations of digitized manufacturing, managed services, healthcare networks, public-sector portals, and remote work infrastructure will naturally produce more visible victims when a widely deployed edge technology is exploited. ENISA’s observations of ransomware activity across Spain, France, Belgium, and other EU countries should therefore be assessed against both national-sector exposure and shared technology deployment patterns ([1](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf)). In practice, a ransomware group may appear to “target” a country because exposed infrastructure, language-accessible affiliates, business density, or local payment capacity makes monetization more efficient after broad exploitation.

### Enterprise Software, Vulnerable Drivers, and Management Planes as Trust-Abuse Pathways

While previous content briefly identified vulnerable drivers and enterprise software as bypass vectors, this section expands the analysis into **trust abuse inside the enterprise stack**. Ransomware initial access at scale does not end at VPNs and firewalls. Attackers also pursue enterprise applications, remote management systems, backup-adjacent software, file-transfer platforms, identity-integrated applications, and signed drivers because these components already possess privileges that malware would otherwise need to obtain noisily.

Enterprise software exploitation is particularly attractive because such platforms often sit at the convergence of data, identity, and administration. File-transfer applications may hold sensitive data and external connectivity. IT management platforms may deploy software, run scripts, collect inventories, and authenticate to many endpoints. Collaboration and document platforms may contain credentials, contracts, customer data, and recovery documentation. Backup and storage management software may have privileged access to large data volumes. ERP, CRM, and HR platforms may hold high-value regulated data. When these systems are exposed to the internet or integrated with weak identity controls, exploitation can produce both extortion data and operational leverage.

Verizon’s 2025 DBIR finding that vulnerability exploitation represented a growing share of breaches is important in this context because exploitation increasingly affects **business-critical enterprise software**, not only network appliances ([3](https://www.verizon.com/business/resources/reports/dbir/)). Ransomware groups and data-extortion actors have repeatedly shown interest in managed file-transfer products, collaboration platforms, remote monitoring and management tools, and administrative applications because they offer high-value access with fewer steps than workstation compromise. ENISA’s observation that cybercriminal claims included both ransomware and ransomware-linked data breaches underscores that initial access may be monetized through encryption, exfiltration, or both ([1](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf)).

The strategic role of vulnerable or abused drivers is different but complementary. Signed drivers are trusted by operating systems because they normally support legitimate hardware, security, monitoring, or administrative functions. In ransomware intrusions, attackers may attempt to abuse vulnerable drivers to interfere with endpoint security tools, evade detection, or manipulate low-level system behavior. This technique is often discussed under “bring your own vulnerable driver” risk. The defensive concern is not simply that a driver can be abused; it is that driver abuse can occur after initial access to degrade visibility before the most damaging phases of an intrusion. Microsoft has emphasized driver blocklists and vulnerable-driver protections as part of reducing kernel-level abuse risk in Windows environments ([8](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/security/application-security/application-control/app-control-for-business/design/microsoft-recommended-driver-block-rules)).

From a ransomware-resilience perspective, driver abuse and enterprise software exploitation converge around the same objective: **control the management fabric before defenders understand the incident scope**. If attackers exploit an enterprise management platform, they may gain legitimate distribution paths. If they compromise an identity-integrated application, they may obtain tokens or credentials. If they abuse a vulnerable driver, they may weaken endpoint controls. If they reach virtualization management, they may disrupt multiple servers simultaneously. If they access backup software, they may delete restore points or tamper with recovery repositories. None of these steps requires detailed public exploit instructions to understand defensively; the pattern is trust inversion.

| Trust-Abuse Target | Legitimate Enterprise Function | Ransomware-Relevant Abuse Pattern | Why It Supports Scale | Defensive Priority |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| IT management platforms | Software deployment, scripting, inventory, remote support | Use of legitimate administrative channels after compromise | One platform can reach many endpoints or clients | Isolate admin consoles, enforce least privilege, log all remote actions, and monitor unusual task creation. |
| Managed file-transfer systems | External data exchange with partners and customers | Data theft without broad endpoint compromise | A single exposed platform can hold high-value bulk data | Internet exposure review, rapid patching, strong authentication, and anomaly detection for bulk downloads. |
| Collaboration and document systems | Knowledge management, project data, credentials in documents | Reconnaissance, data theft, and recovery-plan discovery | High data density and broad user access | Data-loss monitoring, sensitive-document controls, conditional access, and audit-log retention. |
| Backup and storage management software | Data protection, replication, restore orchestration | Tampering with restore points or discovering critical systems | Centralized control over recovery assets | Separate administrative identities, immutable storage, and independent monitoring of deletion or policy changes. |
| Virtualization management | Administration of many servers and workloads | Disruption of multiple systems through a central control plane | One console can affect many workloads | Management-plane segmentation, MFA, dedicated admin workstations, and offline recovery plans. |
| Signed drivers | Hardware support, security tooling, kernel-level functionality | Security-control interference after access | Reusable technique across many Windows estates | Driver blocklists, application control, EDR tamper protection, and privileged-driver installation monitoring. |

The attraction of enterprise software is also economic. Ransomware groups and access brokers do not need to compromise every endpoint manually if they can compromise the systems used to administer those endpoints. This is especially relevant in technology, digital infrastructure, MSP, and business-services environments, which ENISA and GRIT both identify as significant victim categories ([1](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf), [2](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)). It also explains why healthcare remains persistently exposed. Healthcare networks often depend on specialized applications, legacy systems, third-party remote support, identity federation, and high-availability requirements that complicate emergency patching and segmentation.

Veeam’s 2025 ransomware research provides a useful resilience lens. Veeam surveyed **1,300 organizations**, including **900** that had experienced at least one ransomware attack involving encryption or exfiltration in the prior 12 months, and found that nearly **7 in 10** organizations experienced an attack in the past year; among attacked organizations, only **10%** recovered more than **90%** of their data, while **57%** recovered less than **50%** ([9](https://www.veeam.com/company/press-release/veeam-report-finds-close-to-70-percent-of-organizations-still-under-cyber-attack-despite-improved-defenses.html)). Those figures show why attackers are incentivized to target management and recovery-adjacent software. If recovery outcomes are already weak, any initial access path that helps attackers find, disable, encrypt, or undermine backup and restore systems materially increases coercive pressure.

The data-exfiltration dimension further changes how enterprise software exploitation should be evaluated. Veeam’s ransomware trends report notes that threat actors who gain access often use lateral movement, look for ease of exfiltration, or pursue specific objectives such as compromising VMware ESXi hypervisors to coerce payment ([10](https://recovery.cyberfortress.com/hubfs/Veeam%20Documents/ransomware-trends_v2.pdf)). This supports the broader trend from encryption-only ransomware to multi-pressure extortion. A compromised enterprise application may be enough to steal sensitive data even if encryption is never deployed. Conversely, stolen data can be used to pressure victims while attackers also target backups and hypervisors to reduce confidence in recovery.

The vulnerable-driver issue also illustrates a governance challenge. Many organizations focus vulnerability management on internet-facing CVEs and patchable applications but lack equivalent control over kernel driver exposure, local administrator rights, bring-your-own-tool risk, and security-tool tampering. Driver abuse is therefore a second-stage enabler rather than a first-stage entry method in many cases, but it is highly relevant to initial-access-at-scale campaigns because scalable access only becomes profitable if attackers can survive long enough to conduct reconnaissance and extortion preparation. Defenders should treat unexpected driver installation, security-service changes, tamper-protection events, and abnormal administrative tooling as possible ransomware precursor activity, especially after any edge, VPN, or enterprise software exposure.

A critical analytical point is that exploitation of enterprise software and drivers blurs the line between “initial access” and “post-exploitation.” For defenders, the distinction is less useful than the sequence of control. Attackers seek the earliest point at which they can assume a trusted role: VPN user, application server, software deployment administrator, identity operator, backup administrator, virtualization administrator, or kernel-level actor. Once they occupy that trusted role, later ransomware actions may look like administrative operations until it is too late. This is why defensive logging must cover not only malware detections but also management-plane behavior: new admin accounts, unusual API calls, abnormal policy changes, mass remote execution, unexpected backup deletion, new federation settings, and privileged access from edge-originating sessions.

### ICT Supply Chains, MSPs, and Third-Party Access as Downstream Ransomware Multipliers

While earlier sections mentioned software supply-chain dependencies and MSP platforms as vectors, this section focuses on their **victimology consequences**: supply-chain and third-party compromise can turn one intrusion into many potential ransomware victims and can shift impact from the directly compromised entity to downstream organizations that trusted it. This is central to mapping ransomware strategy in 2025 and early 2026 because the most efficient ransomware operators increasingly seek access paths that multiply reach.

Verizon’s 2025 DBIR reported that third-party breaches rose to **30%** of cases, approximately double the prior year’s share ([3](https://www.verizon.com/business/resources/reports/dbir/)). For ransomware analysis, that statistic is more than a third-party-risk metric. It indicates that extortion crews and access brokers benefit from the structural interconnectedness of modern ICT: MSPs administer endpoints, SaaS providers hold business data, cloud platforms host workloads, identity providers federate trust, software vendors distribute updates, and niche suppliers maintain privileged remote access into larger customers. The victim may experience ransomware as a direct attack, but the access path may originate in a vendor, service provider, software dependency, or managed tool.

Managed service providers are especially important because they concentrate administrative privilege. Many small and mid-sized organizations rely on MSPs for endpoint management, patching, backups, identity administration, network support, and security monitoring. This dependence helps explain why SMBs are disproportionately represented in ransomware breach data. Verizon’s finding that ransomware appeared in **88%** of SMB breaches suggests that smaller organizations often face a combination of high operational dependence and limited independent verification of third-party controls ([3](https://www.verizon.com/business/resources/reports/dbir/)). If an MSP’s remote monitoring and management environment is compromised, downstream clients may inherit risk through tools they explicitly trust and allow.

The ICT supply-chain issue also affects larger enterprises and public-sector entities. Large organizations may have stronger internal security teams, but they depend on thousands of vendors, contractors, SaaS providers, integrators, and software components. Attackers can target smaller suppliers as stepping stones into larger ecosystems. This is why national cyber agencies increasingly frame ransomware resilience as an ecosystem problem. CISA’s #StopRansomware resources emphasize risk-reduction practices that include patching, credential protection, backups, network segmentation, and incident reporting, but the same guidance also implicitly assumes that organizations must manage external dependencies because ransomware actors exploit remote access, valid credentials, and vulnerable systems across trust boundaries ([5](https://www.cisa.gov/stopransomware)). The UK National Cyber Security Centre similarly emphasizes malware and ransomware mitigation through preparation, backups, access control, vulnerability management, and incident planning, all of which become harder when critical services are outsourced or federated across suppliers ([11](https://www.ncsc.gov.uk/guidance/mitigating-malware-and-ransomware-attacks)).

Supply-chain ransomware risk should be divided into several categories rather than treated as a single phenomenon.

| Third-Party Pathway | How It Enables Ransomware at Scale | Typical Downstream Risk | Sectors Most Exposed | Key Governance Question |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| MSP or MSSP compromise | One provider may administer many customer environments. | Remote tool abuse, credential exposure, backup tampering, endpoint deployment misuse. | SMBs, healthcare clinics, local government, professional services, regional manufacturing. | Can each customer environment be isolated with unique credentials, logging, and least privilege? |
| SaaS provider compromise | Centralized business data and identity integrations may be exposed. | Data theft, token abuse, business-email compromise, extortion without encryption. | Technology, finance, education, retail, public administration. | Are SaaS logs retained, monitored, and contractually available during incidents? |
| Software update or package compromise | Trusted distribution channels can deliver malicious or vulnerable components. | Re-compromise after rebuild, credential theft, developer-environment compromise. | Technology, software development, cloud-native firms, digital services. | Are software integrity, SBOMs, signing, and dependency controls verified? |
| Vendor remote-access channel | Suppliers maintain access for support and maintenance. | Entry into operational or management networks through trusted tunnels. | Manufacturing, OT, healthcare, energy, transportation. | Is vendor access time-bound, monitored, segmented, and disabled by default? |
| Cloud or hosting dependency | Shared infrastructure supports many tenants or customer workloads. | Outages, data exposure, identity compromise, recovery complexity. | Digital infrastructure, SaaS, e-commerce, public sector. | Are tenant boundaries, privileged access, and recovery responsibilities contractually defined? |
| System integrator access | Integrators hold knowledge of architecture and privileged credentials. | Lateral movement from project environments into production. | Government, defense suppliers, industrials, large enterprises. | Are project credentials rotated and removed after delivery? |

The 2025–early 2026 ransomware environment also shows that supply-chain exposure can alter geographic impact. A ransomware group may compromise an MSP in one country and affect clients in multiple regions. A software dependency compromise may affect users globally, regardless of the attacker’s original geographic focus. Conversely, a local-language MSP, regional cloud provider, or country-specific software platform may create geographically concentrated victim clusters. This means analysts should distinguish between **target geography** and **dependency geography**. Target geography describes where victims are located. Dependency geography describes where the compromised supplier, software ecosystem, hosting provider, or managed access path is concentrated.

ENISA’s EU observations are useful here because they show both sectoral breadth and country-level variation. Spain, France, and Belgium experienced activity from different ransomware brands, with manufacturing repeatedly prominent and digital infrastructure or business services also significant ([1](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025


## Privilege Escalation and Defense Evasion: Vulnerable Drivers, BYOVD Techniques, Zero-Days, and Compressed Patch Windows

### Incremental Scope: Control-Plane Compression After Access, Not Another Initial-Access Inventory

While the previous subtopic reports addressed ransomware ecosystem scale, broad victim demographics, recovery-infrastructure targeting, and perimeter bypass through edge devices and ICT supply chains, this section narrows the lens to a later but strategically decisive phase: how ransomware operators convert an initial foothold into privileged, low-visibility control fast enough to disable defenses, suppress recovery, and increase coercive leverage. The distinction matters because ransomware victimology in 2025 and early 2026 is no longer explained only by “who was exposed” at the perimeter; it is increasingly explained by which victims allow attackers to compress the interval between first access, privilege escalation, security-tool impairment, backup interference, identity abuse, and enterprise-wide impact.

The operational center of gravity is therefore **post-compromise acceleration**. Local elevation-of-privilege vulnerabilities, vulnerable signed drivers, endpoint security tampering, identity-plane misuse, and management-platform abuse do not usually select the victim by themselves. Instead, they determine whether a ransomware intrusion against a hospital, manufacturer, school district, legal services firm, municipality, logistics provider, or managed service provider becomes a contained incident or an enterprise outage. In that sense, privilege escalation and defense evasion function as the “conversion layer” between scalable access and extortion success.

This section also differs from prior coverage of edge devices. The earlier sections analyzed edge exploitation as a scalable way to enter organizations. Here, edge compromise is treated as one input into a broader **compressed patch-window problem**: threat actors exploit internet-facing weaknesses, then use local privilege escalation and defense evasion to maintain momentum before defenders can patch, hunt, isolate, or rebuild. CISA’s 2026 directive on unsupported edge devices explicitly characterizes end-of-support firewalls, routers, network security appliances, and related edge technologies as a persistent and substantial threat because they sit at the boundary, are often internet reachable, and integrate deeply with enterprise identity and privileged access paths ([1](https://www.cybersecuritydive.com/news/cisa-edge-devices-binding-operational-directive/811539/)). That boundary exposure becomes more damaging when internal Windows privilege-escalation flaws or vulnerable drivers give attackers a second route to authority after the appliance foothold.

The strategic ransomware value chain can be summarized as follows:

| Phase of Intrusion Economics | Defensive Control Being Undermined | Why Privilege Escalation / Evasion Matters | Ransomware-Relevant Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| Initial access | Perimeter controls, exposed services, credentials, third-party access | Often gives only partial access, unstable persistence, or limited privileges | Access becomes monetizable only if expanded |
| Privilege escalation | Least privilege, endpoint containment, segmentation assumptions | Enables attackers to move from user/application context to administrative control | Faster domain, server, or backup targeting |
| Defense evasion | EDR telemetry, logging, antivirus, tamper controls | Reduces probability of detection during reconnaissance and staging | Longer undisturbed preparation window |
| Recovery suppression | Backup integrity, identity recovery, disaster recovery plans | Prevents rapid restoration and increases operational panic | Greater pressure to pay or negotiate |
| Extortion execution | Business continuity, legal exposure, customer trust | Encryption and data theft become more coercive when recovery options fail | Higher leverage against targeted sectors |

This framework helps explain why ransomware operators continue to pursue privilege-escalation bugs even when phishing, stolen credentials, and edge exploitation remain common access routes. A low-privilege account, webshell, VPN session, or compromised appliance is useful, but not sufficient for large-scale extortion. Attackers must reach administrative surfaces: domain controllers, virtualization clusters, backup consoles, endpoint-management tools, file servers, and cloud identity administrators. Local privilege escalation and defense evasion shorten that route.

The defensive implication is that patch prioritization cannot be based only on whether a vulnerability is remotely exploitable or internet-facing. In ransomware intrusions, “local” vulnerabilities can be strategically critical once an adversary has any foothold. This is especially true for Windows kernel, driver, file-system, graphics, logging, networking, and authentication components because they may allow attackers to escape constrained execution contexts, tamper with security tooling, or prepare enterprise-wide deployment. Microsoft’s May 2025 Patch Tuesday cycle illustrates this problem: multiple actively exploited elevation-of-privilege vulnerabilities affected Windows components including the Common Log File System, Ancillary Function Driver for WinSock, and Desktop Window Manager Core Library, all of which matter after attackers already have local execution ([2](https://www.crowdstrike.com/en-us/blog/patch-tuesday-analysis-may-2025/), [3](https://www.helpnetsecurity.com/2025/05/13/patch-tuesday-microsoft-fixes-5-actively-exploited-zero-days/)).

From a victimology perspective, this shifts attention toward organizations where privilege escalation quickly translates into operational disruption. Sectors with large Windows server estates, complex identity environments, distributed endpoint fleets, heterogeneous legacy assets, and high recovery-time sensitivity face disproportionate risk. These include healthcare and public health, critical manufacturing, government services, education, information technology, financial services, legal and professional services, logistics, and managed service providers. FBI reporting has consistently shown ransomware incidents affecting critical infrastructure sectors, with 2024 complaints including particularly high volumes across healthcare and public health, critical manufacturing, government facilities, information technology, and financial services ([4](https://www.ic3.gov/AnnualReport/Reports/2024_IC3Report.pdf)). Those sectors are not only attractive because of data sensitivity; they are attractive because privileged disruption creates immediate consequences.

The ransomware groups and affiliate ecosystems active through 2025 and early 2026 benefited from this control-plane logic regardless of brand. Affiliates do not need one universal exploit chain if they can combine commodity credentials, edge exposure, living-off-the-land administration, local privilege escalation, and security-tool impairment. The resulting attack pattern is modular: one actor or broker supplies access, another escalates and evades, another handles exfiltration, and another conducts negotiations. This modularity increases pressure on defenders because control failures at any stage can compensate for stronger controls elsewhere.

The pattern is also geographically uneven but not geographically narrow. U.S., U.K., Canadian, Australian, and European organizations remain highly represented in public ransomware reporting because of larger digital footprints, higher insurance penetration, stronger disclosure norms, and higher perceived ability to pay. However, the privilege-escalation and evasion layer is technology-driven rather than geography-driven. A Windows kernel zero-day, vulnerable signed driver, unsupported firewall, backup console weakness, or MSP remote-management compromise can affect victims across jurisdictions simultaneously. This produces a hybrid victimology: extortion decisions are financially and sectorally targeted, but technical exposure is often globally distributed.

The compression of patch windows reinforces that hybrid model. CISA’s Known Exploited Vulnerabilities program exists because defenders must act on evidence of exploitation, not just theoretical severity, and CISA continues to add exploited vulnerabilities to the catalog as threats emerge ([5](https://www.cisa.gov/news-events/cybersecurity-advisories)). In ransomware-relevant environments, once exploitation is confirmed, the window for normal monthly patch governance may already be too long. Edge appliances, identity infrastructure, backup platforms, hypervisors, and Windows servers often require maintenance windows, compatibility testing, and business approval. Attackers exploit that friction: they do not need every organization to delay, only enough high-value organizations with slow change control, unsupported devices, or incomplete asset visibility.

The strategic result is a ransomware landscape in which privilege escalation and evasion are best understood as **resilience denial accelerants**. They accelerate the attacker’s path to privileged systems, deny defenders telemetry, and enable deliberate targeting of backup and identity infrastructure before an organization can complete emergency response. This is why local EoP flaws and BYOVD techniques should be treated as ransomware-enabling risks, not merely endpoint-hardening issues.

### Vulnerable Signed Drivers and BYOVD as Ransomware Visibility-Denial Infrastructure

While prior content briefly identified vulnerable drivers as trust-abuse pathways, this section expands the analysis into why driver-level abuse is strategically important for ransomware operators specifically. The key issue is not that a vulnerable driver provides initial access at internet scale. In most ransomware cases, it does not. The strategic issue is that a signed kernel driver can become a **visibility-denial instrument** after access has been obtained. That makes it valuable during the short interval when attackers are preparing to tamper with backup systems, elevate identity privileges, disable security agents, stage data theft, or execute encryption.

Bring-your-own-vulnerable-driver techniques exploit a trust asymmetry in endpoint defense. Drivers often operate at highly privileged levels of the operating system because legitimate vendors need low-level access for hardware, monitoring, diagnostics, or security functions. If a driver is digitally signed but contains exploitable weaknesses, attackers may try to introduce or abuse it to interfere with security controls, escalate privileges, or execute privileged actions while benefiting from the apparent legitimacy of signed code. Industry reporting notes that BYOVD involves legitimate but vulnerable drivers, often digitally signed by trusted vendors or Microsoft, and that such drivers can operate at kernel level with the ability to disable security tools or evade default Windows trust checks ([6](https://cubexgroup.com/industry-articles/)).

The ransomware relevance is acute because endpoint detection and response tools are one of the main barriers between partial compromise and enterprise-scale damage. If attackers can blind or degrade EDR on selected servers, they can act against high-value assets with less interference. Backup servers, domain controllers, virtualization hosts, file servers, remote-management servers, and security-management consoles become priority targets. The tactic is therefore not merely technical evasion; it is a way to enable resilience suppression.

The commoditization of driver-abuse knowledge raises the risk. Public defensive research has cataloged large numbers of vulnerable drivers, and reporting notes that open-source projects and tools have lowered the barrier to identifying or operationalizing vulnerable-driver abuse, even though defenders should avoid treating such public knowledge as a guide for offensive replication ([6](https://cubexgroup.com/industry-articles/)). The policy-relevant point is that a technique once associated with more advanced operators can diffuse into ransomware affiliate ecosystems when tooling, driver lists, and procedural knowledge become easier to obtain. This mirrors the broader ransomware-as-a-service model: affiliates often do not need deep vulnerability research capabilities if they can reuse known weak components and commodity tooling.

The following table frames vulnerable-driver abuse from a defensive ransomware perspective without providing operational instructions:

| Driver-Abuse Dimension | Defensive Interpretation | Why It Matters to Ransomware | High-Level Control Objective |
|---|---|---|---|
| Signed-code trust | The operating system may allow components because they appear legitimate | Attackers can exploit legitimacy to reduce detection probability | Enforce vulnerable-driver blocklists and application control |
| Kernel privilege | Driver-level access can affect low-level security visibility | Endpoint agents may be impaired before destructive actions | Monitor tamper events and protect security services |
| Local execution dependency | Usually requires some prior access | Works well after phishing, credential theft, edge compromise, or software exploitation | Treat local EoP and driver events as post-access escalation signals |
| Tooling diffusion | Public catalogs and tools reduce specialist barriers | RaaS affiliates can adopt techniques without original research | Harden policy baselines rather than relying on attacker sophistication assumptions |
| Recovery targeting | Evasion may precede backup deletion or identity manipulation | Less telemetry during the most consequential phase | Correlate driver events with backup, identity, and admin-plane changes |

This threat model is especially relevant to organizations with broad local administrator rights, weak software allowlisting, inconsistent EDR tamper protection, incomplete driver inventory, and unmanaged legacy hardware support packages. Those weaknesses are common in large enterprises, hospitals, manufacturers, school systems, municipal governments, and acquired corporate networks because endpoint fleets accumulate years of vendor software, specialized equipment drivers, and exceptions. In operational technology-adjacent environments, manufacturing and healthcare systems may depend on legacy applications or vendor-supported devices that complicate aggressive driver removal. That gives attackers more places to hide among legitimate dependencies.

The issue also intersects with cyber insurance, legal exposure, and crisis governance. If a ransomware actor disables endpoint tooling before deleting backups or encrypting systems, incident responders may struggle to reconstruct timeline, scope, and data-access evidence. That uncertainty affects breach notification, regulatory reporting, ransom negotiation, and restoration sequencing. For hospitals, uncertainty can delay return to normal clinical operations; for manufacturers, it can complicate safe restart of production lines; for local governments, it can delay restoration of citizen services; for financial services, it can trigger heightened supervisory scrutiny.

Zero-trust architecture does not eliminate this problem unless it extends to endpoint trust decisions and administrative tooling. Many organizations apply zero-trust concepts to user authentication and network segmentation while still allowing broad administrative installation privileges or trusting signed drivers by default. Ransomware operators benefit from that mismatch. If an attacker compromises an administrator account and can introduce or activate vulnerable signed components, the organization’s identity controls may have been bypassed by operating inside a trusted administrative context.

Driver governance should therefore be linked directly to recovery-infrastructure protection. Backup servers and management consoles should have stricter driver and software policies than ordinary workstations. Domain controllers, certificate authorities, privileged access workstations, hypervisor managers, and EDR management servers should be treated similarly. The goal is not only malware prevention; it is preservation of trustworthy recovery and investigative capability. A backup server that is technically recoverable but whose logs, agents, or integrity controls were disabled may not support confident restoration.

The emerging Windows Secure Boot and certificate-maintenance issue reinforces this point. Reporting in the provided source set highlights concern around a critical Windows Secure Boot security-feature bypass, CVE-2026-21265, tied to certificate transitions and the risk that devices lacking newer certificates may become unable to receive relevant Secure Boot security fixes after older certificate expiration dates in 2026 ([6](https://cubexgroup.com/industry-articles/)). For ransomware defense, Secure Boot is relevant because boot integrity, rootkit resistance, and trusted recovery are part of endpoint resilience. Any degradation in boot-chain assurance can make post-incident validation harder, especially on servers that support recovery operations.

This does not mean every ransomware incident uses bootkits, kernel implants, or BYOVD. Most do not need to. The more important analytical conclusion is that ransomware operators select the least costly path to suppress detection and recovery. In some environments, stolen domain admin credentials are sufficient. In others, endpoint tampering or vulnerable-driver abuse may help defeat well-deployed EDR. In still others, unsupported edge devices or MSP tools offer the same control-plane outcome. Defenders should therefore avoid single-technique threat modeling. The attacker objective is durable privileged control; the technique is interchangeable.

The victimology of BYOVD risk is also shaped by procurement and asset lifecycle. Organizations with decentralized IT, numerous subsidiaries, merged networks, medical or industrial equipment, and long-lived Windows images are more likely to contain outdated drivers. Education, healthcare, local government, and manufacturing often fit that pattern. Global enterprises with strong central security teams may still be exposed where regional business units or acquired entities retain older vendor packages. MSP-managed environments introduce another layer: if the provider’s standard endpoint image or remote-support stack includes weak controls, many downstream clients inherit the same exposure.

From a metrics perspective, defenders should not measure this risk only by the count of vulnerable drivers. They should measure the count of vulnerable drivers on **privileged systems**, the number of endpoints where local administrators can install drivers, the percentage of high-value servers with enforced driver block policies, the mean time to remove or block newly identified vulnerable drivers, and the correlation between driver events and privileged administrative activity. These metrics map more directly to ransomware resilience than generic vulnerability counts.

| Ransomware-Relevant Metric | Why It Is More Useful Than Raw Driver Counts | Example Defensive Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Vulnerable drivers on Tier 0 / privileged systems | Prioritizes systems where compromise affects identity or recovery | A single weak driver on a domain controller is more consequential than many on isolated kiosks |
| Driver-install events by privileged accounts | Detects suspicious administrative behavior | Unexpected driver activity during off-hours may signal post-compromise tampering |
| EDR tamper events near backup access | Links evasion to resilience targeting | Security degradation followed by backup-console access should trigger emergency review |
| Blocklist enforcement coverage | Measures whether policy is actually active | Inconsistent enforcement creates attacker choice points |
| Legacy-driver exception inventory | Identifies business units resisting remediation | Exceptions should have compensating controls and expiration dates |

The operational lesson for 2025–early 2026 ransomware defense is that BYOVD belongs in the same risk conversation as backup immutability and privileged access management. If attackers can blind the systems meant to detect backup deletion or identity abuse, then recovery controls may fail silently. Driver governance is therefore a resilience function, not simply an endpoint-hardening task.

### Zero-Day Patch Compression Across Windows Local EoP, Secure Boot, and Edge Infrastructure

While earlier reporting discussed edge exposure and initial-access scale, this section focuses on the timing problem created when zero-days, local privilege-escalation flaws, and edge-device vulnerabilities are exploited before normal enterprise patch cycles can respond. Ransomware defenders face a **patch-compression environment**: the time between disclosure, exploitation, patch availability, testing, deployment, and adversary weaponization is too short for traditional monthly governance when the affected technology is central to identity, endpoint visibility, or network access.

The May 2025 Microsoft patch cycle is a useful case study because it included multiple actively exploited zero-days relevant to ransomware tradecraft. CrowdStrike reported that CVE-2025-32706 and CVE-2025-32701 affected the Windows Common Log File System and allowed elevation of privilege, with Microsoft confirming active exploitation in the wild for one and CrowdStrike Counter Adversary Operations discovering another in late April 2025 before Microsoft patched it in May ([2](https://www.crowdstrike.com/en-us/blog/patch-tuesday-analysis-may-2025/)). Help Net Security separately reported that CVE-2025-32709 in the Windows Ancillary Function Driver for WinSock and CVE-2025-30400 in Microsoft DWM Core Library had also been exploited to escalate privileges on vulnerable machines, with Trend Micro’s Zero Day Initiative warning that such bug classes have historically been used by ransomware groups ([3](https://www.helpnetsecurity.com/2025/05/13/patch-tuesday-microsoft-fixes-5-actively-exploited-zero-days/)).

These are not merely routine endpoint vulnerabilities. Common Log File System, networking-adjacent drivers, and desktop/windowing components represent operating-system surfaces that can be present across large Windows fleets. Once a ransomware affiliate has local code execution through a phish, stolen credential, remote access tool, exposed application, or appliance pivot, a local EoP zero-day can enable rapid privilege gain. That can shorten the time defenders have to detect abnormal user behavior before the attacker becomes a privileged actor.

April 2025 reporting adds another ransomware-relevant example. Microsoft patched 121 vulnerabilities, and the actively exploited CVE-2025-29824 was reportedly executed by PipeMagic malware that had been observed in a RansomExx ransomware campaign in 2022 and later in attacks against organizations in Saudi Arabia, with more recent prevention activity affecting organizations in Brazil and Saudi Arabia ([6](https://cubexgroup.com/industry-articles/)). This is analytically important for two reasons. First, tooling associated with ransomware campaigns can persist across years rather than disappearing with one group brand. Second, geographic targeting can vary by campaign phase: Middle Eastern and Latin American organizations can be affected by the same malware lineage seen earlier in other ransomware contexts.

The following table maps selected 2025–early 2026 vulnerability themes to ransomware-relevant defensive pressure:

| Timeframe / Source Reporting | Vulnerability or Theme | Ransomware-Relevant Role | Defensive Pressure Created |
|---|---|---|---|
| April 2025 | CVE-2025-29824 associated with PipeMagic activity and prior RansomExx campaign context | Post-access exploitation / malware-enabled escalation | Patch quickly and hunt for historical tooling reuse |
| May 2025 | CVE-2025-32706 and CVE-2025-32701 in Windows Common Log File System | Local privilege escalation on Windows systems | Treat exploited local EoP as urgent even if not internet-facing |
| May 2025 | CVE-2025-32709 in Windows Ancillary Function Driver for WinSock | Privilege escalation after local foothold | Prioritize systems exposed to remote access, admin use, and server roles |
| May 2025 | CVE-2025-30400 in Microsoft DWM Core Library | Privilege escalation through widely deployed OS component | Include workstations and admin endpoints in emergency patch scope |
| Late 2025 reporting | CVE-2025-62454 in Cloud Files Mini Filter Driver noted as likely exploitation risk | Privilege escalation in file-system-adjacent component | Watch for EoP paths affecting storage and sync surfaces |
| Early 2026 reporting | CVE-2026-20856 in WSUS highlighted for priority patching | Remote code execution in update infrastructure | Protect software distribution and patch-management trust |
| 2026 reporting | CVE-2026-21265 Secure Boot security-feature bypass concern | Boot-chain and platform trust weakening | Maintain boot integrity for reliable recovery and forensic confidence |

SecurityWeek’s December 2025 reporting that Microsoft patched a Cloud Files Mini Filter Driver elevation-of-privilege flaw, CVE-2025-62454, and assessed it as likely to be exploited, reinforces the defensive priority of file-system and storage-adjacent drivers ([7](https://www.securityweek.com/microsoft-patches-57-vulnerabilities-three-zero-days/)). For ransomware defenders, file-system components are relevant because ransomware operations ultimately manipulate large volumes of files, shadow copies, synchronization clients, and backup-connected storage. Even when a specific vulnerability is not publicly tied to ransomware, the affected component class should influence prioritization.

The January 2026 reporting in the provided source set also highlights Windows Server Update Services, SMB servers, Win32k, management services, Virtualization-Based Security, Windows Hello, and other Windows components as requiring attention, with CVE-2026-20856 in WSUS singled out for priority because of remote code execution risk ([8](https://cyberpress.org/microsoft-fixes-114-vulnerabilities/)). The ransomware relevance of WSUS and management services is substantial: update infrastructure is a trusted distribution layer. If attackers can compromise or abuse patch-management infrastructure, they may gain a pathway to many systems that defenders normally treat as trusted. Even without detailed offensive mechanics, the strategic risk is clear: software distribution infrastructure can become an enterprise amplifier.

This creates a difficult governance problem. Traditional vulnerability management often ranks remote code execution above local privilege escalation. That hierarchy is reasonable for preventing first access, but it can be misleading for ransomware response. In an active intrusion, local privilege-escalation vulnerabilities on administrator workstations, jump boxes, domain controllers, backup servers, and virtualization managers may be more urgent than remote vulnerabilities on less critical systems. Risk scoring must therefore incorporate **intrusion phase relevance**.

A ransomware-aware patch-prioritization model should consider at least five variables:

| Variable | Ransomware-Specific Question | Example Implication |
|---|---|---|
| Exploitation status | Is the vulnerability actively exploited or in CISA KEV? | Emergency action and threat hunting are justified |
| Asset role | Does the affected system administer identity, backups, updates, virtualization, or EDR? | Patch before ordinary workstations or low-value servers |
| Attack phase utility | Does the flaw enable EoP, defense evasion, remote code execution, or credential access? | Local EoP may be urgent after any suspected access |
| Exposure concentration | Is the affected product deployed across many subsidiaries or clients? | MSPs and large enterprises face multiplier risk |
| Recovery dependency | Would compromise impede restoration or forensic confidence? | Backup, logging, and update infrastructure need stricter SLAs |

This model also explains why unsupported edge devices are now a board-level risk rather than a routine network-maintenance issue. CISA’s 2026 binding operational directive requires U.S. federal civilian agencies to remove unsupported edge devices over a defined timeline, and public reporting emphasizes that unsupported edge technologies do not receive timely security updates, are often boundary-facing, and can provide adversaries a beachhead into government systems ([9](https://www.helpnetsecurity.com/2026/02/06/cisa-orders-us-federal-agencies-to-replace-unsupported-edge-devices/)). Although the directive binds federal agencies, CISA, FBI, and the U.K. National Cyber Security Centre positioned the advice as broadly relevant to other organizations ([1](https://www.cybersecuritydive.com/news/cisa-edge-devices-binding-operational-directive/811539/)).

The patch-compression problem is worse for edge devices than for ordinary endpoints for several reasons. First, edge devices often have weaker telemetry than workstations and servers. Second, many organizations do not deploy EDR on network appliances. Third, firmware updates may require outage planning, vendor coordination, or high-availability failover testing. Fourth, edge devices often mediate remote access, authentication flows, and network segmentation. Fifth, successful edge compromise may remain undetected long enough for attackers to conduct credential theft or internal reconnaissance. CyberScoop’s reporting on government warnings over Cisco zero-day attacks noted that campaigns targeting Cisco edge technology had been underway for extended periods before detection and that CISA issued emergency directives after global exploitation was identified ([10](https://cyberscoop.com/cisco-zero-days-cisa-emergency-directive-five-eyes/)).

Ransomware operators benefit from the interaction between edge compromise and local privilege escalation. An edge exploit may provide entry without triggering endpoint alerts. Once inside, attackers can use stolen credentials, administrative protocols, local EoP, or vulnerable-driver abuse to expand control. By the time a patch is applied to the edge device, the intrusion may already have moved inward. This is why patching must be paired with compromise assessment. A patched firewall does not remove compromised credentials, persistence, internal implants, or altered identity policies.

The compressed patch window also affects geographic victimology. Countries with strong disclosure and advisories may patch faster once alerts are issued, but large federated organizations still struggle with asset discovery and change control. Regions with smaller security teams, less mature vulnerability management, or greater dependence on unsupported hardware may experience longer exposure. However, ransomware targeting is not limited to those regions; attackers prioritize monetizable disruption. U.S. and Western European organizations remain prominent because of payment capacity and public reporting, while organizations in the Middle East, Latin America, and Asia-Pacific face the same technology exposure with different reporting visibility. The PipeMagic activity noted in Brazil and Saudi Arabia is an example of geographically distributed exposure outside the most commonly cited U.S./Western Europe ransomware narrative ([6](https://cubexgroup.com/industry-articles/)).

Compressed patch windows also increase dependence on compensating controls. When a patch is unavailable, delayed, or operationally risky, defenders need independent logging, network isolation, privileged-session monitoring, configuration backup, known-good firmware images, and rapid credential rotation procedures. For Windows local EoP flaws, compensating controls include restricting local administrator rights, hardening privileged access workstations, enforcing EDR tamper protection, monitoring exploitation indicators where available, and prioritizing patching on systems that can administer recovery infrastructure. For Secure Boot and certificate-transition issues, compensating controls include asset validation, firmware and boot-policy inventory, and ensuring systems remain eligible for future security updates.

The most important analytical shift is that ransomware patching cannot be managed only by CVSS. Many of the most ransomware-relevant vulnerabilities are “important” rather than “critical” because they require local access. But ransomware affiliates often already have local access through other means. Once that assumption changes, the risk of local EoP increases sharply. A CVSS 7.8 exploited local privilege-escalation flaw on a backup server, privileged admin workstation, or domain controller may be more urgent than a higher-scoring vulnerability on an isolated system.

### Victimology Through the Lens of Privilege Escalation, Evasion, and Recovery Suppression

While prior demographic sections examined sector and geography at a broader level, this section focuses on why the same industries become more vulnerable when privilege escalation and defense evasion intersect with recovery dependencies. The emphasis is not merely “which sectors are attacked,” but **which sectors suffer disproportionate impact when attackers rapidly obtain privileged control and degrade visibility**.

FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center reporting for 2024 documented ransomware complaints across critical infrastructure sectors, with particularly high counts in healthcare and public health, critical manufacturing, government facilities, information technology, and financial services ([4](https://www.ic3.gov/AnnualReport/Reports/2024_IC3Report.pdf)). Those sectors map closely to the privilege-escalation problem because they combine high availability requirements with complex administrative environments. Hospitals need identity, imaging, pharmacy, scheduling, and clinical systems available continuously. Manufacturers rely on production scheduling, engineering systems, enterprise resource planning, and OT-adjacent IT. Government facilities provide citizen services with constrained budgets and legacy systems. IT providers and MSPs hold downstream access. Financial services firms face regulatory consequences and customer trust impacts.

## Conclusion

Ransomware activity in 2025 and early 2026 remained at historically elevated levels despite law-enforcement disruption, brand churn, and fragmentation of the RaaS ecosystem. The main finding is that ransomware has evolved from malware-centric encryption into a scalable extortion economy built around access markets, data theft, recovery denial, and coercive pressure. Victimology remained concentrated in sectors where downtime and data exposure create immediate leverage—especially manufacturing, technology and digital infrastructure, healthcare, retail/wholesale, professional services, and public administration. Geographic concentration remained strongest in the United States and Western developed economies, while EU reporting showed prominent impacts in manufacturing, digital infrastructure, and business services, with country-level clusters shaped by local sector composition and shared technology exposure ([GuidePoint Security, 2026](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf); [ENISA, 2026](https://www.enisa.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2026-01/ENISA%20Threat%20Landscape%202025_v1.2.pdf); [Check Point Research, 2026](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)).

The most important strategic development is deliberate targeting of resilience infrastructure. Attackers increasingly seek to compromise identity systems, backup platforms, hypervisors, disaster recovery environments, remote management tools, and other control planes because these assets determine whether victims can restore operations without paying. Identity compromise is especially consequential because it undermines trust in administrative accounts, authentication, cloud access, and recovery workflows; backup compromise removes the victim’s strongest alternative to ransom payment. This resilience-denial model explains why ransomware groups prioritize “control over recovery” as much as encryption itself, and why sectors with complex identity estates, legacy systems, high uptime requirements, or downstream dependencies face disproportionate impact ([Semperis, 2026](https://www.semperis.com/blog/fighting-ransomware-in-2026-what-cisos-need-to-know/); [CISA, n.d.](https://www.cisa.gov/stopransomware/ransomware-guide); [CYFIRMA, 2026](https://www.cyfirma.com/research/tracking-ransomware-jan-2026/)).

The defensive implication is that organizations must treat ransomware resilience as an enterprise dependency problem, not only an endpoint or backup problem. Priority next steps include reducing exposure of edge devices and management interfaces, urgently remediating known-exploited vulnerabilities, isolating identity and backup administration, enforcing phishing-resistant MFA, testing immutable and offline recovery under identity-compromise assumptions, hardening MSP and supplier access, and monitoring for abuse of vulnerable drivers, remote management tools, and enterprise software control planes. Because ransomware operators increasingly exploit shared ICT dependencies to build victim pools at scale, boards and security leaders should measure resilience by whether critical services can be restored from trusted identity, clean infrastructure, and verified backups—not merely by whether security tools are deployed ([CISA, n.d.](https://www.cisa.gov/known-exploited-vulnerabilities-catalog); [Verizon, 2025](https://www.verizon.com/business/resources/reports/dbir/); [NCSC, n.d.](https://www.ncsc.gov.uk/guidance/mitigating-malware-and-ransomware-attacks)).
