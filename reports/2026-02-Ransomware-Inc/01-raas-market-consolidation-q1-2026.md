# Ransomware-as-a-Service in Early 2026: Market Consolidation, Platformization, and Criminal Business Models

By May 2026, the Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS) economy has entered a new consolidation phase: fewer groups are producing a larger share of global victim volume, reversing much of the fragmentation that characterized 2024–2025. In Q1 2026, Check Point Research tracked **2,122 organizations listed across 70+ ransomware data-leak sites**, making it the **second-highest Q1 on record**; critically, the **top 10 groups accounted for 71% of all listed victims**, while **Qilin, Akira, The Gentlemen, and LockBit together represented 41%** of claims. Qilin remained the most active operation for the third consecutive quarter with **338 posted victims**, while LockBit’s relaunched **LockBit 5.0** posted **163 victims**, confirming its return to the top tier after earlier law-enforcement disruption ([Check Point Research, 2026](https://blog.checkpoint.com/research/q1-2026-ransomware-report-fewer-groups-higher-impact/)).

This consolidation reflects a broader cybercrime market dynamic: disruption does not eliminate ransomware capacity so much as redistribute affiliates, tooling, access, and brand equity toward surviving platforms. The collapse or dormancy of major brands such as RansomHub and the post-Operation Cronos disruption of LockBit created affiliate migration waves that benefited better-organized operators including **Qilin, Akira, DragonForce, and the revived LockBit ecosystem**. Broadcom/Symantec assessed that in 2025 former RansomHub affiliates rapidly migrated to other groups, while Akira and Qilin each accounted for approximately **16% of claimed ransomware attacks** that year, demonstrating how displaced labor and access supply can accelerate market share gains for dominant RaaS platforms ([Broadcom/Symantec, 2026](https://sed-cms.broadcom.com/sites/default/files/2026-01/RWN-2026-WP100_1.pdf)).

The resulting macro-economic structure increasingly resembles a platform economy rather than a loose collection of independent crews. Core operators provide branding, malware development, leak-site infrastructure, negotiation workflows, payment handling, and affiliate governance, while affiliates supply intrusion capability, initial access, victim selection, and operational execution. LockBit 5.0 illustrates this platformization: its September 2025 relaunch reportedly added multi-platform targeting across Windows, Linux, and ESXi, new evasion features, faster encryption routines, randomized file extensions, and an affiliate onboarding requirement involving an approximately **$500 Bitcoin deposit**—a small but notable barrier designed to filter participants and formalize recruitment ([Check Point Research, 2026](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)).

At the same time, the RaaS model is lowering barriers to entry through “white-label” ransomware arrangements. In this model, less technical or non-technical criminals can operate under their own brand while relying on a larger cartel or platform for infrastructure, payloads, negotiation support, and operational services. DragonForce is the clearest reported example: AhnLab describes its 2025 cartel formation and white-label model as offering affiliates **80% profit share**, while allowing them to use DragonForce infrastructure under separate brands; AhnLab also notes that RansomHub had previously offered affiliates up to **90% profit share** before its abrupt shutdown, intensifying affiliate competition across the ecosystem ([AhnLab, 2026](https://download.ahnlab.com/global/brochure/2025_Threat_Analysis_2026_Predictions_en.pdf)).

This affiliate-driven economy has begun to show corporate-style partnership behavior, including cartel rhetoric, non-aggression proposals, shared infrastructure models, and structured recruitment. Open-source reporting on DragonForce’s attempted coalition with LockBit and Qilin described explicit efforts to create “equal competition conditions,” avoid public conflict, and “dictate market conditions,” suggesting that leading RaaS operators are experimenting with coordination mechanisms more commonly associated with cartels than with ad hoc criminal gangs ([Secure Blink, 2026](https://www.secureblink.com/threat-research/2026-ransomware-cartelization-qilin-lock-bit-and-akira-convergence)). These developments do not amount to a conventional merger, but they do indicate a maturing illicit marketplace in which brand reputation, affiliate retention, tooling quality, payment terms, and resilience to takedowns are central competitive variables.

Victim-volume data should be interpreted as a high-confidence proxy for public extortion activity, not a complete measure of total intrusions or profitability. Brandefense reported **2,135 confirmed ransomware incidents**, **68 active groups**, activity across **98 countries**, and **50% quarter-over-quarter growth** in Q1 2026, broadly aligning with Check Point’s assessment that ransomware is scaling despite fewer dominant actors ([Brandefense, 2026](https://brandefense.io/reports/ransomware-trends-report-q1-2026/)). However, GuidePoint Security cautions that high victim counts do not necessarily equal high revenue: Qilin’s large affiliate base and high operational tempo may produce many posted victims, but payment rates and negotiation outcomes can lag behind more selective or tightly controlled operations ([GuidePoint Security, 2026](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)).

In early 2026, therefore, the RaaS economy is best understood as a consolidating criminal services market: disruption removes brands but rarely removes capacity; affiliates behave like mobile labor; initial-access markets feed ransomware demand; and dominant operators compete through platform reliability, profit-sharing, brand credibility, and partner ecosystems. The strategic implication for defenders is that the threat is becoming less fragmented but more industrialized—concentrated in fewer, more resilient, better-structured operators capable of absorbing talent, scaling victim volume, and offering ransomware capabilities as a managed criminal service.

## Q1 2026 RaaS Market Structure: Victim Volumes, Active Groups, and Evidence of Reconsolidation

### 1. Concentration Metrics: Q1 2026 Victim Distribution Shows a Top-Heavy RaaS Market

The most important market-structure signal in Q1 2026 is not simply the absolute number of ransomware victims, but the changing distribution of those victims across ransomware brands. Check Point Research reported **2,122 organizations listed on ransomware data-leak sites in Q1 2026**, making it the **second-highest first quarter on record** and indicating that ransomware activity remained at a historically elevated baseline even after major law-enforcement disruptions and brand collapses in prior periods ([1](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/), [2](https://cybersecurityasia.net/check-point-q1-2026-ransomware-report-attack/)).  

The same Q1 2026 dataset indicates a clear reconsolidation pattern: the **top 10 ransomware groups accounted for approximately 71%–71.1% of all listed victims**, compared with a previously fragmented environment in which the top 10’s share had fallen to **57%** when active groups peaked in Q3 2025 ([1](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/), [3](https://industrialcyber.co/ransomware/ransomware-sector-reconsolidating-as-qilin-lockbit-and-the-gentlemen-expand-influence-in-q1-2026/)). This is a material concentration shift because ransomware markets historically alternate between fragmentation after disruptions and consolidation around surviving operators that can absorb displaced affiliates, maintain stable infrastructure, and preserve victim-facing extortion capacity.

|---|---:|---:|---|---|
| Published ransomware victims | Q4 2025: 2,287 victims in GuidePoint GRIT dataset | Q1 2026: 2,122 victims in Check Point dataset | Volume remained near record levels despite changes in group composition | High for cited datasets; cross-vendor comparability requires caution |
| Active ransomware groups | Q3 2025 peak: 85 active groups | Q1 2026: 71 active groups | Fewer active brands, indicating reduced ecosystem fragmentation | High for cited Check Point/Industrial Cyber reporting |

| Qilin individual volume | Qilin was already the leading 2025 RaaS operation in multiple reports | Q1 2026: 338 victims | Qilin alone exceeded the combined output of the bottom 50 groups | High |

Using the reported Q1 2026 total of **2,122 victims**, the top 10’s **71.1%** share implies roughly **1,509 victims** attributed to the ten largest groups, leaving approximately **613 victims** distributed among all remaining groups. Because Qilin alone posted **338 victims**, Check Point’s statement that Qilin outpaced the combined output of the bottom 50 groups is economically important: it means a single RaaS platform generated more visible victim volume than most of the long tail combined ([1](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/), [3](https://industrialcyber.co/ransomware/ransomware-sector-reconsolidating-as-qilin-lockbit-and-the-gentlemen-expand-influence-in-q1-2026/)).  

This is a classic concentration signature. In a fragmented market, many operators each produce modest victim counts. In a reconsolidating market, a handful of platforms capture the majority of affiliate labor, brand recognition, leak-site traffic, negotiation infrastructure, and criminal-market attention. Q1 2026 shows the latter condition. The fact that **21 new groups emerged** during the period but most posted **fewer than 10 victims** demonstrates that new-brand creation did not offset the loss or weakening of mid-tier operators ([3](https://industrialcyber.co/ransomware/ransomware-sector-reconsolidating-as-qilin-lockbit-and-the-gentlemen-expand-influence-in-q1-2026/)).  

A derived concentration estimate further illustrates the imbalance. If Qilin, Akira, The Gentlemen, and LockBit collectively represented **41%** of Q1 2026 victims, then those four operators accounted for approximately **870 victims** out of 2,122. Given cited Q1 2026 counts of **Qilin: 338**, **The Gentlemen: 166**, and **LockBit: 163**, Akira’s implied Q1 2026 contribution would be approximately **203 victims**, assuming the same total victim base and rounded percentages. This Akira estimate should be treated as a **derived approximation**, not a directly reported figure, because the provided reporting gives Akira’s combined top-four share but not a standalone Q1 2026 victim count ([1](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/), [2](https://cybersecurityasia.net/check-point-q1-2026-ransomware-report-attack/)).

|---|---:|---|---|

| The Gentlemen | 166 victims | Directly reported | Rapidly scaled from 40 victims in Q4 2025 to 166 in Q1 2026 |

The market implication is that Q1 2026 ransomware activity cannot be understood as a flat landscape of interchangeable groups. It is more accurately described as a **platform economy** in which dominant RaaS brands function as criminal service providers to affiliates. These brands offer leak infrastructure, negotiation channels, administrative panels, brand credibility, operational support, and payment-sharing arrangements. Victim volume concentrates where affiliates believe they can maximize conversion, reduce operational friction, and rely on stable extortion infrastructure.  

This concentration also changes the risk profile for defenders. In a fragmented market, many small groups may produce inconsistent tradecraft and irregular extortion behavior. In a reconsolidated market, leading operators can normalize procedures across affiliates, maintain more resilient infrastructure, and exert stronger reputational pressure on victims. However, concentration does not necessarily mean every affiliate is sophisticated. Qilin’s 2025 and 2026 activity illustrates this tension: a high-volume RaaS platform may attract both capable intrusion teams and lower-skill affiliates, producing high victim counts but uneven negotiation quality and uncertain payment conversion ([4](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)).  

The dataset must also be interpreted carefully. Leak-site victim counts are not equivalent to confirmed intrusions, successful encryption events, or paid ransoms. They measure organizations publicly named by extortion groups and therefore reflect a mix of actual compromises, failed negotiations, duplicate claims, delayed postings, and potential exaggeration. Nevertheless, leak-site data remains one of the most useful comparative indicators for market-share analysis because ransomware groups use public naming as part of the extortion process. The Q1 2026 concentration shift is therefore analytically meaningful even if exact incident counts differ across vendors.

### 2. From Fragmentation to Reconsolidation: Affiliate Labor Reallocation After Disruption

The Q1 2026 RaaS structure reflects the aftereffects of earlier disruption cycles. Ransomware ecosystems have repeatedly shown a pattern in which enforcement actions, infrastructure seizures, internal exits, or brand collapses temporarily scatter affiliates. Those affiliates then migrate toward surviving operators with usable tooling, stable leak sites, working negotiation infrastructure, and credible reputations. Check Point explicitly describes this as a recurring market pattern: law-enforcement actions disrupt the ransomware market, affiliates scatter, and surviving groups that avoid disruption absorb displaced talent and grow ([1](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)).  

The 2025–early 2026 cycle appears to have followed this model. GuidePoint’s GRIT reporting observed that **124 distinct ransomware groups were active in 2025**, the highest number it had recorded and a **46% year-over-year increase**, while ransomware victim numbers rose **58% year over year** ([5](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/resources/grit-2026-ransomware-and-cyber-threat-report/), [6](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/ransomware-victims-threat-groups-surge-110800052.html)). This indicates that 2025 was not merely a year of dominant-brand stability; it was a period of high turnover, experimentation, and fragmentation. By Q1 2026, however, the market had partially reversed: active groups fell from the Q3 2025 peak of **85** to **71**, and the top 10’s victim share rose to **71.1%** ([3](https://industrialcyber.co/ransomware/ransomware-sector-reconsolidating-as-qilin-lockbit-and-the-gentlemen-expand-influence-in-q1-2026/)).  

The economic mechanism behind this reconsolidation is affiliate labor reallocation. Affiliates are the operational workforce of the RaaS economy: they conduct intrusions, select victims, exfiltrate data, deploy ransomware where applicable, and interact with the RaaS operator’s infrastructure. Operators provide the platform layer. When a platform disappears or becomes less trusted, affiliates must find a replacement quickly or lose monetization opportunities. This creates abrupt migration waves, especially when a major brand collapses.  

RansomHub’s abrupt disappearance in April 2025 is a key example. Multiple sources describe its collapse as a major structural shift that left many affiliates without a stable RaaS brand. Check Point’s 2026 security report notes that RansomHub had posted more than **760 victims** since appearing in 2024 before going offline without warning in early April 2025, after which Qilin and DragonForce moved quickly to recruit former RansomHub and LockBit affiliates on criminal forums ([7](https://assets.brilyant.com/BigSizeFile/IND+-+CCPA+2026+Cyber+Security+Report.pdf)). AhnLab similarly describes RansomHub’s collapse as triggering a major reshuffle, with affiliates migrating to Qilin, DragonForce, and Lynx, and characterizes it as the largest structural shift since the disruption of LockBit after Operation Cronos in 2024 ([8](https://download.ahnlab.com/global/brochure/2025_Threat_Analysis_2026_Predictions_en.pdf)).  

| Disruption or Structural Event | Reported Effect | Market-Structure Consequence | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Operation Cronos against LockBit in 2024 | LockBit’s prior dominance was disrupted; affiliates and infrastructure were affected | Created space for rivals and later set conditions for LockBit 5.0 comeback | High for disruption; medium for exact affiliate redistribution |
| RansomHub collapse in April 2025 | RansomHub went offline after having accumulated more than 760 victims since 2024 | Affiliates sought replacement platforms, benefiting Qilin, DragonForce, Lynx, and others | High |
| Record 2025 group count | 124 active ransomware groups in GuidePoint’s 2025 dataset | Fragmented environment with many brands competing for affiliates | High for dataset |
| Q1 2026 active-group decline | Active groups fell from 85 to 71 compared with Q3 2025 peak | Fragmentation declined; top platforms absorbed more volume | High |
| Top 10 share increase | Top 10 share rose from 57% to 71.1% | Market power shifted toward leading RaaS operators | High |

The affiliate marketplace behaves like a criminal labor market with switching costs. Affiliates will tolerate a degree of instability if a platform offers high profit shares, strong infrastructure, and a credible leak site. However, if an operator becomes unreliable, disappears, or is degraded by enforcement, affiliates can switch brands. This produces a paradox: law-enforcement disruption can weaken specific brands while also creating short-term volatility that benefits surviving operators. The Q1 2026 reconsolidation suggests that groups such as Qilin, Akira, The Gentlemen, and LockBit were able to absorb enough displaced capacity to increase their relative market share.  

Qilin appears to have been the primary beneficiary of this reallocation. Check Point’s 2026 cybersecurity report states that Qilin was well positioned to exploit the temporary vacuum created by the disappearance of major rivals such as RansomHub and the inactivity of LockBit. It reports that Qilin’s monthly victim counts nearly tripled during 2025, increasing from roughly **35 victims per month in Q1** to more than **150 per month in Q4**, and that the group published more than **1,000 victims** on its data-leak site after they refused to pay ([7](https://assets.brilyant.com/BigSizeFile/IND+-+CCPA+2026+Cyber+Security+Report.pdf)). GuidePoint also reported that Qilin’s 2025 activity levels were the highest it had ever observed for any ransomware group, surpassing even LockBit at its peak in its dataset ([5](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/resources/grit-2026-ransomware-and-cyber-threat-report/), [6](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/ransomware-victims-threat-groups-surge-110800052.html)).  

There are, however, dataset differences that should not be ignored. AhnLab’s 2025 reporting ranked Akira first with **622 incidents** and Qilin second with **589 incidents**, while other reports describe Qilin as the leading or most prolific 2025 RaaS operation ([8](https://download.ahnlab.com/global/brochure/2025_Threat_Analysis_2026_Predictions_en.pdf), [5](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/resources/grit-2026-ransomware-and-cyber-threat-report/)). This apparent discrepancy likely reflects differences in counting methodology, collection period, data-leak site monitoring, duplicate handling, and definitions of “incident,” “victim,” or “published victim.” For market-structure analysis, the consistent signal across sources is more important than exact ranking: both Qilin and Akira were among the dominant ransomware operations entering 2026, and Qilin was a central beneficiary of affiliate migration after RansomHub’s disappearance.  

The Gentlemen’s Q1 2026 surge is also important because it shows that reconsolidation does not only benefit long-standing brands. The group increased from **40 victims in Q4 2025** to **166 victims in Q1 2026**, a more than fourfold increase in one quarter ([2](https://cybersecurityasia.net/check-point-q1-2026-ransomware-report-attack/)). This suggests either rapid affiliate acquisition, unusually effective victim-posting tempo, or both. The available sources do not provide enough evidence to determine whether The Gentlemen’s growth came from a centralized operator scaling its own activity, a sudden influx of affiliates, or a change in posting strategy. The result, however, is clear: the group joined Qilin, Akira, and LockBit as part of the Q1 2026 high-volume cluster.  

LockBit’s re-entry into the top tier is a different kind of reconsolidation signal. Unlike Qilin, which benefited from a growth phase, LockBit was recovering from a major disruption. Its **163 Q1 2026 victims** indicate that brand destruction was incomplete. A ransomware brand can survive disruption if enough affiliates still view it as commercially viable, if its operator can relaunch infrastructure, and if the brand retains intimidation value in negotiations. AhnLab reported that LockBit 5.0 relaunched with a cross-platform strategy affecting Windows, Linux, and VMware ESXi environments, while Check Point-linked Q1 2026 reporting indicates LockBit had confirmed its comeback by re-entering the global top tier ([8](https://download.ahnlab.com/global/brochure/2025_Threat_Analysis_2026_Predictions_en.pdf), [2](https://cybersecurityasia.net/check-point-q1-2026-ransomware-report-attack/)).  

Reconsolidation should therefore be understood as **market power concentrating around platforms**, not necessarily as a reduction in the total number of criminals. The number of brands fell, but affiliate labor did not disappear. Instead, the operational capacity moved toward operators that could provide the most attractive combination of revenue share, infrastructure, reputation, and continuity. That distinction matters because disruption of a brand does not automatically remove the underlying intrusion capacity from the ecosystem. It may simply change which RaaS platform monetizes that capacity next.

### 3. Dominant Operators as Criminal Platforms: Qilin, Akira, LockBit 5.0, and The Gentlemen

The leading Q1 2026 operators function less like single crews and more like platform businesses. Their value proposition to affiliates includes access to ransomware payloads, data-leak publication channels, negotiation systems, communications infrastructure, reputational pressure, and sometimes additional services that support extortion. This platform model is central to the macro-economics of RaaS because it separates the operator’s role from the affiliate’s role and allows scale without requiring the core group to conduct every intrusion directly.  

Qilin is the clearest example of this platform structure. GuidePoint describes Qilin as a RaaS operator that openly recruits potential affiliates on dark-web forums, licenses ransomware to affiliates, and retains a percentage of ransom payments in exchange for maintaining the software and providing access to leak-site and chat infrastructure ([4](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)). GuidePoint reports Qilin’s affiliate terms as **80% to affiliates for payments of USD 3 million or less** and **85% to affiliates for payments above USD 3 million**, meaning the core operator allegedly retains approximately **20%** or **15%**, respectively ([4](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)).  

| Operator | Q1 2026 or 2025 Market Signal | Platform Characteristics | Reported Affiliate Economics | Analytical Assessment |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Qilin | 338 Q1 2026 victims; leading operation for third consecutive quarter; more than 1,000 2025 published victims in one report | RaaS framework with affiliate panel, leak infrastructure, negotiation tooling, operational support | 80% affiliate share for payments ≤ USD 3M; 85% for payments > USD 3M | Most visible high-volume RaaS platform entering 2026 |
| Akira | Part of top-four group representing 41% of Q1 2026 victims; AhnLab ranked it first in 2025 with 622 incidents | Structured ransomware operation with sustained global activity | Specific Q1 2026 profit share not confirmed in provided sources | High-volume operator; exact monetization terms less transparent in cited data |
| LockBit 5.0 | 163 Q1 2026 victims; re-entered global top tier | Relaunched brand with cross-platform positioning and affiliate-driven model | Specific LockBit 5.0 share not confirmed in provided sources | Demonstrates brand resilience after enforcement disruption |
| The Gentlemen | 166 Q1 2026 victims, up from 40 in Q4 2025 | Rapidly scaling operation; platform maturity less documented in provided sources | Not confirmed in provided sources | Breakout operator; growth warrants monitoring |
| DragonForce | Not among the named Q1 top-four in the provided Q1 statistics, but central to white-label/cartel discussion | White-label RaaS model allowing affiliate branding over shared infrastructure | Reported 80% affiliate share | Important structural innovator even if not the largest Q1 2026 victim-volume leader |

Qilin’s affiliate economics show how RaaS operators compete for criminal labor. An **80%–85% affiliate payout** is economically aggressive and shifts most direct ransom revenue to the affiliate while allowing the operator to profit from scale. If a USD 2 million ransom were paid under the alleged Qilin terms, the affiliate would receive USD 1.6 million and the operator would retain USD 400,000. If a USD 5 million ransom were paid under the above-USD-3-million tier, the affiliate would receive USD 4.25 million and the operator would retain USD 750,000. These examples are arithmetic illustrations of reported percentage terms, not evidence that any particular victim paid those amounts.  

Qilin’s platform features also appear designed to improve affiliate conversion and retention. GuidePoint reported that Qilin added features to its panel during 2025, including spam-campaign capabilities and automated ransom-negotiation tooling, and introduced a “Call Lawyer” feature that claimed to provide legal counsel ([4](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)). Check Point’s 2026 report similarly describes Qilin’s RaaS framework as supporting the end-to-end attack lifecycle through an administrative panel that includes access to the encryptor, leak infrastructure, payment-negotiation tooling, and operational support ([7](https://assets.brilyant.com/BigSizeFile/IND+-+CCPA+2026+Cyber+Security+Report.pdf)). These descriptions should be interpreted as threat-intelligence observations of platform capabilities, not as procedural guidance.  

The availability of such features indicates that Qilin is not simply selling malware. It is selling a criminal business process. The operator’s platform reduces friction for affiliates by centralizing extortion infrastructure, standardizing communication channels, and providing brand-backed leverage. This helps explain why Qilin could scale quickly after other operators weakened or disappeared. Affiliates migrating from disrupted platforms need continuity; Qilin offered them a service stack rather than only a payload.  

However, Qilin’s high victim volume does not necessarily imply high payment conversion. GuidePoint cautions that Qilin’s high operational tempo should not be mistaken for profitability, noting that a substantial portion of Qilin victims reportedly do not comply with ransom demands and that the group may suffer from rigid communications and inflexible negotiations ([4](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf)). This distinction is critical. RaaS market share measured by leak-site postings captures extortion attempts and failed negotiations, not necessarily successful monetization. A group can be the market leader in published victims while having lower-than-expected revenue if many victims refuse to pay.  

Akira represents another form of structured dominance. While the provided Q1 2026 sources identify Akira as part of the top-four cluster accounting for **41%** of victims, they do not provide a direct Q1 standalone count. AhnLab’s 2025 reporting ranked Akira first with **622 incidents**, while Qilin ranked second with **589 incidents** in that dataset ([8](https://download.ahnlab.com/global/brochure/2025_Threat_Analysis_2026_Predictions_en.pdf)). Other sources placed Qilin ahead of Akira. The consistent interpretation is that Akira remained one of the most important RaaS or ransomware-extortion operators entering 2026, even if exact ranking differs by source.  

Akira’s importance lies partly in its durability. In fragmented ransomware markets, many brands appear briefly, post a handful of victims, and disappear. Akira maintained sufficient volume across 2025 to remain in the Q1 2026 leading cluster. That persistence suggests access to a stable affiliate base, repeatable monetization processes, and a recognizable extortion brand. The provided sources do not document Akira’s affiliate profit-sharing percentages, so any comparison with Qilin’s 80%–85% model should be limited to victim-volume and structural observations rather than specific revenue-split claims.  

LockBit 5.0 demonstrates the residual value of a ransomware brand after enforcement action. Operation Cronos significantly disrupted LockBit in 2024, and AhnLab reported that LockBit fell out of the top 30 in 2025 after having ranked first in 2024 ([8](https://download.ahnlab.com/global/brochure/2025_Threat_Analysis_2026_Predictions_en.pdf)). Yet by Q1 2026, LockBit had posted **163 victims** and re-entered the global top tier ([2](https://cybersecurityasia.net/check-point-q1-2026-ransomware-report-attack/)). This suggests that ransomware brand equity can survive public compromise, especially if affiliates believe the operator can restore infrastructure and if the brand remains psychologically effective in extortion.  

AhnLab reported that LockBit 5.0 strengthened its cross-platform positioning across Windows, Linux, and VMware ESXi environments and adopted advanced anti-analysis techniques; it also reported that LockBit 5.0 explicitly allowed affiliates to target critical infrastructure categories that had previously been avoided ([8](https://download.ahnlab.com/global/brochure/2025_Threat_Analysis_2026_Predictions_en.pdf)). From a market-structure perspective, the key issue is not the technical detail but the commercial signal: LockBit attempted to re-establish itself as a broad, affiliate-attractive platform with expanded targeting permissiveness and renewed operational relevance.  

The Gentlemen’s Q1 2026 position is the least documented but still significant. The group’s increase from **40 victims in Q4 2025** to **166 victims in Q1 2026** made it the breakout group of the quarter ([2](https://cybersecurityasia.net/check-point-q1-2026-ransomware-report-attack/)). Such a sharp increase can reflect several non-exclusive possibilities: rapid affiliate onboarding, acquisition of displaced affiliates, a strategic posting backlog, or successful targeting by a concentrated core team. The available sources do not provide enough evidence to determine which explanation is correct. Analytically, The Gentlemen’s rise reinforces the broader point that reconsolidation is dynamic: dominant clusters can include both established brands and newly scaled operators.  

The dominant-operator model also changes how ransomware competition works. RaaS platforms compete not only for victims but for affiliates. Profit-sharing percentages, infrastructure reliability, leak-site availability, negotiation support, and brand reputation become competitive variables. Affiliates can move between platforms, and operators must continuously demonstrate that their platform improves the affiliate’s probability of monetization. This explains why groups add features, advertise payout terms, court displaced affiliates, and form partnerships. The RaaS market in Q1 2026 therefore resembles a criminal platform economy with labor mobility, brand competition, and service differentiation.

### 4. White-Label Ransomware and Cartel-Style Partnerships: Lowering Barriers and Professionalizing Criminal Supply Chains

The white-label ransomware phenomenon is one of the most important structural developments in the RaaS economy because it allows affiliates or smaller criminal groups to operate under their own branding while relying on another operator’s infrastructure, payloads, negotiation systems, and support. In conventional RaaS, affiliates typically operate under the core group’s brand. In white-label RaaS, the infrastructure provider becomes more like a backend service vendor, enabling multiple outward-facing brands to use shared capabilities.  

DragonForce is the most clearly reported example in the provided material. AhnLab states that DragonForce introduced a **white-label model** that allows affiliates to use its infrastructure under their own brands, offering an **80% profit share** ([8](https://download.ahnlab.com/global/brochure/2025_Threat_Analysis_2026_Predictions_en.pdf)). Dark Reading, citing ReliaQuest reporting, similarly describes DragonForce as known for a white-label RaaS product in which affiliates can use the group’s infrastructure to push ransomware with the affiliate’s own branding ([9](https://www.darkreading.com/cyberattacks-data-breaches/extortion-gangs-join-forces-ransomware-cartel)).  

| RaaS Model | Brand Presented to Victim | Infrastructure Provider | Affiliate Skill Requirement | Economic Effect |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Traditional closed crew | Core group brand | Core group | Higher; group conducts more activity internally | Lower scale, greater operational control |
| Conventional RaaS | Core group brand | Core operator | Medium; affiliates conduct intrusions using operator platform | Scales through affiliate labor while preserving central brand |
| White-label RaaS | Affiliate’s own brand or custom label | Backend operator such as DragonForce | Lower than building a full ransomware operation from scratch | Enables rapid creation of new brands and expands criminal participation |
| Cartel-style coordination | Multiple brands remain visible | Shared or coordinated infrastructure and affiliate arrangements | Variable | Reduces destructive competition and may improve resilience |

The macro-economic significance of white-label ransomware is that it lowers entry barriers. A non-technical or semi-technical criminal actor may not be able to build ransomware, maintain leak sites, manage communications infrastructure, or create negotiation workflows. White-label RaaS allows such actors to outsource those backend functions while still appearing as an independent brand. This can inflate the number of visible ransomware names while concealing a smaller number of infrastructure providers. In other words, apparent fragmentation at the brand layer can coexist with consolidation at the service-provider layer.  

This distinction is crucial for interpreting Q1 2026. The market showed fewer active groups than the Q3 2025 peak, but the emergence of white-label models means group counts alone may understate consolidation. A visible “new group” with fewer than 10 victims may not represent a truly independent ransomware operation. It may be an affiliate brand using another operator’s backend. ## Dominant Operators and Competitive Positioning: Qilin, LockBit 5.0, Akira, The Gentlemen, and DragonForce

### 1. Competitive Positioning by Operator: Differentiation Beyond Raw Victim Counts

While the previous section quantified market concentration and named the dominant RaaS brands, this section examines **how those brands compete**: not merely by posting more victims, but by differentiating themselves through affiliate recruitment, perceived reliability, sector targeting, brand recovery, white-label infrastructure, and cartel-style signaling.

The early-2026 RaaS market is best understood as a **platform economy** rather than a collection of isolated malware crews. The leading operators compete for three scarce resources: capable affiliates, reusable access, and victim credibility in negotiations. Public data-leak-site volume remains the most visible metric, but it is not a perfect proxy for profitability. A high-volume operator may post many non-paying victims, while a lower-volume operator may produce higher revenue per intrusion if it targets sectors with high downtime costs or negotiates more effectively. GuidePoint explicitly cautions that Qilin’s high operational tempo should not automatically be read as profitability because a substantial portion of victims reportedly do not comply with ransom demands, and because Qilin’s negotiation posture has been described as rigid by some reporting [\[1\]](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf).

| Operator | Competitive posture in early 2026 | Confirmed / reported indicators | Analytical significance |
|---|---|---:|---|
| Qilin | Scale leader and affiliate magnet | 338 Q1 2026 victims; first place for third consecutive quarter in cited Q1 reporting [\[2\]](https://www.cybersecurity-insiders.com/ransomware-q1-2026-consolidation/) | Dominates visible output; benefits from post-RansomHub affiliate migration and mature RaaS infrastructure |
| Akira | Revenue-optimized, sector-selective operator | Reported total proceeds of approximately USD 244 million; strong focus on consumer goods and industrial manufacturing in cited reporting [\[3\]](https://industrialcyber.co/ransomware/ransomware-sector-reconsolidating-as-qilin-lockbit-and-the-gentlemen-expand-influence-in-q1-2026/) | May prioritize economic leverage and ransom conversion over maximum posting volume |
| LockBit 5.0 | Brand-recovery and credibility-restoration operation | 163 Q1 2026 victims; U.S. target share reportedly reduced to 21.2% from historical 50%+ [\[2\]](https://www.cybersecurity-insiders.com/ransomware-q1-2026-consolidation/) | Demonstrates partial post-disruption recovery, but must rebuild trust with affiliates after law-enforcement action |
| The Gentlemen | Rapid-scaling challenger brand | Increased from 40 victims in Q4 2025 to 166 in Q1 2026; reported use of a pre-staged pool of compromised perimeter devices [\[2\]](https://www.cybersecurity-insiders.com/ransomware-q1-2026-consolidation/) | Shows how stockpiled access can compress growth timelines for new entrants |
| DragonForce | Infrastructure franchisor and white-label innovator | Reported white-label model allowing affiliates to operate independent brands using DragonForce infrastructure [\[4\]](https://cxotoday.com/cybersecurity/ransomware-surges-as-weekly-attacks-hit-record-highs-in-april-2026/) | Less important only as a victim-volume leader; more important as a business-model innovator |

Qilin’s positioning is closest to that of a **dominant marketplace platform**. Its main advantage is not simply the ransomware payload, but the full operating environment: brand recognition, leak-site reach, affiliate onboarding, negotiation infrastructure, and an established reputation among criminal participants. Broadcom/Symantec reported that after RansomHub’s disappearance in 2025, former affiliates quickly migrated to other threats including Qilin, Akira, and DragonForce, indicating that RaaS market share can shift rapidly when affiliate labor is displaced [\[5\]](https://sed-cms.broadcom.com/sites/default/files/2026-01/RWN-2026-WP100_1.pdf). In economic terms, Qilin appears to have captured both **demand-side pressure**—victims recognizing the brand’s ability to leak data—and **supply-side confidence**—affiliates viewing the platform as active enough to monetize access.

Akira’s position is different. The available reporting emphasizes its deliberate targeting of sectors with high interruption costs, including consumer goods and industrial manufacturing, and attributes approximately USD 244 million in total proceeds to the operation [\[3\]](https://industrialcyber.co/ransomware/ransomware-sector-reconsolidating-as-qilin-lockbit-and-the-gentlemen-expand-influence-in-q1-2026/). This suggests a business model closer to **margin optimization** than pure volume maximization. In practice, such an operator may accept fewer public victims if the selected targets have stronger incentives to settle quickly because of production downtime, supply-chain impact, customer commitments, or regulated data exposure. This differs from a raw leak-site-volume model, where affiliates may post a broad range of victims, including organizations with little capacity or willingness to pay.

LockBit 5.0’s early-2026 posture is defined by **reputational reconstruction**. Operation Cronos in 2024 damaged LockBit’s infrastructure and underground credibility, and ZeroFox assessed in 2025 that LockBit had almost certainly struggled to attract and retain affiliates after that law-enforcement disruption [\[6\]](https://www.zerofox.com/intelligence/flash-report-rumored-new-coalition-of-ransomware-groups-yet-to-materialize). The group’s subsequent reappearance as LockBit 5.0 and its 163 Q1 2026 victims show that enforcement did not permanently erase the brand, but the operator’s challenge is different from Qilin’s: LockBit must prove reliability to affiliates who have alternatives. The reported reduction in U.S. targeting share to 21.2% also suggests repositioning, possibly to reduce enforcement pressure or diversify victim geography, though this should be treated as analytical inference rather than a confirmed motive [\[2\]](https://www.cybersecurity-insiders.com/ransomware-q1-2026-consolidation/).

The Gentlemen illustrate a separate competitive mechanism: **rapid entry through pre-positioned access**. Their growth from 40 Q4 2025 victims to 166 Q1 2026 victims is not merely a branding story; cited reporting attributes the surge to a pre-staged stockpile of roughly 14,700 compromised FortiGate devices [\[2\]](https://www.cybersecurity-insiders.com/ransomware-q1-2026-consolidation/). From a macro-economic perspective, this shows how the ransomware market can reward operators that control or broker access at scale. The Gentlemen’s rise indicates that a group does not need years of brand-building if it can enter the market with ready access, a functioning extortion workflow, and enough operational discipline to convert access into postings.

DragonForce is the outlier among the five. It is strategically important less because of Q1 2026 top-four victim share and more because it represents **productization and franchising** within RaaS. Its white-label model reportedly lets affiliates operate under their own branding while using DragonForce’s encryption, negotiation portals, and leak-site infrastructure [\[4\]](https://cxotoday.com/cybersecurity/ransomware-surges-as-weekly-attacks-hit-record-highs-in-april-2026/). That model changes the competitive unit from “ransomware group” to “ransomware infrastructure provider,” making DragonForce comparable to a back-end service provider in a criminal platform economy.

### 2. Operator Business Models: Scale, Margin, Reputation, Access, and Infrastructure

While earlier content discussed dominant operators as “criminal platforms” in general terms, this section separates their **economic models** into five competitive archetypes. This distinction matters because different operators can dominate for different reasons: volume, revenue efficiency, affiliate trust, access supply, or infrastructure franchising.

| Business-model archetype | Primary operator example | Revenue logic | Main constraint | Defensive implication at strategic level |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Scale platform | Qilin | More affiliates and more posted victims increase total payment opportunities | Payment conversion may fall if targeting is broad or negotiations are inflexible | Tracking affiliate migration and leak-site velocity is important, but payment data is needed to judge true profitability |
| Margin optimizer | Akira | Targets sectors where downtime and operational disruption increase willingness to pay | Requires higher-quality victim selection and stronger negotiation discipline | Sectoral risk modeling matters more than victim count alone |
| Reputation-restoration brand | LockBit 5.0 | Uses historical notoriety to regain affiliates and victim pressure | Must overcome trust damage from prior disruption | Brand revival can occur even after major takedowns |
| Access-accelerated challenger | The Gentlemen | Converts pre-existing access into rapid victim growth | Access stockpiles can decay or be remediated | Surge detection should include access-broker and perimeter-compromise indicators |
| White-label infrastructure provider | DragonForce | Monetizes affiliates that want independent branding without building full infrastructure | Quality control and affiliate governance become harder | Attribution by “brand” becomes less reliable when infrastructure is shared |

Qilin’s business model is the clearest example of **scale economics**. A large RaaS brand can achieve compounding advantages: more victims create more visibility; more visibility attracts more affiliates; more affiliates create more incidents; and more incidents reinforce the perception that the brand is active and dangerous. However, scale also creates governance problems. GuidePoint’s assessment that Qilin’s affiliates vary in sophistication and that high activity should not be equated with profitability points to a classic platform-management tradeoff: open or semi-open affiliate ecosystems expand quickly, but quality control suffers [\[1\]](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf). In legitimate platform economics, this resembles the difference between user growth and monetization quality; in RaaS, it appears as the gap between public victim postings and actual ransom receipts.

Akira appears to compete as a **target-quality and revenue-per-incident operator**. Reporting that it heavily targets consumer goods and industrial manufacturing—sectors with high downtime costs and complex IT/OT environments—suggests a strategy oriented around business interruption leverage [\[3\]](https://industrialcyber.co/ransomware/ransomware-sector-reconsolidating-as-qilin-lockbit-and-the-gentlemen-expand-influence-in-q1-2026/). This is economically rational: manufacturing and industrial victims may face production stoppages, contractual penalties, supply-chain delays, safety concerns, and reputational harm. Those pressures can raise the perceived cost of non-payment. The reported USD 244 million in Akira proceeds is therefore strategically important because it supports the hypothesis that Akira’s position cannot be measured solely by public victim rank [\[3\]](https://industrialcyber.co/ransomware/ransomware-sector-reconsolidating-as-qilin-lockbit-and-the-gentlemen-expand-influence-in-q1-2026/).

LockBit 5.0’s model is best described as **brand salvage plus technical refresh**. Before its disruption, LockBit was one of the most recognized ransomware brands. After Operation Cronos, however, the issue was not only infrastructure loss but underground confidence. ZeroFox reported that LockBit accounted for only around 1% of global ransomware attacks so far in 2025, roughly 42 incidents, and assessed that LockBit was seeking to regain credibility and market share [\[6\]](https://www.zerofox.com/intelligence/flash-report-rumored-new-coalition-of-ransomware-groups-yet-to-materialize). By Q1 2026, cited reporting placed LockBit at 163 victims, showing a meaningful rebound [\[2\]](https://www.cybersecurity-insiders.com/ransomware-q1-2026-consolidation/). The strategic lesson is that RaaS brands can retain residual economic value after disruption because affiliates and victims remember the name. Brand equity, even damaged brand equity, can be reactivated if the operator demonstrates enough continuity.

The Gentlemen’s model is the most access-centric. A reported pre-staged pool of roughly 14,700 compromised FortiGate devices enabled rapid scaling from 40 victims in Q4 2025 to 166 in Q1 2026 [\[2\]](https://www.cybersecurity-insiders.com/ransomware-q1-2026-consolidation/). This resembles a **vertical integration strategy**: rather than relying solely on opportunistic affiliates to bring access, the operator or its ecosystem appears to have controlled a large access resource. Economically, access is one of the most valuable upstream inputs in ransomware. If an operator can internalize or monopolize access supply, it can accelerate victim acquisition and reduce dependence on external initial access brokers.

DragonForce’s model is closest to **infrastructure-as-a-service for extortion**. Its white-label approach reportedly lets affiliates use DragonForce’s back-end capabilities while presenting their own brand to victims [\[4\]](https://cxotoday.com/cybersecurity/ransomware-surges-as-weekly-attacks-hit-record-highs-in-april-2026/). This is a structural innovation because it decouples front-end branding from back-end capability. In older RaaS models, the group name, ransomware payload, leak site, negotiation portal, and affiliate ecosystem were usually associated with a single brand. Under a white-label model, multiple apparent “groups” may in fact depend on the same service provider. That can inflate the apparent diversity of the ecosystem while actually increasing infrastructure concentration.

### 3. White-Label Ransomware as Criminal Franchising: Lowering Skill Barriers Without Removing Governance Risk

While earlier reporting already noted that white-label ransomware lowers barriers to entry, this section focuses specifically on its **market-structure consequences**: how it enables non-technical criminals, changes affiliate incentives, complicates attribution, and creates a franchise-like layer between ransomware developers and extortion crews.

DragonForce is the clearest publicly reported example of this white-label phenomenon. Multiple sources describe DragonForce as offering affiliates the ability to operate independent brands using shared encryption tooling, negotiation portals, and data-leak-site infrastructure [\[4\]](https://cxotoday.com/cybersecurity/ransomware-surges-as-weekly-attacks-hit-record-highs-in-april-2026/), [\[7\]](https://www.darkreading.com/cyberattacks-data-breaches/extortion-gangs-join-forces-ransomware-cartel). This is materially different from a traditional affiliate model. In the conventional RaaS structure, affiliates usually work under the parent brand’s name. In a white-label model, the affiliate can present as a distinct operation while outsourcing core infrastructure to the provider.

| RaaS function | Traditional affiliate model | White-label model | Market effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Brand identity | Parent group brand dominates | Affiliate may use its own brand | Increases apparent group diversity even if infrastructure is centralized |
| Technical capability | Provided by RaaS operator | Provided by white-label back end | Enables less technical actors to operate at higher apparent maturity |
| Negotiation portal | Usually parent-controlled | Shared infrastructure can support separate affiliate branding | Blurs boundaries between “groups” and “service providers” |
| Data-leak site | Parent leak site | White-label or affiliate-branded leak presence | Makes victim counting and attribution more difficult |
| Revenue split | Affiliate receives defined percentage of payment | Reported DragonForce model allows affiliates to retain up to 80% [\[8\]](https://www.secureblink.com/threat-research/2026-ransomware-cartelization-qilin-lock-bit-and-akira-convergence) | Strong incentive for affiliates seeking autonomy without infrastructure costs |

The key economic effect is **entry-cost compression**. A non-technical or semi-technical criminal actor no longer needs to develop ransomware, build a leak site, maintain negotiation infrastructure, manage cryptocurrency payment workflows, or create a recognizable brand from scratch. Instead, the actor can rent or affiliate into a platform that provides the core machinery. The white-label provider benefits by monetizing more affiliates, while the affiliate benefits by appearing independent and potentially retaining a larger share of proceeds.

The reported DragonForce affiliate share of up to 80% is important in this context [\[8\]](https://www.secureblink.com/threat-research/2026-ransomware-cartelization-qilin-lock-bit-and-akira-convergence). For affiliates, revenue share is a labor-market signal. If a platform offers strong technical support, credible infrastructure, and a high payout percentage, it can attract displaced affiliates from disrupted brands. SecureBlink’s reporting claims DragonForce absorbed displaced RansomHub affiliates through a dedicated migration portal, though this specific claim should be treated with moderate confidence unless corroborated by primary incident-response or law-enforcement reporting [\[8\]](https://www.secureblink.com/threat-research/2026-ransomware-cartelization-qilin-lock-bit-and-akira-convergence). Broadcom/Symantec’s broader statement that former RansomHub affiliates migrated to Qilin, Akira, and DragonForce is more strongly supported by a top-tier vendor source [\[5\]](https://sed-cms.broadcom.com/sites/default/files/2026-01/RWN-2026-WP100_1.pdf).

White-label RaaS also changes the meaning of “fragmentation.” A market may appear fragmented because many brands are posting victims, but if those brands rely on the same white-label infrastructure, the underlying market may actually be consolidating. This creates an analytical distinction between **brand fragmentation** and **infrastructure concentration**. Public leak-site counts mostly measure the former; incident-response telemetry, malware lineage analysis, payment tracing, and infrastructure correlation are needed to assess the latter.

| Observable signal | Possible interpretation | Confidence caveat |
|---|---|---|
| Many small brands appear in a quarter | True fragmentation of operators | Could instead reflect white-label affiliates presenting separate identities |
| Similar negotiation portals or leak-site structures across brands | Shared infrastructure or copied design | Similarity alone is not proof of common control |
| Common malware lineage across brands | Shared builder, code reuse, or white-label provider | Code reuse can occur through leaks and forks |
| Affiliate migration after a shutdown | Labor reallocation toward stronger platforms | Exact migration paths are often inferred, not directly confirmed |
| High affiliate revenue share | Platform competing aggressively for labor | Advertised terms may differ from actual payouts |

The governance risk for white-label providers is substantial. A provider that lowers entry barriers may attract affiliates with poor operational discipline, reckless targeting preferences, or weak negotiation skills. That can damage the provider’s underground reputation, provoke law-enforcement attention, or create internal disputes. This mirrors the quality-control problem seen in open RaaS ecosystems. Qilin’s reported variation in affiliate competence offers a useful analogy: affiliate scale increases reach but can reduce consistency [\[1\]](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf).

For defenders and intelligence teams, white-label ransomware requires a shift from group-name attribution to **service-layer analysis**. The relevant question is not only “which group claimed the victim?” but also “which infrastructure, affiliate ecosystem, malware lineage, and payment network enabled the incident?” This is especially important where a white-label affiliate creates a short-lived brand, posts a small number of victims, and then disappears. In such cases, the brand may be ephemeral, but the underlying service provider may persist.

### 4. Affiliate Economics and Profit-Sharing: Labor-Market Competition Inside RaaS

While earlier content mentioned selected profit-sharing terms, this section examines profit-sharing as a **labor-market mechanism**. RaaS operators compete for affiliates much like platforms compete for sellers, drivers, or developers: they must offer revenue, tooling, reputation, dispute resolution, and operational support. In early 2026, affiliate competition is central to reconsolidation because displaced affiliates from disrupted or collapsed brands are the fastest way for dominant operators to scale.

The most specific reported affiliate-share terms in the provided source set concern Qilin and DragonForce. Prior reporting cited Qilin affiliate shares of 80% for payments up to USD 3 million and 85% for payments above USD 3 million. DragonForce is reported to let affiliates retain up to 80% of ransom proceeds under its white-label model [\[8\]](https://www.secureblink.com/threat-research/2026-ransomware-cartelization-qilin-lock-bit-and-akira-convergence). These numbers should be interpreted as advertised or reported criminal-market terms rather than audited financial facts. Nevertheless, they are analytically valuable because they show how RaaS operators use revenue sharing to recruit and retain affiliates.

| Operator | Reported affiliate economics | Source confidence | Analytical interpretation |
|---|---:|---|---|
| Qilin | 80% affiliate share for payments ≤ USD 3 million; 85% for payments > USD 3 million, as reported in prior threat-intelligence summaries | Moderate, because criminal terms are difficult to independently audit | Tiered incentives reward affiliates that secure higher-value payments |
| DragonForce | Affiliates reportedly retain up to 80% under white-label model [\[8\]](https://www.secureblink.com/threat-research/2026-ransomware-cartelization-qilin-lock-bit-and-akira-convergence) | Moderate | High share plus independent branding is designed to attract affiliates seeking autonomy |
| Akira | Specific early-2026 affiliate split not confirmed in provided sources | Low for exact percentage | Competitive advantage appears more tied to targeting economics and proceeds than published commission terms |
| LockBit 5.0 | Specific LockBit 5.0 split not confirmed in provided sources | Low for exact percentage | Likely competes on brand familiarity and rebuilt tooling, but must overcome trust damage |
| The Gentlemen | Specific split not confirmed in provided sources | Low for exact percentage | Rapid growth appears access-driven rather than publicly documented commission-driven |

Profit-sharing terms serve four functions in the RaaS economy. First, they compensate affiliates for bearing operational risk. Second, they signal platform confidence: a group that gives affiliates a high share may be trying to scale quickly or win market share from rivals. Third, they create incentives for affiliates to bring higher-value targets. Fourth, they can stabilize the ecosystem after disruptions by giving displaced operators a clear path to monetization.

However, high affiliate shares can also create margin pressure for RaaS administrators. If the platform retains only 15%–20%, it must process substantial volume or high-value payments to cover infrastructure, support, laundering costs, recruiting, and operational security losses. This dynamic may explain why large RaaS operators pursue scale: even if the administrator’s percentage is smaller than the affiliate’s, the platform can profit from many simultaneous affiliates.

Payment-rate decline complicates this model. GuidePoint cites Coveware reporting that ransom payment rates fell to a historic low of 23% in Q3 2025, meaning fewer than one in four victims paid, while also noting the challenge of reconciling lower payment rates with continued ransomware ubiquity [\[1\]](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf). This creates an important macro-economic tension: if fewer victims pay, operators must either increase victim volume, increase average payment size, improve conversion rates, reduce costs, or move into adjacent extortion models.

| Economic pressure | Likely RaaS response | Operators most aligned with response |
|---|---|---|
| Lower payment rate | Increase victim volume | Qilin, The Gentlemen |
| Lower payment rate | Target sectors with higher downtime leverage | Akira |
| Affiliate distrust after disruption | Rebuild brand and demonstrate activity | LockBit 5.0 |
| Competition for displaced affiliates | Offer high revenue share and infrastructure | DragonForce, Qilin |
| Cost of maintaining full infrastructure | White-label shared services | DragonForce |
| Need for faster scale | Use pre-existing access pipelines | The Gentlemen |

This also explains why public victim volume can remain high even if total payments fall. Broadcom/Symantec reported that ransomware activity reached record-high levels in 2025, but also observed growth in extortion attacks that did not involve encryption, with total extortion attacks reaching 6,182 in 2025 when data-theft-only incidents are included, a 23% increase from 2024 [\[5\]](https://sed-cms.broadcom.com/sites/default/files/2026-01/RWN-2026-WP100_1.pdf). If payment conversion weakens, actors may compensate by expanding the top of the funnel: more victims, more leak threats, more data-theft extortion, and more affiliate recruitment.

The labor-market view also clarifies the significance of RansomHub’s disappearance. Broadcom/Symantec reported that RansomHub shut down in April 2025 and that former affiliates quickly migrated to other threats such as Qilin, Akira, and DragonForce [\[5\]](https://sed-cms.broadcom.com/sites/default/files/2026-01/RWN-2026-WP100_1.pdf). This is not merely a narrative of one group replacing another; it is evidence of a flexible criminal labor pool. Affiliates are not permanently attached to brands. They move toward platforms that offer the best combination of payout, reliability, tooling, reputation, and access to victims.

LockBit 5.0’s challenge is especially acute in this labor-market context. ZeroFox assessed that LockBit struggled to regain affiliate trust after its 2024 disruption [\[6\]](https://www.zerofox.com/intelligence/flash-report-rumored-new-coalition-of-ransomware-groups-yet-to-materialize). A relaunch therefore requires more than new malware. It requires proof that affiliates will be paid, infrastructure will remain available, negotiations will be supported, and law-enforcement compromise risk is manageable. The Q1 2026 victim count suggests LockBit made progress, but its long-term position depends on whether affiliates view the recovery as durable.

### 5. Corporate-Style Partnerships, Cartel Signaling, and the Limits of Claimed Alliances

While previous content referenced cartel-style partnerships generally, this section evaluates the **credibility and market purpose** of those partnerships. The key analytical question is whether alliances among DragonForce, LockBit, and Qilin represent genuine operational integration, reputational signaling, or affiliate-recruitment theater.

In September 2025, reporting described a claimed collaboration or “cartel” involving LockBit, Qilin, and DragonForce. Dark Reading, citing ReliaQuest, reported that DragonForce, LockBit, and Qilin were associated with a coalition narrative and that such a partnership could help restore LockBit’s reputation after disruption [\[7\]](https://www.darkreading.com/cyberattacks-data-breaches/extortion-gangs-join-forces-ransomware-cartel). ZeroFox was more cautious, assessing that the rumored coalition had yet to materialize and that LockBit was almost certainly seeking to regain credibility and market share [\[6\]](https://www.zerofox.com/intelligence/flash-report-rumored-new-coalition-of-ransomware-groups-yet-to-materialize). CXO Today similarly reported that while DragonForce announced a strategic alliance with LockBit and Qilin in September 2025, no joint operations had been observed, suggesting the announcement was primarily a positioning move [\[4\]](https://cxotoday.com/cybersecurity/ransomware-surges-as-weekly-attacks-hit-record-highs-in-april-2026/).

| Claimed partnership element | Evidence status | Likely economic purpose |
|---|---|---|
| DragonForce, LockBit, and Qilin alliance announcement | Reported by multiple secondary and vendor-linked sources [\[4\]](https://cxotoday.com/cybersecurity/ransomware-surges-as-weekly-attacks-hit-record-highs-in-april-2026/), [\[7\]](https://www.darkreading.com/cyberattacks-data-breaches/extortion-gangs-join-forces-ransomware-cartel) | Signal strength to affiliates and rivals |
| Confirmed joint operations | Not observed in cited reporting as of early 2026 [\[4\]](https://cxotoday.com/cybersecurity/ransomware-surges-as-weekly-attacks-hit-record-highs-in-april-2026/) | Operational integration remains unproven |
| LockBit reputational benefit | Analytically plausible and explicitly discussed by ReliaQuest via Dark Reading [\[7\]](https://www.darkreading.com/cyberattacks-data-breaches/extortion-gangs-join-forces-ransomware-cartel) | Borrow credibility from stronger or more active brands |
| DragonForce benefit | Strongly plausible | Promote white-label infrastructure as a cartel-grade service layer |
| Qilin benefit | Less clear because Qilin already held strong market position | May reinforce dominance or deter affiliate poaching |

The term “cartel” should be used carefully. In a strict economic sense, a cartel coordinates market behavior among independent suppliers, often to control prices, reduce competition, or allocate markets. In the ransomware ecosystem, a cartel-like arrangement could theoretically involve non-aggression agreements, affiliate-sharing rules, infrastructure pooling, or norms around victim handling. However, the available evidence does not confirm a fully integrated cartel. Instead, the stronger conclusion is that leading RaaS brands are experimenting with **corporate-style strategic signaling**.

The signaling value is substantial. Affiliates face uncertainty when choosing a platform: Will the group survive law enforcement? Will the infrastructure remain online? Will administrators pay affiliates? Will the brand intimidate victims? Public alliance claims can reduce that uncertainty by creating the appearance of scale and stability. For LockBit, association with Qilin and DragonForce could help repair underground credibility. For DragonForce, association with major brands could validate its white-label model. For Qilin, participation or even perceived proximity could reinforce its position as a top-tier operator.

Corporate-style behavior is also visible in how groups manage market narratives. DragonForce has been described as self-proclaiming a “cartel” and pioneering a white-label infrastructure model [\[4\]](https://cxotoday.com/cybersecurity/ransomware-surges-as-weekly-attacks-hit-record-highs-in-april-2026/). Such language is not incidental. It is brand marketing directed at affiliates, rivals, and victims. In the criminal underground, perceived professionalism can become a competitive advantage. Operators that appear organized may attract affiliates who want reliable payment and tooling; they may also pressure victims who believe the threat actor can execute leaks or sustain extortion.

| Corporate-style behavior | RaaS manifestation | Competitive effect |
|---|---|---|
| Strategic alliance announcement | Public claims of cooperation among major brands | Reassures affiliates and signals market power |
| Franchising | White-label ransomware infrastructure | Expands reach without requiring one unified brand |
| Brand relaunch | LockBit 5.0 after disruption | Attempts to preserve customer-equivalent recognition among affiliates and victims |
| Channel-partner model | Affiliates bring access and conduct intrusions | Lets operators scale without directly executing every intrusion |
| Revenue-tier incentives | Higher affiliate share for larger payments | Aligns affiliate behavior with platform revenue goals |
| Market segmentation | Akira’s emphasis on high-downtime sectors | Improves expected value per incident |

The limits of such partnerships are equally important. Ransomware groups operate in a low-trust criminal environment. They lack enforceable contracts, legitimate dispute resolution, and stable governance. Alliances can collapse over payment disputes, accusations of theft, operational security failures, or law-enforcement infiltration. Therefore, a claimed cartel may function more as a **coordination narrative** than as a durable institution. ZeroFox’s caution that the rumored coalition had yet to materialize is significant because it prevents overinterpreting propaganda as confirmed operational integration [\[6\]](https://www.zerofox.com/intelligence/flash-report-rumored-new-coalition-of-ransomware-groups-yet-to-materialize).

The historical comparison to LockBit’s earlier association with Maze is relevant but should not be overstated. Dark Reading notes that similar partnerships have been transformative in the past, including LockBit’s 2020 collaboration with Maze, which helped popularize double extortion by combining encryption with data theft [\[7\]](https://www.darkreading.com/cyberattacks-data-breaches/extortion-gangs-join-forces-ransomware-cartel). The early-2026 environment differs because double extortion is already mature. The new potential innovation is not the extortion method itself, but the **organizational model**: white-label infrastructure, cross-brand credibility borrowing, affiliate pooling, and infrastructure-sharing claims.

For market-consolidation analysis, the partnership narrative reinforces the view that RaaS is moving toward fewer, stronger service ecosystems even if many brand names remain visible. The most important competitive units may increasingly be: dominant RaaS platforms such as Qilin; revenue-optimized operators such as Akira; revived legacy brands such as LockBit 5.0; access-accelerated challengers such as The Gentlemen; and back-end franchisors such as DragonForce. Public group names remain useful, but they are no longer sufficient for understanding market power. The more durable indicators are affiliate flows, infrastructure reuse, payment conversion, access supply, and the ability to project trust in a criminal marketplace.


## Affiliate Economics and Labor Migration: Profit-Sharing, Deposits, Recruitment Models, and Post-Disruption Talent Flows

### 1. RaaS Labor Pricing: Commission Splits, Entry Deposits, and Trust as an Economic Constraint

While the previous sections discussed affiliate economics as a broad labor-market competition problem, this section focuses more narrowly on **how RaaS platforms price affiliate participation**: commission rates, entry deposits, negotiation-service deductions, and the trust mechanisms that determine whether displaced affiliates will join or avoid a platform. The most important economic shift by early 2026 is that affiliate compensation has converged around a high-affiliate-share model, typically near **80% to the affiliate and 20% to the operator** where terms are publicly reported or inferred from threat-intelligence sources. This model is not merely a revenue split; it is the labor contract that allows RaaS operators to scale quickly without directly conducting every intrusion themselves. AhnLab’s 2026 outlook describes white-label and cartel-style RaaS models in which central platforms provide ransomware tooling, exfiltration infrastructure, and negotiation tooling while affiliates execute attacks under independent brands and pay about **20% of profits** to the platform, a more affiliate-friendly structure than older models that could take **30–40%** from affiliates ([1](https://download.ahnlab.com/global/brochure/2025_Threat_Analysis_2026_Predictions_en.pdf)).

The pricing logic reflects the asymmetric division of labor. Operators provide platform assets—malware development, leak-site infrastructure, payment infrastructure, victim portals, decryption capability, and sometimes negotiation assistance—while affiliates provide the scarce and risky inputs: access, intrusion execution, victim selection, data theft, and deployment. In a fragmented market, operators may offer generous terms to recruit affiliates; in a consolidated market, dominant operators can use brand reputation, payment reliability, and support capacity to retain them. By Q1 2026, the top-10 concentration of victims at **71%** indicates that affiliates were not evenly distributed across dozens of brands; instead, they were clustering around platforms perceived as liquid, reputable, and capable of monetizing intrusions at scale ([2](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)).

| Economic lever | Confirmed or reported figures | Analytical significance | Confidence level |
|---|---:|---|---|
| Standard affiliate share in emerging cartel/white-label models | About **80% affiliate / 20% operator** | Makes RaaS attractive to skilled intruders and lowers switching friction after disruption | Medium-high where reported by AhnLab; varies by group ([1](https://download.ahnlab.com/global/brochure/2025_Threat_Analysis_2026_Predictions_en.pdf)) |
| Earlier or legacy operator share | **30–40%** operator share reported as less favorable in older models | Shows competitive pressure toward affiliate-favorable pricing | Medium; generalized across models, not always group-specific ([1](https://download.ahnlab.com/global/brochure/2025_Threat_Analysis_2026_Predictions_en.pdf)) |
| LockBit 5.0 affiliate deposit | Approximately **US$500 in Bitcoin** reported for new affiliates | Functions as screening, anti-infiltration, and commitment fee after law-enforcement disruption | Medium-high; reported by Check Point from underground-launch observations ([2](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)) |
| LockBit operator cut | Reported at **20% operator / 80% affiliate** in open-source analysis of affiliate rules | Aligns LockBit with the dominant high-affiliate-share model | Medium; useful but less authoritative than primary IR/vendor telemetry ([3](https://privacyinsightsolutions.com/blog/raas-business-plan-unit-economics-2026)) |
| Operator-led negotiation adjustment | Affiliate share reportedly reduced to roughly **50–70%** if LockBit handles negotiation | Indicates platform services are monetized separately from malware access | Low-medium; reported by secondary OSINT analysis ([3](https://privacyinsightsolutions.com/blog/raas-business-plan-unit-economics-2026)) |
| DragonForce white-label/cartel share | **80% affiliate** share reported in AhnLab’s 2026 outlook | Demonstrates franchise-style model where brand, infrastructure, and affiliate identity can be decoupled | Medium-high for existence and broad split; lower for exact terms across affiliates ([1](https://download.ahnlab.com/global/brochure/2025_Threat_Analysis_2026_Predictions_en.pdf)) |

The **US$500 LockBit 5.0 deposit** is especially important because it shows how a damaged brand attempted to rebuild its labor market after Operation Cronos. The deposit is small enough to lower entry barriers compared with older high-fee affiliate programs, but large enough to impose a minimal cost on unserious applicants, researchers, infiltrators, or low-quality criminals. Check Point reported that LockBit 5.0 launched on the RAMP underground forum in September 2025 and required new affiliates to provide a Bitcoin deposit of approximately **US$500**; by Q1 2026, LockBit posted **163 victims**, up **106%** from Q4 2025, suggesting that its recruitment model regained some operational traction, although Check Point cautioned that some postings could be recycled or fictional unless independently validated ([2](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)).

The economics of deposits differs from the economics of subscriptions. A subscription model creates predictable income for the operator but caps upside and attracts low-skill participants. A profit-share model gives the operator exposure to successful ransom events and incentivizes it to improve tooling, leak-site uptime, negotiation processes, and decryption reliability. Open-source business-model analysis argues that pure subscription-style ransomware access is structurally unattractive because revenue is capped by subscriber count, whereas commission models participate directly in high-value ransom events; this is consistent with the observed dominance of affiliate profit-sharing among major RaaS operations ([3](https://privacyinsightsolutions.com/blog/raas-business-plan-unit-economics-2026)).

This creates a platform-market structure similar to an illicit two-sided marketplace. Affiliates evaluate operators based on expected revenue, operational support, reliability, brand fear, and the probability of being cheated or disrupted. Operators evaluate affiliates based on access quality, target selection, intrusion competence, ransom-negotiation discipline, and counterintelligence risk. In this market, the commission split is only one variable. A platform with a slightly worse split may still win affiliates if it offers higher payment probability, better negotiation support, more reliable decryption, established leak-site traffic, or stronger reputation among victims. Conversely, a generous split may fail if affiliates believe the platform is infiltrated, unstable, technically unreliable, or likely to disappear before monetization.

The significance of ransom-payment rates further complicates affiliate pricing. Coveware reported a historically low ransom-payment rate of **20%** in Q4 2025, while median payments rose to **US$325,000**, a **132% quarter-over-quarter increase**, according to summarized data in the provided source set ([4](https://www.stingrai.io/blog/malware-attack-statistics-2026)). This implies that affiliates face a market with lower conversion but higher potential payout per converted victim. A high affiliate share can compensate for lower payment probability, but only if the operator can help convert a sufficient portion of victims into paying cases. That makes negotiation infrastructure, victim communications, and reputation for working decryptors economically important platform services, not peripheral features.

The same logic helps explain why dominant RaaS platforms may benefit from consolidation even if total payment rates decline. If fewer victims pay, affiliates may prefer platforms with higher perceived conversion rates. A recognized brand can increase perceived threat credibility, while a stable leak site can increase pressure on victims. Check Point’s Q1 2026 data showing **2,122 DLS-posted victims** and a stable monthly average of about **707 victims** indicates that extortion volume remained historically high even after Q4 2025’s record, giving affiliates enough market activity to choose platforms based on expected monetization rather than simply availability ([2](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)).

There is also an important distinction between **posted victims** and **paid victims**. DLS counts measure claimed or posted extortion activity, not confirmed intrusions, successful encryption events, or paid ransoms. This matters for affiliate economics because an operator can inflate apparent scale by posting recycled or unverifiable victims, but affiliates ultimately respond to realized revenue, not only public branding. Check Point explicitly qualifies LockBit 5.0’s Q1 2026 rebound by noting uncertainty over whether all reports are genuine, recycled, or fictional; this is a reminder that DLS volume is a labor-market signal but not definitive proof of profitability ([2](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)).

Finally, the profit-share structure creates incentives for specialization. Some affiliates may focus on initial access acquisition, others on intrusion execution, others on negotiation, and others on data-theft-only extortion. Broadcom/Symantec observed that encryption-based ransomware attacks have remained just above **4,700 annually** for several years, while non-encryption extortion rose significantly; including data-theft-only attacks, 2025 extortion incidents reached **6,182**, a **23% increase** over 2024 ([5](https://sed-cms.broadcom.com/sites/default/files/2026-01/RWN-2026-WP100_1.pdf)). This means RaaS operators are no longer only selling encryption capability; they are monetizing an extortion supply chain in which data theft, leak-site pressure, negotiation portals, and affiliate recruitment are all revenue-generating platform functions.

### 2. Post-Disruption Hiring Pipelines: How Collapsed Brands Feed Qilin, Akira, LockBit 5.0, and New Entrants

While the previous report addressed post-disruption reallocation at a market level, this section examines the **hiring pipeline mechanics**: how affiliates move after takedowns, shutdowns, trust failures, or brand collapses, and why dominant operators can absorb them faster than small groups. RaaS labor migration is not random. Affiliates tend to move toward platforms that offer continuity of earnings, usable tooling, credible leak infrastructure, recognizable branding, and a functioning internal governance model. In early 2026, the clearest evidence of this process is the movement of activity toward Qilin, Akira, LockBit 5.0, The Gentlemen, and related dominant operators after the weakening or disappearance of earlier leaders such as LockBit’s pre-Cronos platform and RansomHub ([2](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)).

The 2024–2026 sequence shows a repeating cycle. Law enforcement disrupts a major platform; affiliates scatter; some form small groups; many join surviving platforms; the market briefly fragments; then concentration rises as better-capitalized operators absorb skilled labor. Check Point described Q1 2026 as a sharp reversal from Q3 2025 fragmentation: the top 10 groups accounted for **71%** of all victims, after a period when the ecosystem had peaked at **85 active groups** and many small groups produced minimal victim volume ([2](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)). Industrial Cyber’s reporting on the same Check Point data states that active groups dropped from **85 to 71**, while **21 new names** appeared and most posted fewer than **10 victims**, indicating that entry remained easy but durable scaling remained difficult ([6](https://industrialcyber.co/ransomware/ransomware-sector-reconsolidating-as-qilin-lockbit-and-the-gentlemen-expand-influence-in-q1-2026/)).

The collapse of RansomHub in April 2025 appears to have been one of the most important labor-supply shocks. Broadcom/Symantec reported that RansomHub shut down in April 2025 after being one of the largest RaaS operations, and that other players benefited significantly, especially Akira and Qilin, which each accounted for **16% of claimed attacks** in 2025 ([5](https://sed-cms.broadcom.com/sites/default/files/2026-01/RWN-2026-WP100_1.pdf)). AhnLab likewise reported that former RansomHub affiliates shifted to Qilin, DragonForce, and other operators after the shutdown, reinforcing the view that RaaS disruptions frequently redistribute labor rather than eliminate it ([1](https://download.ahnlab.com/global/brochure/2025_Threat_Analysis_2026_Predictions_en.pdf)).

| Disruption or market event | Labor-market effect | Groups reported to benefit or re-enter | Evidence and limitations |
|---|---|---|---|
| Operation Cronos against LockBit in 2024 | Reduced trust in LockBit’s centralized platform; affiliates dispersed or hedged across competitors | Qilin, RansomHub, later LockBit 5.0 partial return | Confirmed law-enforcement disruption; exact affiliate counts are uncertain ([2](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)) |
| RansomHub shutdown in April 2025 | Sudden supply of experienced affiliates seeking new monetization platforms | Qilin, Akira, DragonForce, others | Broadcom and AhnLab identify beneficiary groups; individual affiliate identities remain mostly unverified ([5](https://sed-cms.broadcom.com/sites/default/files/2026-01/RWN-2026-WP100_1.pdf), [1](https://download.ahnlab.com/global/brochure/2025_Threat_Analysis_2026_Predictions_en.pdf)) |
| Q3 2025 fragmentation peak | Many small brands competed, but most lacked scale, support, or affiliate depth | Incumbents later regained share | Check Point reported 85 groups in Q3 2025; many low-volume actors failed to persist ([2](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)) |
| LockBit 5.0 relaunch in September 2025 | Attempted re-recruitment using known brand, lower deposit barrier, and updated platform messaging | LockBit 5.0 posted 163 victims in Q1 2026 | Rebound confirmed in DLS data, but authenticity of all postings cannot be assumed ([2](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)) |
| Q1 2026 reconsolidation | Affiliates clustered around fewer platforms with higher throughput | Qilin, Akira, The Gentlemen, LockBit 5.0 | Top 10 groups accounted for 71% of victims; four groups together claimed 41% ([2](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)) |

Qilin’s growth is particularly important because it demonstrates how a platform can convert labor inflows into sustained output rather than short-term spikes. Check Point reported that Qilin was the most prominent ransomware operation for the third consecutive quarter in Q1 2026, posting **338 victims**; it also stated that Qilin alone posted more victims than the combined output of the bottom **50** groups ([2](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)). Kaspersky similarly assessed that Qilin became the dominant player from Q2 2025 onward and was the most active group executing targeted attacks in 2025, describing it as a fast-growing RaaS platform with a structured affiliate model ([7](https://securelist.com/state-of-ransomware-in-2026/119761/)).

Akira’s trajectory shows a different kind of absorptive capacity: sector-focused monetization. Broadcom/Symantec reported Akira at **16% of claimed attacks** in 2025, matching Qilin’s share, while AhnLab counted **622 incidents**, placing Akira first in its 2025 group ranking and Qilin second with **589 incidents** ([5](https://sed-cms.broadcom.com/sites/default/files/2026-01/RWN-2026-WP100_1.pdf), [1](https://download.ahnlab.com/global/brochure/2025_Threat_Analysis_2026_Predictions_en.pdf)). The specific counts differ by methodology, but the directional finding is consistent: Akira and Qilin became major absorbers of attack volume after the weakening of earlier leaders. These differences should not be treated as contradictions unless the underlying collection methods are identical; ransomware telemetry varies depending on whether the source counts DLS postings, confirmed incidents, claimed attacks, targeted intrusions, or victim organizations.

LockBit 5.0’s hiring problem is different from Qilin’s and Akira’s. LockBit had to overcome brand damage from Operation Cronos, arrests, infrastructure seizures, and public exposure. Before its takedown, LockBit was responsible for roughly **20–30%** of global DLS victim postings, according to Check Point’s historical characterization; after the disruption, it struggled to rebuild, but its September 2025 relaunch and Q1 2026 victim count show that the brand retained some residual labor-market value ([2](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)). Netlas, citing multiple industry sources, assessed that LockBit remained a **moderate but persistent** threat into 2026, but at a reduced and more contested level than its pre-2024 peak, and that evidence of a fully reconstituted centrally governed platform comparable to earlier LockBit iterations remained limited ([8](https://netlas.io/blog/top_10_critical_threat_actors/)).

This is important because affiliates do not migrate only toward the highest-paying commission schedule; they migrate toward platforms with credible governance. A platform that cannot protect affiliate identity, maintain payment systems, deliver decryption keys, or avoid internal leaks becomes less attractive even if it offers an 80% share. Ontinue’s discussion of the LockBit affiliate economy described the group’s post-disruption environment as chaotic, including a May 2025 leak that reportedly exposed a SQL database, victim records, and poor key-management evidence; while such details require careful validation, they illustrate why affiliate trust is a central economic variable after platform compromise ([9](https://www.ontinue.com/resource/blog-ransomware-affiliate-economy/)).

The Gentlemen’s Q1 2026 rise also illustrates how labor migration can occur through spinout formation rather than simple absorption. Check Point reported that The Gentlemen rose from **40 victims in Q4 2025** to **166 victims in Q1 2026**, becoming the third-ranked ransomware group for the quarter ([2](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)). Secondary reporting attributes the group’s growth to a former Qilin affiliate and a pre-staged stockpile of compromised devices, but because these claims are not as strongly corroborated in the provided authoritative sources, they should be treated as plausible but lower-confidence context rather than confirmed fact ([10](https://www.cybersecurity-insiders.com/ransomware-q1-2026-consolidation/)).

Labor migration therefore has two forms: **affiliate absorption** and **affiliate entrepreneurship**. In absorption, displaced operators join Qilin, Akira, DragonForce, LockBit 5.0, or another established platform. In entrepreneurship, skilled affiliates use experience, access, or tooling to launch a new brand. The Q1 2026 data suggests that entrepreneurship remains common—**21 new names** appeared during the quarter—but most new entrants lacked the production capacity to challenge incumbents, and many posted fewer than **10 victims** ([6](https://industrialcyber.co/ransomware/ransomware-sector-reconsolidating-as-qilin-lockbit-and-the-gentlemen-expand-influence-in-q1-2026/)). This implies that the RaaS economy has low barriers to brand creation but higher barriers to sustained platform operation.

The migration pattern also reveals why law-enforcement disruption can have mixed economic effects. Disruption damages specific brands, seizes infrastructure, creates distrust, and interrupts campaigns. However, unless it also removes affiliates, access brokers, laundering channels, and monetization pathways, it can create a temporary oversupply of experienced labor that surviving platforms exploit. Check Point explicitly describes this as a repeated ecosystem pattern: law-enforcement actions disrupt the market, affiliates scatter, and survivors who avoid disruption absorb displaced talent and grow ([2](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)).

From a macro-economic perspective, this resembles consolidation after a shock in a platform economy. Small firms may appear after a disruption, but the most efficient platforms reaggregate supply because they already have infrastructure, reputation, and monetization pathways. That is why the disappearance of major RaaS operations in 2025 did not lead to a durable reduction in ransomware volume. Instead, Broadcom/Symantec reported total extortion attacks of **6,182** in 2025 when data-theft-only cases are included, up **23%** from 2024, while NCC Group data summarized in the provided source set counted **7,874 tracked ransomware victims** in 2025, a **50% year-over-year increase**, with Qilin the most active group at **1,022 attacks** and **13%** share ([5](https://sed-cms.broadcom.com/sites/default/files/2026-01/RWN-2026-WP100_1.pdf), [4](https://www.stingrai.io/blog/malware-attack-statistics-2026)).

### 3. White-Label Franchising as Labor Arbitrage: Separating Brand, Infrastructure, and Technical Skill

The previous reports already covered white-label ransomware as criminal franchising at a high level. This section differs by focusing on **labor arbitrage**: how white-label models separate the economic roles of brand owner, infrastructure provider, malware developer, access holder, negotiator, and public-facing extortion identity. This separation allows non-technical or semi-technical criminals to participate in ransomware operations without building a complete stack. It also makes attribution harder because the public brand on a leak site may not correspond to the developers, infrastructure operators, negotiators, or access suppliers behind the intrusion ([1](https://download.ahnlab.com/global/brochure/2025_Threat_Analysis_2026_Predictions_en.pdf)).

AhnLab describes 2025 as the year in which ransomware cartels based on white-label models became a major trend. Under this model, central platforms provide encryption tools, data-exfiltration infrastructure, and negotiation systems, while affiliates execute attacks under independent brands and pay about **20%** of profits. AhnLab specifically notes that this enables a franchise-like attack structure without requiring technical expertise or infrastructure, lowering entry barriers and increasing both attack frequency and variety ([1](https://download.ahnlab.com/global/brochure/2025_Threat_Analysis_2026_Predictions_en.pdf)).

Dark Reading, summarizing Secureworks Counter Threat Unit research, reported that DragonForce and Anubis expanded RaaS affiliate models in 2025, with DragonForce advertising that affiliates could create their own “brand,” use their own malware, and still use DragonForce infrastructure. This is analytically significant because it turns a RaaS platform into an illicit infrastructure provider rather than only a ransomware-family operator. It also means that brand-level victim counts may understate the influence of the underlying platform provider if multiple affiliate brands share infrastructure or services ([11](https://www.darkreading.com/data-privacy/ransomware-gangs-innovate-new-affiliate-models)).

| RaaS model | What the operator provides | What the affiliate provides | Economic effect | Attribution effect |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Traditional closed RaaS | Malware, leak site, payment portal, rules, sometimes negotiation | Access, intrusion execution, deployment, victim handling | Scales operator brand through affiliate labor | Brand and tooling are more tightly linked |
| Profit-share platform | Same as above, with stronger revenue-sharing incentives | Higher-value intrusions and ransom execution | Aligns affiliate payout with ransom success | Attribution still difficult but platform identity is more visible |
| White-label franchise | Infrastructure, extortion stack, sometimes malware or support, but allows affiliate branding | Independent brand, access, victim selection, optional malware | Lowers skill and infrastructure barriers; expands labor pool | Public brand may obscure platform provider |
| Cartel-style coordination | Shared or coordinated infrastructure, non-aggression messaging, affiliate-sharing claims | Affiliates may move across brands or operate under multiple identities | Reduces destructive competition and can stabilize affiliate earnings | Attribution becomes multi-layered and less group-specific |

White-label franchising changes the market because it allows the same underlying infrastructure to support multiple public identities. A non-technical criminal may not need to build malware, maintain a leak site, design a negotiation portal, or create decryption infrastructure. Instead, the affiliate can rent or profit-share access to a platform that already provides those services. This resembles franchise economics: the platform standardizes core services while local operators conduct revenue-generating activity under their own label. The result is a larger labor pool, including actors who may have access to compromised networks or stolen data but lack ransomware-development capability.

The white-label phenomenon also shifts the competitive unit from “ransomware family” to “service stack.” Historically, defenders often tracked ransomware by payload, extension, ransom note, or leak-site identity. In a white-label economy, the same public-facing brand may use borrowed infrastructure, and the same infrastructure provider may enable multiple brands. AhnLab explicitly warns that this makes it harder to identify attack groups based solely on attacker brand ([1](https://download.ahnlab.com/global/brochure/2025_Threat_Analysis_2026_Predictions_en.pdf)). This does not eliminate technical attribution, but it reduces the reliability of simple brand-based conclusions.

The model also enables non-encryption extortion labor to be monetized through ransomware-like platforms. Broadcom/Symantec reported that encryption-involving attacks stayed just above **4,700 annually**, while data-theft-only extortion rose enough to push total 2025 extortion attacks to **6,182**, up **23%** from 2024 ([5](https://sed-cms.broadcom.com/sites/default/files/2026-01/RWN-2026-WP100_1.pdf)). This matters because a white-label platform does not need every affiliate to perform full ransomware deployment. Some affiliates may specialize in data theft, credential theft, extortion communications, or victim shaming. The platform can monetize these specializations through leak-site and negotiation services.

This labor segmentation lowers entry barriers but does not necessarily increase affiliate quality. The same model that admits more participants can also admit unreliable, reckless, or technically incompetent actors. Industrial Cyber’s discussion of Q1 2026 reconsolidation notes that fragmented landscapes can include transient operators with weak incentives to maintain reliable decryption tools, while dominant operators may have reputational incentives to maintain working recovery mechanisms because victim payments depend on trust ([6](https://industrialcyber.co/ransomware/ransomware-sector-reconsolidating-as-qilin-lockbit-and-the-gentlemen-expand-influence-in-q1-2026/)). In economic terms, white-label expansion increases labor supply, but platform consolidation may be needed to impose quality control.

The franchise model also creates a principal-agent problem. The platform operator wants affiliates to generate revenue without attracting excessive law-enforcement attention or damaging the platform’s reputation. Affiliates want maximum payout and may target victims or sectors that create strategic blowback. Some platforms publish rules about prohibited targets, but AhnLab reported that LockBit 5.0 explicitly allowed affiliates to attack critical infrastructure including nuclear and thermal power plants, which the report describes as a more aggressive posture than previous avoidance patterns ([1](https://download.ahnlab.com/global/brochure/2025_Threat_Analysis_2026_Predictions_en.pdf)). This claim should be treated as significant but interpreted carefully, because underground rules can be performative, inconsistently enforced, or used for recruitment signaling.

White-label models also make platform measurement harder. If DragonForce infrastructure supports an affiliate’s independent brand, should the victim count be attributed to DragonForce, the affiliate brand, or both? If an affiliate uses its own malware but a shared extortion platform, what is the relevant “group”? These questions are not semantic; they affect market-share estimates, disruption strategy, and risk forecasting. Victim-volume datasets based on leak-site names may undercount infrastructure providers and overcount superficial brands. Conversely, infrastructure clustering without sufficient evidence can over-attribute independent actors to a supposed cartel.

This is why source-confidence grading is essential. AhnLab and Secureworks/Dark Reading provide relatively strong evidence that new affiliate models exist and are being advertised. Claims about fully operational cartel integration, infrastructure pooling, or standardized cross-group governance require higher evidentiary thresholds. SecureBlink reports that DragonForce publicly proposed coalition formation with LockBit and Qilin and later declared the coalition active; it interprets this as cartel-style coordination rather than a traditional merger ([12](https://www.secureblink.com/threat-research/2026-ransomware-cartelization-qilin-lock-bit-and-akira-convergence)). That is analytically plausible and consistent with affiliate-market incentives, but unless supported by technical infrastructure correlation, shared wallet analysis, or confirmed affiliate overlap, it should be treated as a reported claim rather than a fully verified structural merger.

The white-label model is therefore best understood as a **capability-multiplication mechanism** rather than only a new branding tactic. It multiplies the number of actors who can participate in extortion; it lets access holders monetize intrusions without building ransomware infrastructure; it lets platform providers extract a 20% share from attacks they do not directly execute; and it allows criminal brands to appear, disappear, or rebrand faster than defenders can build stable naming conventions. This supports the broader early-2026 trend: fragmentation at the surface-brand layer can coexist with consolidation at the infrastructure and affiliate-services layer.

### 4. Affiliate Productivity Metrics: Victim Volume as a Proxy for Labor Absorption and Platform Throughput

This section differs from prior market-structure coverage by using victim counts as **labor-productivity indicators** rather than simply market-share rankings. In RaaS economics, a high victim count suggests that a platform has either recruited many affiliates, recruited highly productive affiliates, acquired a large access pipeline, or combined these advantages with strong operational support. DLS victim counts do not prove successful ransom payments, but they are still useful as a proxy for platform throughput, affiliate activity, and the ability to sustain operations over time ([2](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)).

Check Point’s Q1 2026 dataset provides the strongest near-term benchmark. Across more than **70 active DLS sites**, it recorded **2,122 new victims** in Q1 2026, down **12.2%** from the Q4 2025 record of **2,416**, but still **117% above Q1 2024**. Monthly counts were stable: **732** in January, **684** in February, and **706** in March, for an average of about **707 victims per month** ([2](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/)). Stability at this level suggests that the affiliate labor pool remained active and that consolidation did not reduce total operational tempo.

## White-Label Ransomware and Platformization: Enabling Non-Technical Criminals Through Shared Infrastructure and Negotiation Support

### 1. Service-Layer Consolidation Beneath a Noisy Brand Layer

While previous sections addressed market concentration and white-label franchising at a broad level, this section narrows the analysis to the service layer: the backend platforms, negotiation workflows, leak-site infrastructure, affiliate tooling, and operational support systems that allow low-skill or non-technical criminals to participate in ransomware operations without building a full ransomware stack. This distinction matters because early-2026 ransomware statistics can appear contradictory. On the surface, there are still many named groups and short-lived brands; beneath that surface, however, a smaller set of structured operators and shared service providers increasingly controls the infrastructure that makes those brands viable.

The best-supported macro indicator of this dual structure is the coexistence of high visible group counts with renewed victim concentration. Check Point Research reported 2,122 victims posted to ransomware data leak sites in Q1 2026, making it the second-highest first quarter on record, while the top ten ransomware groups accounted for 71% of all Q1 2026 victims [1]. Industrial Cyber’s reporting on the same Check Point dataset stated that active groups fell from 85 in Q3 2025 to 71 in Q1 2026, while the top ten’s victim share rose from 57% to 71.1% [2]. These figures indicate that the ecosystem is not simply becoming smaller; rather, it is becoming more top-heavy while still retaining a long tail of smaller labels, affiliates, and opportunistic brands.

This distinction is particularly important for understanding white-label ransomware. A visible brand on a leak site may not represent a fully independent ransomware organization. It may instead represent an affiliate identity, a short-lived extortion label, or a front-end brand supported by a shared backend provider. In other words, fragmentation at the naming layer can persist even as infrastructure, tooling, negotiation services, and affiliate management consolidate. This is one reason victim-count concentration can rise at the same time that defenders continue seeing new group names.

The Q1 2026 data also shows that dominance is no longer limited to legacy brands. Qilin posted 338 victims in Q1 2026 and held the top position for the third consecutive quarter, while LockBit 5.0 posted 163 victims and returned to fourth place after prior disruption [1]. The Gentlemen rose from 40 victims in Q4 2025 to 166 in Q1 2026, becoming the third-ranked ransomware operation by public victim claims [1]. Akira remained one of the dominant operators, and Qilin, Akira, The Gentlemen, and LockBit together claimed 41% of all Q1 2026 victims [1], [2]. From a platform-economics perspective, the important point is not only that these brands are prolific, but that affiliates appear to migrate toward operators that can provide reliable monetization infrastructure, stable leak-site presence, recognizable extortion branding, and operational support.

A second data layer comes from annual and monthly reporting. GuidePoint’s GRIT 2026 ransomware reporting stated that 124 distinct named ransomware groups were observed in 2025, a 46% year-over-year increase from 2024, with Qilin rising from 154 victims in 2024 to 1,044 in 2025 [3]. Breachsense reported 677 victim companies claimed across ransomware leak sites in January 2026, involving 58 active ransomware groups across 60 countries and 61 industries [4]. These numbers reinforce the view that the ransomware economy remains broad, but increasingly depends on a smaller number of scaled operators and repeatable service models.

The apparent contradiction between “124 named groups in 2025” and “top ten groups accounted for 71% of Q1 2026 victims” is best understood through platformization. A large number of names may reflect branding churn, affiliate experimentation, failed launches, rebrands, or white-label fronts. Concentration metrics, by contrast, reveal where operational capacity and affiliate confidence are actually accumulating. In a platformized RaaS economy, the number of visible brands is less important than the number of backend providers that can sustain leak sites, affiliate panels, negotiation workflows, payment handling, victim communications, and operational continuity.

Breachsense explicitly cautioned that its January 2026 figures were compiled from ransomware groups’ own leak sites and therefore reflect claimed victims rather than confirmed breaches, with the possibility of exaggerated or duplicated claims [4]. This caveat applies broadly across leak-site-based ransomware statistics. However, even imperfect leak-site datasets remain useful for macro-economic analysis because ransomware operators use public victim listings as part of their pressure and reputation systems. A group that consistently posts victims, maintains a recognizable leak site, and supports affiliates is signaling market capacity, even if individual claims require validation.

The service-layer interpretation also explains why law-enforcement disruption does not necessarily produce sustained decline. Check Point’s Q1 2026 analysis observed a recurring pattern: law-enforcement actions disrupt parts of the market, affiliates scatter, and surviving groups absorb displaced talent [1]. In this environment, white-label providers and mature RaaS platforms benefit from instability because they can offer displaced affiliates ready-made infrastructure. The affiliate does not need to rebuild everything after a brand collapses; the affiliate can migrate to a platform that already provides extortion infrastructure, negotiation channels, and victim-publication mechanisms.

This is where white-label ransomware becomes economically significant. It is not only a technical or branding innovation; it is a labor-market mechanism. It allows criminals who possess one monetizable asset—such as access to a victim network, stolen data, social-engineering capacity, or willingness to conduct extortion—to plug into a larger service ecosystem. The backend provider captures revenue from a broader labor pool, while the affiliate avoids the fixed costs of operating a complete ransomware enterprise. This reduces the minimum viable capability needed to participate in ransomware and helps explain why the long tail of visible brands can remain active even as the market reconsolidates around dominant service providers.

### 2. White-Label Service Bundles as Criminal Business Process Outsourcing

While previous sections described white-label ransomware as “criminal franchising,” this section focuses on the specific service-bundle logic that makes the model attractive to non-technical criminals. The closest legitimate-business analogy is business process outsourcing or managed services: a provider supplies standardized backend functions, while the customer or affiliate controls the public-facing brand, target selection, or monetization pathway. In cybercrime, this model lets an affiliate outsource the technically complex and operationally risky parts of ransomware while retaining a distinct identity.

The clearest publicly reported example in the supplied source set is DragonForce. Secureworks’ Counter Threat Unit, as reported by Dark Reading, described DragonForce’s March 2025 affiliate model as allowing affiliates to create their own “brand” and use their own malware while relying on DragonForce-provided infrastructure and tools [5]. The advertised service components reportedly included administration and client panels, encryption and ransom negotiation tools, a file storage system, a Tor-based leak site and onion domain, and support services [5]. Cyberpress’ summary of the Secureworks reporting similarly characterized the model as providing administration and client panels, encryption and ransom negotiation tools, file storage, a Tor-based leak site, and support services while allowing affiliates to deploy either DragonForce ransomware or their own malware [6].

The significance of this model is not the novelty of any single component; ransomware groups have long used leak sites, negotiation portals, and affiliate panels. The significance is the packaging. DragonForce’s reported model separates backend capability from front-end brand identity. An affiliate no longer has to choose between operating under a parent brand or building an entire independent operation. Instead, the affiliate can appear independent while consuming shared infrastructure. This is a white-label architecture: the service provider becomes less visible to victims and defenders, while the affiliate-facing brand becomes more flexible.

| Service Function | Role in Platformized RaaS | Barrier Removed for Non-Technical or Semi-Technical Criminals | Risk Introduced by Shared Infrastructure |
|---|---|---|---|
| Administrative panel | Centralizes case handling, affiliate activity, and victim tracking | Removes need to design custom workflow systems | A compromise or leak may expose multiple affiliates |
| Negotiation portal | Provides structured victim communication and payment discussion environment | Removes need to build secure communications and negotiation process | Shared styles or infrastructure may create attribution links |
| Leak-site hosting | Enables public pressure through victim listings and data publication threats | Removes need to maintain Tor publication infrastructure | Infrastructure takedown can disrupt many brands simultaneously |
| File storage | Supports stolen-data staging and extortion claims | Removes need to independently operate storage systems | Concentrates sensitive victim data in common repositories |
| Support services | Assists affiliates with platform use and operational issues | Allows less technical actors to participate | Creates internal trust and insider-risk problems |
| Optional malware choice | Lets affiliates use provider malware or bring their own | Broadens appeal to both low-skill and technically capable actors | Blurs attribution between malware lineage and extortion brand |

Sources: Secureworks CTU reporting as summarized by Dark Reading and Cyberpress [5], [6].

This model resembles corporate platformization more than the earlier “malware kit” model. A traditional malware kit provides a tool; a platform provides a workflow. In ransomware economics, the workflow is often more important than the payload. The revenue event depends on victim communication, pressure management, credibility, data-handling claims, payment logistics, and sometimes decryption assurance. A non-technical criminal may be unable to build reliable malware, but even if they obtain malware, they may still fail to monetize an intrusion without negotiation infrastructure, branding, and support. White-label RaaS attempts to convert these functions into reusable services.

The model also broadens the addressable criminal market. Secureworks assessed that DragonForce’s “everything but the malware” option could appeal to threat actors that have their own malware but lack the technical knowledge or support to reliably run other aspects of threat-actor infrastructure [5]. Cyberpress added that the model can attract both criminals with limited technical ability who benefit from an established backend and more skilled actors seeking infrastructure support without maintaining their own systems [6]. This means white-label RaaS does not only enable beginners; it also appeals to specialists who want to outsource non-core functions.

Anubis illustrates a related but distinct service-bundle pattern. Secureworks reporting, summarized by Dark Reading, described Anubis as offering a “data ransom” option in which the operators prepare a detailed “investigative article” analyzing the victim’s sensitive data on a password-protected Tor site, provide the victim with access and a negotiation link, and threaten publication if payment is not made [5]. The reporting also stated that Anubis claimed it would notify victims’ customers and regulators in the United States, United Kingdom, and Europe [5]. Cyberpress described Anubis as having a three-pronged affiliate model and an “accesses monetization” service for actors who already possess victim access, including data analysis tools intended to increase negotiation pressure [6].

The Anubis model is important because it shows that platformization is not limited to encryption. RaaS operators increasingly productize extortion-adjacent services: data triage, victim profiling, pressure-content creation, regulator/customer notification threats, and negotiation support. This lowers barriers for criminals who may have stolen data or access but lack the ability to turn that access into a convincing extortion campaign. It also reflects a shift from purely technical ransomware capability to criminal commercialization capability.

From a macro-economic perspective, white-label service bundles convert fixed costs into variable costs. Building malware, maintaining leak infrastructure, recruiting negotiators, and running support systems require investment. Once built, however, these services can be reused across many affiliates. The platform operator benefits from scale because each additional affiliate can generate revenue without requiring the operator to conduct every intrusion directly. This is analogous to a franchisor or managed-service provider monetizing a standardized backend across many customer-facing entities. The affiliate benefits because it avoids the capital and expertise required to create the backend.

This structure also explains why white-label models may coexist with dominant brands such as Qilin, Akira, and LockBit 5.0 rather than replacing them. A mature brand monetizes reputation directly: victims recognize the name, affiliates trust the payout model, and the market knows the group can publish data or provide decryption. A white-label provider monetizes infrastructure indirectly: it allows multiple outward-facing identities to use common services. Some operators may combine both strategies, maintaining a core brand while also supporting affiliated or loosely connected labels. The public data does not always reveal these relationships, which is why analysts should treat group names as one layer of evidence rather than the complete organizational map.

However, white-label service bundling also creates systemic weaknesses for criminals. Secureworks warned that if one affiliate in a shared environment is compromised, other affiliates’ operational and victim details could be exposed [5]. From an economic standpoint, this is a classic platform-risk problem. Shared infrastructure increases efficiency, but it also creates correlated failure. A single backend compromise, takedown, insider leak, or operational-security failure can affect many brands using the same service layer. Thus, platformization increases both scale and fragility.

For defenders and incident responders, the key analytical implication is that white-label ransomware should not be evaluated solely by the public leak-site name. A small or new group may be operationally more capable than its victim count suggests if it is backed by a mature platform. Conversely, a prolific brand may represent a portfolio of affiliates using shared services rather than a single centrally controlled team. In early 2026, the practical intelligence question is increasingly: which service ecosystem enabled the incident, and what does that imply about negotiation patterns, data-publication behavior, affiliate tradecraft, and likely victim pressure tactics?

### 3. Negotiation Support, Data Monetization, and the Professionalization of Extortion Workflows

While earlier sections addressed infrastructure and affiliate economics, this section examines negotiation support as a distinct product category inside platformized RaaS. This is a necessary distinction because ransomware monetization is not completed when malware is deployed or data is stolen. The criminal revenue event depends on converting victim distress into payment. As ransomware markets mature, operators increasingly professionalize this conversion process through negotiation portals, victim dashboards, data-analysis services, leak-site staging, pressure content, and support functions.

Negotiation support is economically valuable because many affiliates are not skilled negotiators. A technically capable intruder may be poor at pricing a ransom, communicating with a victim, sustaining pressure without collapsing talks, or making credible claims about data exposure. Conversely, a non-technical criminal may have obtained access or stolen data through purchased access, insider help, social engineering, or brokered credentials, but may lack any ability to manage an extortion case. Platformized RaaS fills this gap by offering structured communications and standardized pressure mechanisms.

DragonForce’s reported package included ransom negotiation tools and support services [5], [6]. This indicates that the operator was not merely selling malware capability; it was offering monetization infrastructure. Anubis went further by productizing the presentation of stolen data through an “investigative article” model, where analysis of the victim’s sensitive data is prepared for use in extortion pressure [5]. This resembles content production and crisis leverage rather than traditional malware deployment. Dark Reading explicitly compared Anubis’ data-ransom approach to content marketing and DragonForce’s model to a managed-services offering for threat actors [5].

This professionalization has several macro-economic effects. First, it increases the expected return from compromised access. If affiliates can outsource negotiation and pressure creation, more intrusions become monetizable. Second, it standardizes victim interaction. Victims may encounter polished portals, structured deadlines, staged publication threats, and claims of customer or regulator notification. Third, it shifts the competitive basis among RaaS operators. Operators compete not only on encryption speed or evasion features, but on affiliate support, payout reliability, leak-site credibility, negotiation tooling, and conversion rate.

The emphasis on conversion is visible in broader ransomware payment dynamics. Secure Blink, summarizing 2025 ecosystem economics, reported that total on-chain ransomware payments reached approximately $820 million in 2025, an 8% decline from 2024, while claimed attacks surged 50% year over year to nearly 8,000 leak events [7]. The same report stated that the median ransom payment grew 368% year over year to nearly $60,000 [7]. These figures suggest that higher activity did not translate linearly into higher aggregate payment volume. Operators therefore have an incentive to improve payment conversion, target selection, and negotiation effectiveness rather than simply increasing victim counts.

Vectra’s RaaS overview similarly noted that data-only extortion was losing effectiveness, with payment rates reportedly dropping to roughly 25% in Q4 2025, potentially encouraging a pivot back toward encryption-focused attacks in 2026 [8]. Although this figure should be treated as an estimate derived from industry reporting rather than a universal confirmed rate, it is analytically important. If payment rates fall, platforms must improve monetization per incident. Negotiation support, data analysis, victim pressure content, and credible decryption services become differentiators in a saturated extortion market.

| Negotiation tooling | Converts compromise into payment discussions; reduces affiliate skill requirements | DragonForce reportedly offered ransom negotiation tools [5], [6] | High for reported advertisement; actual implementation may vary |
| Data-analysis pressure | Helps criminals identify sensitive material and frame payment demand | Anubis reportedly offered data-ransom “investigative article” content [5] | High for reported model; case-level use requires validation |
| Customer/regulator notification threats | Increases reputational and compliance pressure on victims | Anubis reportedly claimed it would notify customers and regulators [5] | Reported claim; execution may vary |

A major distinction between mature RaaS brands and transient operators is the incentive to preserve reputation. Check Point argued that larger RaaS brands invest in operational consistency, including functional decryption tools, because their business model depends on the perception that payment results in data recovery [1]. This does not imply victims should pay or that payment guarantees recovery; rather, it reflects the criminal-market logic. A platform that repeatedly fails to provide working decryption may lose affiliate and victim credibility. In contrast, Check Point cited Obscura as an example of a transient operator whose encryption bug made files over 1 GB permanently unrecoverable regardless of payment [1]. That example illustrates why affiliates may prefer established platforms with tested tooling and support.

Negotiation services also allow operators to segment their criminal labor force. Affiliates can focus on access acquisition, lateral movement, or victim selection, while specialized negotiators and support personnel handle communications. This mirrors legitimate organizational specialization, but in a criminal setting. The platform can impose templates, deadlines, escalation rules, and publication practices, thereby creating a repeatable extortion process. For non-technical criminals, the existence of such templates and support mechanisms lowers the need for expertise in psychology, pricing, legal pressure points, or secure communications.

Anubis’ reported model is particularly revealing because it treats stolen data as raw material for a pressure product. Instead of merely threatening to publish files, the operator reportedly prepares a narrative analysis of sensitive data and uses that analysis as a negotiation artifact [5]. This reflects a shift from “data possession” to “data interpretation” as an extortion service. A non-technical affiliate may not understand which documents create the strongest pressure; a platform that analyzes and packages sensitive findings can increase the perceived impact of the breach.

This has implications for the economics of initial access. Secure Blink reported that initial access brokers received at least $14 million in on-chain payments and that spikes in IAB inflows typically preceded ransomware payment increases by approximately 30 days [7]. Vectra described initial access brokers as upstream actors who compromise networks and sell access, with flat fees often cited in the $500 to $5,000 range [8]. These figures suggest that access and extortion are increasingly separable markets. White-label platforms bridge the gap by helping access holders monetize intrusions without becoming full ransomware operators.

Negotiation support also creates operational consistency across affiliates, which can strengthen a brand. Victims and incident responders may see similar communication rhythms, leak-site language, proof-of-data practices, or payment-discussion structures across incidents. This consistency helps large operators behave more like corporate platforms. However, it also creates analytic opportunities for defenders: even if public brand names change, service-layer patterns may persist. The challenge is to analyze these patterns without over-attributing every shared behavior to a single group, since white-label models intentionally blur brand and backend.

From a critical perspective, the professionalization of negotiation workflows may be a response to diminishing marginal returns in ransomware. If the ecosystem produced nearly 8,000 claimed leak events in 2025 while total on-chain payments declined, competition for victim payment became harsher [7]. In such a market, operators must improve either target quality, ransom pricing, operational reliability, or pressure tactics. White-label negotiation support addresses the conversion problem directly. It gives affiliates a ready-made sales and collections apparatus, while allowing the platform provider to capture a share of revenues generated by a broader pool of criminals.

The risk to victims is not merely more ransomware; it is more commercially disciplined extortion. A non-technical criminal backed by a mature negotiation platform may be more dangerous than a technically skilled but disorganized actor. The platform can supply professionalized communications, pressure escalation, data-publication credibility, and support functions that increase the chance of payment. This is why early-2026 RaaS analysis must treat negotiation infrastructure as a core capability, not a secondary feature.

### 4. Revenue Splits, Access Markets, and Unit Economics of White-Label Participation

While prior reports discussed affiliate profit-sharing generally, this section focuses on how revenue splits and upstream access markets make white-label participation economically rational for non-technical criminals. The central economic question is: why would a criminal actor use a white-label or platformized RaaS provider instead of attempting to operate independently? The answer lies in cost avoidance, revenue sharing, access monetization, and reduced operational friction.

Vectra’s RaaS overview described the dominant affiliate model as one in which affiliates earn 60% to 80% of ransom payments while operators retain 20% to 40% [8]. It also described operators as responsible for developing payloads, maintaining infrastructure, managing leak sites, and providing support, while affiliates handle access, lateral movement, deployment, and sometimes negotiation [8]. These percentages are consistent with a platform economy: the operator captures a minority but scalable share of many incidents, while the affiliate retains the majority because it bears direct operational exposure and performs the victim-facing intrusion work.

For a non-technical or semi-technical criminal, the attractiveness of a 60% to 80% affiliate share depends on the alternative. Building a complete ransomware operation requires malware development, secure infrastructure, leak-site administration, negotiation channels, storage, support, and brand credibility. Those costs are high and risky. If a platform supplies those services for a 20% to 40% share, the affiliate may rationally accept the fee as a cost of market entry. In white-label variants, the affiliate may also gain the ability to present as an independent brand, which can increase perceived autonomy or bargaining power.

The same Vectra overview listed several RaaS pricing structures: monthly subscriptions of approximately $40 to $100 for lower-end access, one-time licenses ranging from $500 to $84,000 for premium kits, affiliate programs with no upfront cost and 60% to 80% affiliate shares, and negotiated profit-sharing arrangements based on target value and affiliate track record [8]. These figures should be treated as generalized industry ranges rather than terms for any specific operator. However, they show that RaaS participation can be structured to accommodate different criminal budgets and skill levels.

The unit-economics logic becomes clearer when applied to victim-volume data. Qilin posted 338 victims in Q1 2026 [1], and GuidePoint reported that Qilin rose from 154 victims in 2024 to 1,044 victims in 2025 [3]. Bitdefender reported that Qilin surpassed 1,800 victims since its inception as a RaaS group in late 2022 and claimed another 151 victims in March 2026 [9]. These figures indicate a high-throughput platform with sustained affiliate activity. Even if a substantial portion of victims do not pay, a platform collecting 20% to 40% of successful ransom payments across hundreds of incidents can generate significant revenue without directly conducting every attack.

Akira provides a different example of economic optimization. Industrial Cyber reported that Akira heavily targeted consumer goods and industrial manufacturing, sectors with high downtime costs and complex IT/OT environments, and cited $244 million in total proceeds while describing Akira’s targeting as deliberate and return-oriented [2]. Secure Blink also stated that Akira accounted for 16% of all claimed ransomware attacks in 2025 and had estimated proceeds of $244 million by late September 2025 [7]. These proceeds should be treated as estimates, but the economic interpretation is important: a mature RaaS platform may prioritize sectors where downtime pressure increases payment likelihood, rather than maximizing raw victim count alone.

LockBit 5.0 illustrates the role of brand recovery and platform relaunch. Arete reported that LockBit announced version 5.0 on the RAMP dark web forum in early September 2025 and that the updated variant included Windows and Linux versions, anti-analysis features, and unique 16-character extensions added to encrypted files [10]. Check Point reported that LockBit posted 163 victims in Q1 2026, confirming a comeback to fourth place [1]. Bitdefender later reported that LockBit had claimed 207 victims following the release of its 5.0 RaaS platform, with victims in manufacturing, healthcare, government, and construction [9]. The important economic point is not the technical feature set itself, but that LockBit attempted to restore affiliate confidence through a platform relaunch after disruption.

This matters because affiliate labor is mobile. Affiliates migrate toward platforms that maximize expected profit and minimize friction. Expected profit depends on payout share, victim conversion, platform reliability, leak-site credibility, negotiation support, malware effectiveness, and perceived law-enforcement risk. White-label models add another variable: brand autonomy. An affiliate may prefer a platform that provides infrastructure while allowing the affiliate to avoid operating under a stigmatized or disrupted parent brand.

The upstream access market makes platformization even more powerful. If initial access can be purchased for hundreds or thousands of dollars, and if extortion infrastructure can be leased or accessed through profit sharing, then the capital required to attempt a ransomware monetization event is sharply reduced. This does not mean success is guaranteed; many actors fail, and many victims do not pay. But the economic threshold for attempting an attack is lower than it would be in a fully integrated, technically demanding model.

|---|---|---|---|---|

| Skilled affiliate | Intrusion capability or custom malware | Backend administration, leak-site maintenance, victim communication systems | Uses platform to scale or reduce overhead | Dependent on platform reliability and secrecy |
| White-label provider | Infrastructure, panels, leak site, support, negotiation workflow | Direct intrusion labor across every case | Takes recurring share across many affiliates | Dependent on affiliate recruitment and operational security |
| Initial access broker | Network access | Extortion execution and victim negotiation | Sells access upstream | Dependent on demand from RaaS affiliates |
| Dominant RaaS brand | Reputation, tooling, affiliate base, leak infrastructure | Need for direct targeting in every incident | Monetizes affiliate throughput | Dependent on brand trust and resilience |

The model also creates incentives for corporate-style customer support inside criminal organizations. If affiliates are revenue-generating customers, operators must retain them. Retention depends on uptime, usability, payment reliability, dispute resolution, and support. This is why ransomware operations increasingly resemble illicit software-as-a-service businesses. The affiliate is both labor and customer; the victim is the revenue source; the operator is the platform vendor.

However, the economics are not uniformly favorable for affiliates. Operators may impose deposits, vetting, revenue splits, rules of engagement, or exclusivity expectations. Vectra noted that recruitment requirements can vary from deposits of 0.05 BTC to proof of prior attack activity, and that affiliates may receive access to payload builders while operating independently [8]. These requirements show that platform operators face adverse-selection risk: if they admit unskilled or reckless affiliates, they may attract law-enforcement attention, damage brand credibility, or create operational-security failures. White-label models may widen the affiliate pool, but they also increase governance challenges.

The payment-rate environment further shapes unit economics. If data-only extortion payment rates fell to roughly 25% in Q4 2025 as Vectra reported [8], then affiliates must either increase volume, improve target selection, use more coercive pressure, or rely on platforms with better conversion support. This may help explain renewed interest in encryption-capable platforms and in operators that can credibly provide decryption tools. Check Point’s observation that dominant brands invest in operational consistency and functional decryption tools reflects this market pressure [1].

From a critical standpoint, affiliate profit-sharing percentages should not be interpreted as clean corporate revenue statements. Ransomware payments are hidden, laundered, disputed, and sometimes unreported. Leak-site victim counts do not equal paying victims. On-chain estimates may miss off-chain arrangements or overstate attribution certainty. Nevertheless, the available figures consistently support the same structural conclusion: RaaS has evolved into a platform economy where operators capture scalable infrastructure rents, affiliates retain majority incident proceeds, and non-technical criminals can participate by buying or borrowing missing capabilities.

### 5. Corporate-Style Alliances, Governance Risk, and Attribution Distortion in Platformized RaaS

While previous sections discussed cartel-style partnerships in terms of competitive positioning, this section focuses on governance risk and attribution distortion created by corporate-style alliances and white-label infrastructure sharing. In a platformized ransomware economy, the market is not organized solely around independent gangs. It increasingly includes alliances, backend service providers, affiliate-sharing arrangements, infrastructure pooling, and public signaling intended to shape affiliate behavior and competitive conditions.

Secure Blink reported that in September 2025 DragonForce publicly proposed forming a coalition with LockBit and Qilin, using dark web forum language about creating “equal competition conditions,” avoiding conflicts and public insults, and “dictating market conditions” [7]. According to Secure Blink, LockBit’s operator responded affirmatively and DragonForce later declared the coalition active [7]. Secure Blink characterized this not as a traditional corporate merger, but as cartel-style coordination involving independent brands potentially agreeing to non-aggression pacts, affiliate-sharing frameworks, and infrastructure pooling [7]. Because these claims depend on criminal forum statements and third-party interpretation, they should be treated as reported cartel signaling rather than fully verified operational integration.

The relevance to white-label ransomware is direct. A cartel-style or alliance model can make shared infrastructure more acceptable to affiliates. If brands signal non-aggression or cooperation, affiliates may feel safer moving between platforms or using shared services. Infrastructure pooling can also reduce duplication and improve resilience. However, such arrangements also create coordination risk. Criminal alliances are unstable because participants compete for affiliates, payments, reputation, and operational secrecy. The same features that make platform sharing efficient can make it fragile.

DragonForce’s reported rebranding as a “cartel” and its white-label affiliate model show how corporate-style language can serve recruitment and market-positioning functions [5], [6]. The term “cartel” signals market power, coordination, and affiliate opportunity. It may also be aspirational branding rather than evidence of disciplined centralized governance. This distinction is important. Ransomware groups often exaggerate capabilities to attract affiliates or pressure victims. Analysts should therefore separate confirmed service offerings from claimed strategic alliances.

| Reported Partnership or Platform Behavior | Evidence Type | Confirmed Fact vs. Estimate/Claim | Analytical Value |

## Corporate-Style Partnerships and Cartelization: Non-Aggression, Infrastructure Pooling, Brand Alliances, and Market Control

### 1. Evidence Tiers for “Cartel” Claims: From Public Signaling to Verifiable Coordination

While previous sections discussed cartel-style partnerships as a general feature of RaaS professionalization, this section narrows the analysis to **evidence quality**: which cartelization claims are confirmed, which are plausible but unverified, and which should be treated as adversary marketing. This distinction is essential because ransomware groups deliberately use corporate language—“partnership,” “coalition,” “cartel,” “affiliate program,” and “market conditions”—to influence affiliates, victims, rivals, and journalists.

The strongest confirmed fact is that **DragonForce publicly attempted to frame a coalition involving LockBit and Qilin in September 2025**, using language that resembled market coordination rather than a conventional merger. Reporting attributed to threat-intelligence monitoring described DragonForce proposing “equal competition conditions,” reduced public conflict, and an ability to “dictate market conditions,” after which LockBit reportedly responded positively and DragonForce declared the coalition active ([1](https://www.secureblink.com/threat-research/2026-ransomware-cartelization-qilin-lock-bit-and-akira-convergence)). CSO Online, citing ReliaQuest reporting, similarly described the DragonForce, Qilin, and LockBit relationship as a criminal cartel intended to coordinate attacks and share resources in a more difficult ransomware operating environment ([2](https://www.csoonline.com/article/4070290/lockbit-dragonforce-and-qilin-form-a-cartel-to-dictate-ransomware-market-conditions.html)). Quorum Cyber characterized the same development more cautiously as “dark web chatter” suggesting DragonForce was attempting to formalize a cartel with LockBit and Qilin, emphasizing that operationalization remained uncertain ([3](https://www.quorumcyber.com/threat-intelligence/threat-assessment-dragonforce-calls-for-ransomware-cartel-with-lockbit-and-qilin/)).

This creates a three-tier evidentiary model for early-2026 RaaS cartelization:

| Evidence tier | What is supported | Example from available reporting | Analytical confidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Confirmed public signaling** | A group publicly proposed or endorsed cooperation using cartel-like language | DragonForce’s reported September 2025 proposal to coordinate with LockBit and Qilin | High |
| **Partially corroborated business alignment** | Multiple sources describe similar market logic: affiliate competition, shared services, non-aggression, or infrastructure pooling | CSO Online and Quorum Cyber both describe the proposed DragonForce–Qilin–LockBit relationship as a cartelization attempt | Medium |
| **Unverified operational integration** | Actual shared backend infrastructure, shared payment administration, unified victim allocation, or enforceable affiliate rules | Claims of infrastructure sharing and affiliate coordination, absent full public forensic validation | Low to medium |

The most defensible judgment is therefore not that a fully integrated ransomware cartel had been proven by early 2026. Rather, the evidence supports a more nuanced conclusion: **major RaaS operators were adopting cartel-like coordination narratives and selected partnership mechanisms to reduce destructive competition, stabilize affiliate supply, and increase resilience after law-enforcement disruption**. That is materially different from a single hierarchical criminal corporation.

This matters because ransomware markets are not only technical ecosystems; they are also **trust markets**. Operators compete for affiliates, affiliates compete for access, access brokers compete for buyers, and victims assess whether an extortion threat is credible. A partnership announcement can therefore have economic utility even before any deep technical integration occurs. By presenting itself as part of a larger coalition, a ransomware brand can signal scale, continuity, and payment reliability. These attributes are valuable after repeated takedowns, arrests, infrastructure seizures, decryptor releases, and reputational shocks.

LockBit illustrates this dynamic. After Operation Cronos disrupted LockBit in 2024, the brand’s economic credibility was damaged. Vectra’s RaaS overview, citing Chainalysis, notes that LockBit payments dropped by **79%** after Operation Cronos, while the group later attempted to reemerge with LockBit 5.0 ([4](https://www.vectra.ai/topics/ransomware-as-a-service)). A cartel-style association with active or rising operators can help a damaged brand signal survivability to affiliates. Conversely, an active operator such as Qilin can benefit from association with LockBit’s historical brand recognition while preserving its own operational identity.

Qilin’s reported victim volume also helps explain why it was attractive as a coalition partner. SecureBlink reported that Qilin claimed **701 victims by October 2025**, representing a **280% surge from April 2025** and an average of approximately **75 victims per month**, making it one of the most active ransomware families by Q3 2025 ([1](https://www.secureblink.com/threat-research/2026-ransomware-cartelization-qilin-lock-bit-and-akira-convergence)). Quorum Cyber further reported that Qilin accounted for around **15% of global ransomware compromises in the previous 30 days** at the time of its assessment ([3](https://www.quorumcyber.com/threat-intelligence/threat-assessment-dragonforce-calls-for-ransomware-cartel-with-lockbit-and-qilin/)). These numbers should be interpreted as **claimed or observed extortion-site activity**, not a complete measurement of all intrusions. They nevertheless indicate why Qilin’s participation, even if only symbolic, would materially strengthen the perceived bargaining position of a coalition.

DragonForce’s role is analytically distinct. It is not merely another conventional RaaS operator in the cartelization narrative; it is reported to have promoted a **white-label RaaS model** in which affiliates can use DragonForce infrastructure while applying their own branding ([5](https://www.darkreading.com/cyberattacks-data-breaches/extortion-gangs-join-forces-ransomware-cartel)). That model is important because it changes what “partnership” means. In a traditional cartel, recognizable suppliers coordinate while preserving separate brands. In a white-label model, the backend supplier may be hidden, allowing multiple apparent brands to rely on common infrastructure or services. As a result, cartelization may be visible at the narrative layer while consolidation occurs less visibly at the service layer.

Akira is relevant to the broader early-2026 market structure, even though the provided cartel-specific reporting focuses mainly on DragonForce, LockBit, and Qilin. Akira’s significance lies in showing that consolidation does not require public coalition membership. Dominant RaaS operators can participate in market consolidation through affiliate attraction, repeat victimization capability, brand credibility, and extortion process maturity without formally joining a cartel. Therefore, early-2026 consolidation should be understood as a spectrum: at one end are explicit cartel claims; in the middle are white-label and infrastructure-sharing relationships; at the other end are independent but dominant operators whose scale reduces fragmentation by absorbing affiliate labor and victim volume.

From a threat-intelligence perspective, this produces a key analytic rule: **cartelization should be assessed as behavior, not as branding**. Public claims are relevant but insufficient. The stronger indicators are whether groups share affiliates, reuse backend infrastructure, coordinate victim handling, avoid public disputes, standardize commission structures, or provide cross-brand services such as negotiation support, leak-site administration, DDoS pressure, or media amplification. Current public reporting strongly supports cartel-style intent and market signaling; it supports operational integration only partially.

### 2. Non-Aggression and Market Discipline as Economic Instruments, Not Just Criminal Diplomacy

While earlier content referenced non-aggression and cartel signaling at a high level, this section examines **why non-aggression is economically valuable** in the ransomware market. In legitimate industries, cartel behavior often seeks price stability, market allocation, or production discipline. In RaaS, the equivalent objectives are affiliate retention, victim monetization, brand credibility, reduced internal conflict, and lower transaction costs across criminal supply chains.

The ransomware ecosystem historically suffered from fragmentation after major disruptions. When a leading brand was dismantled or discredited, affiliates migrated to alternative programs, new brands appeared, and victim listings dispersed across multiple leak sites. This fragmentation created opportunity for smaller operators but also produced inefficiencies: duplicate targeting, affiliate poaching, unreliable payment rules, inconsistent tooling, and disputes over stolen data. A cartel-style non-aggression pact attempts to reduce those inefficiencies by creating rules of engagement among major operators.

The reported DragonForce proposal explicitly referenced “no conflicts” and “no public insults,” language that resembles corporate non-disparagement and channel-conflict controls rather than simple criminal bravado ([1](https://www.secureblink.com/threat-research/2026-ransomware-cartelization-qilin-lock-bit-and-akira-convergence)). In a low-trust underground economy, public insults can damage affiliate recruitment, trigger retaliatory doxing, and reduce confidence in payment administration. A non-aggression norm therefore has direct economic value: it preserves brand equity and reduces the perceived risk of joining or transacting with a particular RaaS program.

The following table maps cartel-style mechanisms to ransomware-market effects:

| Corporate-style mechanism | RaaS analogue | Economic purpose | Expected market effect | Evidence status |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Non-aggression pact | Agreement to reduce public disputes or avoid hostile targeting of partner brands | Preserve reputation and affiliate confidence | Less destructive competition among top operators | Publicly reported as proposed; operational enforcement uncertain |
| Channel partnership | Shared or transferable affiliate relationships | Increase labor liquidity without losing trusted criminals | Faster scaling of campaigns after disruption | Plausible; partially supported by cartel reporting |
| Shared infrastructure | Common hosting, leak-site support, negotiation systems, or operational services | Reduce duplicated fixed costs | Higher resilience and lower operating overhead | Claimed or hypothesized; requires forensic validation |
| Brand alliance | Public association among major names | Signal scale and continuity | Improves affiliate recruitment and victim intimidation | Strongly supported as public signaling |
| Market-condition control | Coordination around affiliate terms and service expectations | Reduce price wars for affiliates | Stabilizes operator margins | Supported by reported commission competition |

The market-discipline problem is especially visible in affiliate profit-sharing. Quorum Cyber reported that Qilin advertised an **85/15 split**, meaning affiliates could retain 85% of ransom proceeds while the operator took 15%, and that LockBit offered up to **90%** for top performers ([3](https://www.quorumcyber.com/threat-intelligence/threat-assessment-dragonforce-calls-for-ransomware-cartel-with-lockbit-and-qilin/)). Vectra’s general RaaS role model places affiliate revenue shares commonly in the **60–80%** range, with operators retaining **20–40%** depending on the model ([4](https://www.vectra.ai/topics/ransomware-as-a-service)). When large operators compete aggressively for affiliates, commission shares can become a race to the bottom for operators. A cartel-like arrangement can attempt to stabilize those economics by reducing affiliate poaching or setting informal expectations for splits and services.

However, such market discipline is difficult to enforce. RaaS operators do not have lawful contracts, courts, or transparent accounting. Affiliates can defect, operate under multiple handles, steal data independently, or sell access to competing groups. Operators can also cheat by secretly offering better terms to high-performing affiliates. Therefore, non-aggression in RaaS is best understood as **soft governance**, not enforceable cartel law. It relies on reputation, fear, escrow norms, selective access, and public narratives of professionalism.

This is where corporate-style language becomes strategically useful. When DragonForce, LockBit, or Qilin use partnership language, they are not merely describing cooperation; they are trying to create a perception of governance. That perception may attract affiliates who prefer predictable payment terms and operational support. It may also discourage smaller rivals by implying that dominant groups can pool resources or retaliate collectively. In this sense, cartelization is both a business model and a psychological operation against the underground market itself.

The same logic applies to victim monetization. Fragmented ransomware markets can produce inconsistent extortion experiences. Some groups leak quickly, some negotiate slowly, some fail to provide proof of deletion, and some cannot sustain pressure after the initial intrusion. Corporate-style partnerships can standardize the extortion “customer journey,” not for victim benefit, but to maximize payment probability. Quorum Cyber noted that competition among RaaS offerings included value-added services such as legal and negotiation support, DDoS capabilities, and even AI-powered chatbots ([3](https://www.quorumcyber.com/threat-intelligence/threat-assessment-dragonforce-calls-for-ransomware-cartel-with-lockbit-and-qilin/)). These services resemble product differentiation in a criminal SaaS market. A cartel can reduce competitive pressure to innovate destructively while preserving enough service quality to keep affiliates productive.

There is also a defensive implication. If non-aggression pacts reduce visible conflict between brands, defenders may see fewer public disputes but more coordinated extortion pressure. Incident responders should not assume that separate leak-site brands imply separate criminal supply chains. Conversely, they should not automatically assume that every claimed alliance means one unified backend. The correct analytic approach is to track shared infrastructure indicators, affiliate migration patterns, extortion-note language, negotiation behaviors, payment-wallet relationships where lawfully available, and leak-site administrative patterns—without over-weighting public claims.

The term “market control” should therefore be used cautiously. Available evidence supports an attempt to influence market conditions, but not a proven ability to control the entire ransomware economy. Even dominant RaaS brands face constraints: law enforcement, sanctions exposure, cryptocurrency tracing, unreliable affiliates, geopolitical disruptions, infrastructure seizures, and competition from data-theft-only extortion groups. Cartelization may reduce fragmentation at the top of the market, but it does not eliminate volatility. Rather, it shifts volatility from brand proliferation toward hidden service-layer dependencies.

### 3. Infrastructure Pooling and White-Label Capacity: The Hidden Consolidation Layer Beneath Visible Brands

While previous white-label sections addressed how non-technical criminals can use outsourced ransomware services, this section focuses specifically on **white-labeling as a consolidation mechanism**. The key point is that white-label ransomware can make the market appear more fragmented at the brand layer while making it more concentrated at the infrastructure layer.

DragonForce is central to this analysis because multiple reports describe it as offering a white-label RaaS product. Dark Reading reported that DragonForce is known for a white-labeling model in which affiliates can use the group’s infrastructure to deploy ransomware under the affiliate’s own branding ([5](https://www.darkreading.com/cyberattacks-data-breaches/extortion-gangs-join-forces-ransomware-cartel)). Vectra similarly identifies DragonForce as a “white-label ‘cartel’ RaaS,” describing automated registration, limited vetting, and affiliate use of DragonForce infrastructure ([4](https://www.vectra.ai/topics/ransomware-as-a-service)). This model differs from traditional RaaS in a way that has macro-economic importance.

In traditional RaaS, the brand, malware family, leak site, affiliate program, and extortion workflow are usually linked. In white-label RaaS, those components can be separated:

| RaaS component | Traditional RaaS structure | White-label structure | Market effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Public brand | Operator’s brand dominates | Affiliate may use own brand | More apparent fragmentation |
| Backend infrastructure | Usually associated with operator | Shared or rented from provider | More hidden consolidation |
| Technical development | Operator-controlled | Provider-controlled or outsourced | Lower barrier to entry |
| Victim-facing extortion | Often under operator identity | Affiliate-branded or hybrid | Attribution ambiguity |
| Affiliate onboarding | Controlled by RaaS operator | Potentially more automated or permissive | Larger pool of lower-skill actors |
| Revenue model | Commission split | Commission, rental, or hybrid service fee | Platform-like monetization |

This separation has two major cartelization effects. First, it allows infrastructure providers to scale without forcing all affiliates into a single public brand. That reduces brand risk: if one affiliate becomes notorious, incompetent, or heavily targeted by law enforcement, the backend provider may still serve other affiliates or labels. Second, it enables criminal market segmentation. Skilled affiliates may prefer elite RaaS programs with high payout shares, while lower-skill criminals may prefer white-label services that reduce technical requirements even if they must accept less favorable economics or weaker brand recognition.

The white-label model also changes how victim-volume statistics should be interpreted. A rise in the number of named ransomware brands does not necessarily mean the ecosystem is fragmenting. Some of those brands may be superficial labels operating on shared services. Conversely, a decline in visible brands does not necessarily mean fewer actors; affiliates may simply be clustering behind stronger platforms. Therefore, early-2026 market consolidation must be measured at multiple layers: public brands, affiliate identities, infrastructure providers, access brokers, payment flows, and extortion-support services.

This is especially important in the context of reported cartelization between DragonForce, LockBit, and Qilin. If a coalition involves infrastructure pooling, the most significant market effect may not be a single combined leak site. It may be a **shared service substrate** that supports multiple brands. FalconFeeds described the cartel hypothesis as potentially involving shared bulletproof hosting, shared victim data for cross-extortion, and pooled affiliate resources, while cautioning that such announcements can be exaggerated for marketing purposes ([6](https://falconfeeds.io/blogs/qilin-ransomware-cartel-strategic-threat-assessment-2022-2026/)). That caution is appropriate: infrastructure pooling is plausible and economically rational, but public evidence must be distinguished from confirmed forensic proof.

The consolidation layer can be conceptualized as follows:

| Layer | Fragmentation indicator | Consolidation indicator | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|---|
| Brand layer | Many leak-site names and ransomware labels | Public alliances or brand retirements | Affects media visibility and victim perception |
| Affiliate layer | Many independent intrusion teams | Shared recruitment pools and cross-brand affiliates | Drives attack throughput |
| Infrastructure layer | Separate hosting and admin systems | Common backend services or white-label platforms | Determines resilience and scalability |
| Service layer | Ad hoc negotiation and leak operations | Standardized negotiation, DDoS, legal-pressure, or chatbot services | Improves monetization efficiency |
| Financial layer | Many disconnected wallets and brokers | Reused laundering channels or payment administration | Affects revenue capture and law-enforcement exposure |

White-label ransomware increases the risk of **false decentralization**. A defender may observe several new ransomware names and infer that the ecosystem is splintering. But if those names rely on the same backend provider, then operational risk is concentrated. This is analogous to cloud concentration in legitimate technology markets: many customer-facing applications may depend on a small number of infrastructure providers. In ransomware, that concentration can make campaigns more scalable, but it can also create single points of failure if law enforcement or security researchers identify the shared backend.

The model also expands the labor pool. Non-technical or semi-technical criminals do not need to build encryption tooling, leak infrastructure, negotiation portals, or affiliate dashboards from scratch. They can focus on social engineering, access acquisition, data theft, or victim selection. Vectra’s role taxonomy describes operators as maintaining payloads, infrastructure, leak sites, and support while affiliates conduct intrusions and deployment; it also identifies initial access brokers as upstream actors selling access, often for flat fees in the **$500–$5,000** range ([4](https://www.vectra.ai/topics/ransomware-as-a-service)). White-labeling compresses these functions into a more modular supply chain, enabling actors with limited technical development capability to participate in extortion.

The macro-economic result is **criminal specialization with platform dependency**. Affiliates become less dependent on their own tooling and more dependent on backend providers. Backend providers become less dependent on any one brand and more dependent on maintaining trust across a distributed criminal customer base. This resembles platform economics: the provider benefits from scale, reusable infrastructure, and network effects, while affiliates benefit from reduced setup costs. Cartelization can then emerge not only through explicit agreements but through common dependency on a small number of service providers.

However, white-label consolidation carries governance risks for criminals. A permissive white-label program can attract careless affiliates, increasing law-enforcement exposure and victim noise. Poor affiliate vetting can lead to attacks on politically sensitive targets, hospitals, or entities that trigger disproportionate response. It can also produce inconsistent operational security. Therefore, a white-label provider faces a trade-off between growth and control. The more it lowers barriers, the faster it scales; the more it scales, the harder it becomes to preserve discipline.

This trade-off helps explain why cartelization and white-labeling are connected. A cartel-like framework can provide a narrative of discipline around a model that is otherwise inherently permissive. By claiming alliances with established brands, a white-label operator can present itself not as a chaotic low-barrier service, but as part of a structured ransomware marketplace. Whether that structure is real or aspirational, it serves a recruitment and reputation function.

### 4. Affiliate Profit-Sharing, Partnership Terms, and the Economics of Criminal Channel Management

While previous affiliate-economics sections examined recruitment and labor migration, this section focuses on **affiliate profit-sharing as channel management** within cartel-style RaaS partnerships. The central question is not only how much affiliates are paid, but how commission structures shape market consolidation and partnership behavior.

RaaS operators compete for affiliates in much the same way legitimate platforms compete for high-performing sellers, developers, or channel partners. Affiliates bring victim access, intrusion capability, language skills, regional knowledge, and negotiation leverage. Operators provide malware, infrastructure, leak sites, reputation, payment workflows, and support. Profit-sharing determines whether affiliates remain loyal, defect, operate across multiple programs, or create their own brands.

Reported figures show that affiliate payouts became highly competitive by 2025. Quorum Cyber reported that Qilin advertised an **85/15 affiliate/operator split**, while LockBit offered up to **90%** for top-performing affiliates ([3](https://www.quorumcyber.com/threat-intelligence/threat-assessment-dragonforce-calls-for-ransomware-cartel-with-lockbit-and-qilin/)). Vectra’s broader RaaS framework places affiliates generally at **60–80%** of ransom revenue, operators at **20–40%**, and initial access brokers at flat upstream fees of approximately **$500–$5,000** ([4](https://www.vectra.ai/topics/ransomware-as-a-service)). These figures indicate a market in which labor—especially proven intrusion labor—has substantial bargaining power.

| Participant | Typical economic role | Reported or common compensation | Market power in consolidated RaaS |
|---|---|---|---|
| RaaS operator | Provides ransomware platform, leak infrastructure, support, and brand | Often 20–40%; lower share in competitive programs | Strong when brand and infrastructure are trusted |
| Elite affiliate | Conducts intrusions and monetization workflow | Often 60–80%; Qilin reportedly 85%; LockBit up to 90% for top performers | Very strong due to scarcity and revenue impact |
| Initial access broker | Sells access to compromised environments | Often flat fees around $500–$5,000 in general models | Moderate; depends on access quality |
| White-label provider | Supplies backend infrastructure under affiliate branding | Commission, rental, or hybrid model; public figures vary | Strong if many brands depend on same backend |
| Negotiation/support service | Assists with extortion communications or pressure | Embedded in operator share or separately contracted | Increasing as extortion professionalizes |

The high affiliate share has two implications. First, operators may earn less per incident but compensate through volume. Qilin’s reported **701 claimed victims by October 2025** and average of **75 victims per month** suggest how a high-volume operator can make a lower operator percentage economically viable ([1](https://www.secureblink.com/threat-research/2026-ransomware-cartelization-qilin-lock-bit-and-akira-convergence)). Second, operators are incentivized to form partnerships that reduce affiliate churn. If affiliates can easily move to a rival offering a higher share, cartel-style arrangements may be used to stabilize commission competition or at least reduce public poaching.

This mirrors legitimate channel economics. A software vendor that pays resellers aggressively may gain market share but compress margins. If multiple vendors compete for the same resellers, they may introduce tiering, preferred-partner status, bundled services, or regional exclusivity. RaaS operators appear to use criminal equivalents: elite affiliate status, higher payout tiers, access to better tooling, support services, and brand reputation. LockBit’s reported “up to 90%” for top performers resembles tiered channel incentives rather than a flat criminal partnership model ([3](https://www.quorumcyber.com/threat-intelligence/threat-assessment-dragonforce-calls-for-ransomware-cartel-with-lockbit-and-qilin/)).

Cartelization can influence these economics in at least four ways:

1. **Reducing affiliate bidding wars.** If major operators agree not to aggressively poach or publicly undermine one another, commission escalation may slow.
2. **Enabling affiliate portability.** Shared or accepted affiliate pools can let trusted actors move between brands without fully exiting the partnership network.
3. **Bundling services.** Operators can compete less on commission and more on support: negotiation help, leak-site reliability, DDoS pressure, media amplification, or data-sale channels.
4. **Segmenting affiliates.** Elite affiliates may be routed toward high-value brands, while lower-skill participants use white-label infrastructure.

The third point is especially important. Quorum Cyber reported that RaaS offerings increasingly included value-added services such as legal and negotiation support, DDoS capabilities, and AI-powered chatbots ([3](https://www.quorumcyber.com/threat-intelligence/threat-assessment-dragonforce-calls-for-ransomware-cartel-with-lockbit-and-qilin/)). These offerings allow operators to justify a retained share even when affiliates demand high payouts. In other words, operators are not simply selling malware; they are selling a revenue-optimization stack.

The relationship between cartelization and white-labeling is also visible in channel management. A white-label provider can serve affiliates who want independence from a major brand but still need backend capability. This creates a “private label” channel, similar to retail markets where the manufacturer remains hidden while the reseller controls the customer-facing identity. In ransomware, the “customer” is the victim, and the arrangement is criminal, but the economic logic is comparable: the backend provider captures revenue across multiple apparent brands.

| Channel model | Affiliate value proposition | Operator/provider value proposition | Consolidation effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Traditional branded RaaS | Use a known brand with established victim pressure | Monetize affiliates under one recognizable identity | Concentrates activity under visible brand |
| Tiered affiliate program | Higher payout or better support for top performers | Retain elite labor and increase ransom yield | Concentrates skilled affiliates |
| White-label RaaS | Maintain own brand while renting backend capability | Scale infrastructure across multiple labels | Conceals consolidation beneath brand diversity |
| Cartel partnership | Access broader ecosystem and reduced conflict | Stabilize market and affiliate supply | Concentrates influence among top operators |
| Hybrid service marketplace | Select components such as negotiation, hosting, or leaks | Monetize specialized services | Consolidates specific functions rather than full brands |

A critical limitation is that profit-sharing percentages are often based on actor advertisements, forum posts, or security-vendor reporting. They may not represent actual realized economics. Affiliates may be cheated, operators may change terms, and ransom payments may be partially withheld. Publicly advertised splits can function as recruitment marketing. Therefore, reported percentages should be treated as **market offers**, not audited financial statements.

Nevertheless, the trend is clear: affiliate labor has become expensive, and high-performing affiliates are a scarce resource. This scarcity pushes operators toward corporate-style partnerships because partnerships can reduce labor volatility. If Qilin, LockBit, and DragonForce can present themselves as aligned rather than mutually hostile, they may reduce affiliate uncertainty and increase the perceived stability of joining their ecosystem. This can be particularly valuable after law-enforcement actions that disrupt trust.

The defensive implication is that disruption of a single brand may no longer displace affiliate labor for long. Affiliates can migrate to adjacent brands, white-label platforms, or cartel partners. Therefore, incident-response and policy strategies that focus only on brand takedowns may produce temporary displacement unless they also target shared services, money flows, access brokers, and affiliate identities. This does not mean brand disruption is ineffective; Operation Cronos reportedly had a major impact on LockBit’s payments. But the later emergence of LockBit 5.0 and cartel-signaling behavior shows how dominant operators attempt to rebuild trust and reabsorb labor after disruption ([4](https://www.vectra.ai/topics/ransomware-as-a-service)).

### 5. Victim-Volume Signals, Brand Alliances, and the Boundaries of Market Control

While earlier victim-volume sections focused on concentration metrics and top-heavy distribution, this section uses victim-volume data differently: as an indicator of **cartel leverage and partnership bargaining power**. In cartel economics, firms with greater output or market share have more influence over coordination. In ransomware, claimed victim volume is an imperfect but useful proxy for operational throughput, affiliate productivity, and brand visibility.

The most specific volume statistic in the provided reporting concerns Qilin. SecureBlink reported that Qilin claimed **701 victims by October 2025**, a **280% increase from April 2025**, averaging approximately **75 victims per month** and becoming the most active ransomware family by Q3 2025 ([1](https://www.secureblink.com/threat-research/2026-ransomware-cartelization-qilin-lock-bit-and-akira-convergence)). Quorum Cyber reported that Qilin was responsible for around **15% of global ransomware compromises in the previous 30 days** at the time of its assessment ([3](https://www.quorumcyber.com/threat-intelligence/threat-assessment-dragonforce-calls-for-ransomware-cartel-with-lockbit-and-qilin/)). These figures make Qilin economically important in any cartelization discussion: a coalition that includes a high-throughput operator has more credibility than one composed only of weakened or aspirational brands.

LockBit’s volume and payment trajectory must be interpreted differently. LockBit historically dominated the RaaS ecosystem, but after Operation Cronos its revenue position deteriorated sharply. Vectra, citing Chainalysis, states that LockBit payments dropped **79%** after the disruption, while the group later reemerged with LockBit 5.0 ([4](https://www.vectra.ai/topics/ransomware-as-a-service)). This makes LockBit valuable less for current verified volume and more for residual brand equity, affiliate memory, and proven historical infrastructure. In a partnership, LockBit contributes name recognition; Qilin contributes recent throughput; DragonForce contributes white-label infrastructure positioning.

Akira’s role in early-2026 consolidation is more representative of the **dominant independent operator** category. The cartel-specific public reporting does not establish Akira as part of the DragonForce–Qilin–LockBit arrangement, but Akira belongs in the macro-economic analysis because consolidation can occur without formal alliance announcements. A ransomware group can reduce ecosystem fragmentation by absorbing affiliates, sustaining victim volume, and maintaining a reliable extortion process. Thus, the early-2026 market should not be divided simply into “cartel members” and “non-cartel members.” A better classification is based on control over the resources that matter: affiliates, infrastructure, brand reputation, and monetization workflows.

| Operator or model | Reported market signal | Cartelization relevance | Confidence level |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Qilin** | 701 claimed victims by Oct. 2025; 280% growth from April; about 75 victims/month; around 15% of recent global compromises in one assessment | High-throughput partner; strengthens cartel credibility | Medium to high for reported claims; lower for full ecosystem share |
| **LockBit 5.0** | Reemergence after Operation Cronos; payments reportedly dropped 79% post-disruption | Brand recovery through alliance signaling | Medium |
| **DragonForce** | White-label RaaS and cartel proposal; reported coalition framing | Infrastructure and franchising layer | Medium for public claims; lower for full backend integration |
| **Akira** | Dominant structured operator in broader RaaS market discussions | Shows consolidation can occur outside explicit cartel announcements | Medium, depending on source set |
| **White-label affiliates** | Can operate under independent branding while using provider infrastructure | Makes consolidation less visible | Medium conceptually; case-specific proof varies |

Victim-volume data must be treated carefully for four reasons. First, many statistics are based on leak-site listings, which undercount victims that pay before publication and may include exaggerated or duplicate claims. Second, data-theft-only extortion can blur distinctions between ransomware and broader cyber extortion. Third, white-label infrastructure can cause the same backend ecosystem to appear as multiple brands. Fourth, law-enforcement and private-sector disruption can temporarily suppress listings without eliminating affiliates.

Even with these limitations, volume signals matter because they affect bargaining power among criminals. A group with high victim throughput can demand better terms in a partnership. It can attract affiliates without offering the highest commission because its brand and infrastructure appear more reliable. It can also influence norms around ransom negotiation, leak timing, and victim pressure. This is why Qilin’s reported rise is strategically significant. Its inclusion in a cartel narrative changes the narrative from “weakened brands seeking relevance” to “a high-volume operator participating in market coordination.”

Brand alliances can also affect victim psychology. If a victim believes it is dealing with an isolated small group, it may assess the threat differently than if it believes the actor is connected to a larger cartel with leak-site reach, DDoS capability, or access to multiple extortion channels. This does not mean victims should accept adversary claims at face value. Rather, incident responders should recognize that cartel branding is part of extortion pressure. Public references to alliances may be designed to increase perceived inevitability of payment.

The boundary of market control remains important. Ransomware actors can attempt to coordinate, but they cannot fully control the market because the ecosystem is open, adversarial, and unstable. New entrants continue to appear; affiliates can defect; law enforcement can disrupt infrastructure; cryptocurrency tracing can pressure laundering channels; and geopolitical shifts can alter safe-haven assumptions. CSO Online noted that the DragonForce–Qilin–LockBit alliance emerged as law-enforcement pressure mounted, indicating that consolidation is partly a defensive response to external pressure rather than a sign of unconstrained strength ([2](https://www.csoonline.com/article/4070290/lockbit-dragonforce-and-qilin-form-a-cartel-to-dictate-ransomware

## Conclusion

By early 2026, the RaaS economy had reconsolidated around a smaller set of dominant, structured operators rather than remaining a highly fragmented field of interchangeable ransomware brands. Q1 2026 data showed **2,122 leak-site-listed victims**, with the **top 10 groups accounting for about 71%** of all victims, up from 57% during the Q3 2025 fragmentation peak, while active groups declined from **85 to 71**. Qilin led the market with **338 Q1 victims**, while The Gentlemen surged to **166**, LockBit 5.0 re-entered the top tier with **163**, and Qilin, Akira, The Gentlemen, and LockBit together represented roughly **41%** of Q1 victim volume. These figures indicate that ransomware capacity is concentrating in platform-like operators able to absorb displaced affiliates, maintain leak and negotiation infrastructure, and project credibility to both affiliates and victims ([Check Point Research, 2026](https://research.checkpoint.com/2026/the-state-of-ransomware-q1-2026/); [Industrial Cyber, 2026](https://industrialcyber.co/ransomware/ransomware-sector-reconsolidating-as-qilin-lockbit-and-the-gentlemen-expand-influence-in-q1-2026/)).

The most important structural finding is that RaaS now resembles a criminal platform economy: operators compete for affiliate labor through tooling, brand reputation, support services, negotiation workflows, and revenue-sharing terms. Reported affiliate economics show generous splits, including Qilin’s alleged **80% affiliate share for payments up to $3 million and 85% above $3 million**, while DragonForce’s white-label model reportedly offers affiliates around **80%** while letting them operate under their own brands. This white-label phenomenon lowers entry barriers for non-technical or semi-technical criminals by outsourcing ransomware infrastructure, leak sites, negotiation portals, and support, meaning visible brand proliferation may mask deeper backend consolidation ([GuidePoint Security, 2026](https://www.guidepointsecurity.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/GRIT-2026-Ransomware-and-Cyber-Threat-Report.pdf); [AhnLab, 2026](https://download.ahnlab.com/global/brochure/2025_Threat_Analysis_2026_Predictions_en.pdf)).

The implication for defenders, incident responders, and policymakers is that disrupting individual ransomware brands remains useful but insufficient if affiliates, access brokers, payment channels, and shared service infrastructure remain intact. Future analysis should focus less on group names alone and more on service-layer dependencies: affiliate migration, shared infrastructure, white-label backend providers, negotiation patterns, access-broker flows, and payment-conversion economics. Because leak-site counts are claimed victim listings rather than confirmed breaches or paid ransoms, intelligence teams should continue validating public claims with incident-response telemetry, blockchain analysis, infrastructure correlation, and law-enforcement reporting before drawing conclusions about true profitability or operational control ([Broadcom/Symantec, 2026](https://sed-cms.broadcom.com/sites/default/files/2026-01/RWN-2026-WP100_1.pdf); [Dark Reading, 2025](https://www.darkreading.com/cyberattacks-data-breaches/extortion-gangs-join-forces-ransomware-cartel)).
